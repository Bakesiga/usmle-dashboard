Days 14 and 15 lecture summary · First Aid 2025 pp. 682 to 689

1 · Lung Volumes and Capacities FA pp. 682 to 683

  • Four volumes: tidal volume (about 500 mL, one normal breath), inspiratory reserve volume (extra air you can pull in beyond a normal breath), expiratory reserve volume (extra air you can push out beyond a normal breath), and residual volume (what remains after a maximal exhale, which spirometry cannot measure).
  • Four capacities (a capacity is two or more volumes added together): inspiratory capacity (tidal plus inspiratory reserve), functional residual capacity (expiratory reserve plus residual, the resting volume after a normal exhale), vital capacity (tidal plus both reserves, the most air you can move), and total lung capacity (all four volumes summed).
  • Anything that contains residual volume (so RV, FRC, and TLC) cannot be measured by simple spirometry. You need helium dilution or body plethysmography.
  • Functional residual capacity is the equilibrium point where the inward elastic recoil of the lung exactly balances the outward recoil of the chest wall. Pulmonary vascular resistance is also at its lowest at FRC.

2 · Dead Space and Ventilation FA p. 683

  • Minute ventilation equals tidal volume times respiratory rate. Alveolar ventilation equals (tidal volume minus dead space) times respiratory rate.
  • Anatomic dead space is the conducting airways (about 150 mL): they move air but do no gas exchange. Physiologic dead space adds alveolar dead space, meaning alveoli that are ventilated but not perfused. In healthy lungs physiologic dead space is about equal to anatomic dead space.

3 · Oxygen-Hemoglobin Dissociation Curve FA pp. 686 to 687

  • The curve is sigmoidal (S-shaped) because of positive cooperativity: hemoglobin has four subunits, and binding one oxygen molecule raises the affinity for the next. Myoglobin, with a single subunit, has a hyperbolic curve instead.
  • A right shift means decreased affinity, so hemoglobin releases more oxygen to the tissues. Causes (the Bohr effect): increased CO2, increased acidity (lower pH), increased temperature, increased 2,3-BPG, and exercise. Mnemonic CADET, "CADET face right": CO2, Acid, 2,3-DPG, Exercise, Temperature.
  • A left shift means increased affinity, so hemoglobin holds onto oxygen. Causes: fetal hemoglobin (binds 2,3-BPG poorly), carbon monoxide, methemoglobin, and decreased CO2, acid, temperature, or 2,3-BPG.

4 · Hemoglobin Modifications FA pp. 686 to 687

  • Methemoglobin: the iron is oxidized to the ferric (Fe3+) state and cannot bind oxygen, which produces a functional anemia and a left shift of the remaining sites. Causes include nitrites, dapsone, and local anesthetics. Blood looks chocolate-colored and the cyanosis does not improve with oxygen. Treat with methylene blue. Note that ferric iron also binds cyanide, which is why nitrites are deliberately used in cyanide poisoning to pull cyanide off the mitochondria.
  • Carboxyhemoglobin: carbon monoxide binds hemoglobin roughly 200 times more avidly than oxygen, lowering oxygen-carrying capacity and shifting the curve left. Classic findings: cherry-red skin, a normal arterial oxygen partial pressure, but a low measured saturation. Treat with 100% oxygen, or hyperbaric oxygen in severe cases.

5 · Oxygen Content and Delivery FA p. 687

  • Oxygen content equals (1.34 times hemoglobin times saturation) plus (0.003 times the arterial oxygen partial pressure). Almost all oxygen is bound to hemoglobin, and the dissolved portion is tiny.
  • Oxygen delivery equals cardiac output times oxygen content.
  • Key teaching point: anemia lowers oxygen content because hemoglobin is low, but the arterial oxygen partial pressure and the saturation percentage stay normal. Carbon monoxide poisoning lowers both saturation and content while the partial pressure stays normal.

6 · Alveolar Gas Equation and A-a Gradient FA p. 685

  • The alveolar oxygen partial pressure can be estimated as 150 minus (arterial CO2 divided by 0.8) on room air at sea level.
  • The A-a gradient is the alveolar minus the arterial oxygen partial pressure. Normal is about 5 to 15 mm Hg, and it rises with age.
  • A normal A-a gradient with hypoxemia points to high altitude or hypoventilation (for example opioid overdose or obesity hypoventilation). An increased A-a gradient points to V/Q mismatch, a diffusion problem (such as fibrosis), or a right-to-left shunt.

7 · Hypoxemia, Hypoxia, and Ischemia FA p. 686

  • Hypoxemia is a low arterial oxygen partial pressure. Hypoxia is inadequate oxygen delivery to tissue. Ischemia is inadequate blood flow.
  • A shunt does not correct with 100% oxygen because blood bypasses ventilated alveoli entirely. A V/Q mismatch does improve with 100% oxygen. That single distinction is heavily tested.

8 · Ventilation-Perfusion (V/Q) Matching FA p. 685

  • The ideal V/Q is 1. At the apex the V/Q is high (around 3): both ventilation and perfusion are lower there, but perfusion falls more, so it behaves a little like wasted ventilation (dead space). At the base the V/Q is low (around 0.6): both are higher, but perfusion is higher still, so it behaves a little like wasted perfusion (shunt).
  • A V/Q approaching zero is airway obstruction (a shunt, where 100% oxygen does not help). A V/Q approaching infinity is blood-flow obstruction such as a pulmonary embolism (dead space).
  • The high-oxygen apex is why reactivated tuberculosis and centrilobular emphysema favor the upper lobes.

9 · CO2 Transport FA p. 687

  • Carbon dioxide travels three ways: as bicarbonate (about 90%, generated by carbonic anhydrase in red cells with the chloride shift), bound to hemoglobin as carbaminohemoglobin (about 5%), and dissolved (about 5%).
  • The Haldane effect: in the lungs, oxygenating hemoglobin promotes the release of CO2. The Bohr effect: in the tissues, CO2 and acid promote the release of oxygen.

10 · Pulmonary Circulation FA p. 684

  • The pulmonary circulation is normally low-resistance and high-compliance.
  • Hypoxic vasoconstriction is the key reflex: alveolar hypoxia constricts local pulmonary vessels to shunt blood toward better-ventilated lung. This is the opposite of the systemic circulation, where hypoxia dilates. Chronic hypoxic vasoconstriction drives pulmonary hypertension and cor pulmonale.
  • Perfusion-limited gases (oxygen normally, CO2, nitrous oxide) equilibrate fully along the capillary. Diffusion-limited gases (carbon monoxide, and oxygen in fibrosis, emphysema, or strenuous exercise) do not.

11 · Response to High Altitude FA p. 688

  • Low atmospheric pressure lowers inspired oxygen, which lowers arterial oxygen, which triggers hyperventilation and a respiratory alkalosis.
  • Compensations: more erythropoietin and a higher hematocrit, more 2,3-BPG (a right shift to unload oxygen), and renal excretion of bicarbonate to offset the alkalosis (acetazolamide accelerates this and treats altitude sickness). Chronic hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction causes pulmonary hypertension and right ventricular hypertrophy.

12 · Response to Exercise FA p. 688

  • Oxygen consumption and CO2 production rise, and ventilation rises to match, so the arterial oxygen and CO2 partial pressures stay essentially normal.
  • Pulmonary blood flow increases and the V/Q ratio becomes more uniform from apex to base. Venous oxygen content falls and venous CO2 content rises. At extreme exertion the pH can drop from lactic acidosis.

13 · Cyanide versus Carbon Monoxide FA p. 689

  • Both poison aerobic metabolism by inhibiting complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase) of the electron transport chain, forcing anaerobic metabolism and a lactic acidosis.

Key numbers and rules

  2. Normal A-a gradient: 5 to 15.
  3. Shunt does not correct with 100% oxygen; V/Q mismatch does.
  4. Oxygen content = 1.34 times Hb times saturation plus 0.003 times partial pressure.
