Embryology · Heart tube, looping & septation FA 2025 pp. 284-286

The primitive heart tube forms from lateral plate mesoderm and is the first organ to function (first beat at week 4). The tube has five cranial-to-caudal regions that loop rightward (D-loop) and remodel into the adult chambers. Septation proceeds in four ordered acts: atrial, AV cushion, ventricular, then outflow. Each failure produces a named congenital lesion.

Aorticopulmonary septum: formed by neural crest cells migrating into the truncus and bulbus, then spiraling as it descends to split the truncus into aorta and pulmonary trunk. Three failure modes: failed formation (persistent truncus), failed spiraling (TGA), failed alignment with anterosuperior infundibular shift (tetralogy of Fallot). 22q11.2 deletion (DiGeorge) disrupts neural crest, hence the conotruncal cluster plus thymic aplasia and hypocalcemia.

Anchor Septation runs apex up, base down: muscular IVS grows up from apex, membranous IVS closes last (and is where most VSDs live). AP septum spirals: think of a barber pole twisting as it descends.
Trap Do not confuse the three outflow failures. Truncus = no septum at all (failed formation). TGA = septum present but straight, not spiraled (parallel circuits, ductal dependent). TOF = septum present but malaligned anterosuperiorly (VSD + overriding aorta + RVOT obstruction + RVH).

Embryology · Fetal circulation & adult remnants FA 2025 p. 287

Three in-utero shunts let blood bypass the non-functional liver and lungs: the ductus venosus (skips the liver), the foramen ovale (RA to LA, sends oxygenated blood to the upper body and brain), and the ductus arteriosus (PA to aorta, distal to the L subclavian). Highest O2 saturation is in the umbilical vein (~80%), highest large-vessel O2 in the fetus is the IVC. Fetal arterial saturation is ~65 to 70%, compensated by fetal hemoglobin's higher O2 affinity and higher [Hb].

The neonatal flip: first breath drops PVR sharply (alveolar O2 vasodilates pulmonary bed); cord clamp raises SVR sharply (placental low-resistance bed removed). LA pressure now exceeds RA pressure, pushing the foramen ovale flap shut. Rising PaO2 and falling PGE2 close the DA functionally over 24 to 48 hours.

Mnemonic PGE keeps PDA going, INDOmethacin shuts the DOor. PGE1 (alprostadil) maintains ductal patency in ductal-dependent cyanotic lesions (TGA, severe coarctation, HLHS, pulmonary atresia). Indomethacin (or ibuprofen, paracetamol) closes a symptomatic PDA in preterm infants.
Trap The umbilical vein carries oxygenated blood (placenta to fetus), and the umbilical arteries carry deoxygenated blood (fetus to placenta). Opposite of adult systemic logic.

Surface anatomy · Chambers & valves on the chest wall FA 2025 p. 288

From the front, the RV is most anterior (it sits directly behind the sternum and lower left parasternal chest). The LA is most posterior (it abuts the esophagus and L main bronchus). The apex is LV, palpable at the left 5th intercostal space midclavicular line. On a PA chest X-ray, the right cardiac border is RA; the left border, top to bottom, is aortic knob, then main PA, then LA appendage, then LV.

  • Stab wound, left lower parasternal chest → most often hits the RV first → hemopericardium → tamponade.
  • LA enlargement (classic in mitral stenosis) compresses the esophagus (dysphagia), L main bronchus (cough), and L recurrent laryngeal nerve (Ortner syndrome, hoarseness). Widened carinal angle on CXR.
  • Silhouette sign on CXR: RML opacity silhouettes the RA border; lingular opacity silhouettes the LV border; LLL opacity silhouettes the descending aorta.
  • Auscultation sites: aortic (R 2nd ICS), pulmonic (L 2nd ICS), Erb point (L 3rd ICS, where AR is heard), tricuspid (L lower sternal border), mitral (apex, L 5th ICS MCL).
  • LA appendage = the stasis pouch for atrial fibrillation clot. This is the embolic source in cardioembolic stroke and the reason TEE is done before cardioversion.

On chest CT: trace the great vessels by axial level. At the aortic arch level, the order anterior to posterior is SVC, ascending aorta, then arch wrapping over the trachea, with the descending aorta on the left of the spine. At the carinal level, the main PA branches into right and left, with the right PA crossing posterior to the ascending aorta. On abdominal CT, the IVC sits to the right of the aorta, anterior to the lumbar vertebrae; the left renal vein crosses anterior to the aorta and posterior to the SMA (the nutcracker corridor).

Trap The apex beat is LV, not RV. Students confuse the most anterior chamber (RV) with the apex (LV). RV occupies the lower sternal area but does not form the apex.

Coronary blood flow · Dominance, nodes & ECG territories FA 2025 p. 288

The RCA runs in the right AV groove and gives off the SA nodal artery (in ~60% of people), the AV nodal artery (in ~90% of right-dominant hearts), the acute marginal branch (RV free wall), and continues as the PDA when the heart is right-dominant. The LCA divides into the LAD (anterior interventricular groove, anterior LV + anterior 2/3 of IVS + apex) and the LCx (left AV groove, lateral LV). The PDA runs in the posterior interventricular groove and supplies the inferior LV, posterior 1/3 of IVS, the posteromedial papillary muscle, and the AV node in right-dominant hearts.

Dominance rule Whoever feeds the PDA feeds the AV node. Right-dominant (~70 to 85%): RCA → PDA → AV node, so an RCA inferior MI often comes with AV nodal block. Left-dominant (~5 to 10%): LCx → PDA → AV node. Co-dominant (~10 to 20%): both contribute.

Papillary muscles: the posteromedial muscle has a single blood supply from the PDA, which is why it ruptures 3 to 5 days after an inferior MI (acute mitral regurgitation, flash pulmonary edema). The anterolateral muscle has dual supply (LAD + LCx) and is far more resistant to ischemic rupture. The LV is perfused mostly in diastole because systolic intramural pressure compresses subendocardial vessels; the RV is perfused throughout the cycle because its systolic pressure is low.

Trap Inferior MI plus new Mobitz I (Wenckebach) AV block in a right-dominant patient does not mean pacemaker. The ischemic AV nodal block usually resolves with reperfusion. Resist the urge to implant early.

CVS Days 1-3 Review · Pathology I

Page 3 · Congenital + aortic emergencies + pericarditis

Cyanotic & acyanotic congenital heart disease FA 2025 pp. 304-307

Acyanotic (left-to-right shunts) present later, with failure to thrive or murmurs; severe long-standing shunts reverse to right-to-left when pulmonary vascular resistance climbs (Eisenmenger syndrome). The three classic acyanotic lesions are VSD (most common congenital cardiac defect, usually membranous), ASD (secundum most common; primum in Down syndrome with AV canal), and PDA (continuous machinery murmur at the left infraclavicular area, bounding pulses, wide pulse pressure).

Cyanotic (right-to-left shunts) present at birth or in early infancy. The five classics: Tetralogy of Fallot, Transposition of the great arteries, Truncus arteriosus, Tricuspid atresia, and Total anomalous pulmonary venous return.

Mnemonic · TOF = PROVe Pulmonary stenosis (RVOT obstruction, the lesion that drives severity), Right ventricular hypertrophy, Overriding aorta, VSD. Boot-shaped heart on CXR. Mechanism = anterosuperior displacement of the infundibular septum (failed AP septum alignment).

Why squatting / knee-chest position helps a TOF "tet spell": squatting kinks the femoral arteries, which sharply raises systemic vascular resistance. Higher SVR makes it harder for blood to shunt right-to-left across the VSD into the aorta, so more blood is forced through the obstructed RVOT into the pulmonary artery, getting oxygenated. The same logic explains why infants with tet spells improve when brought to the knees-to-chest position by a parent: it is a self-rescue maneuver that increases afterload.

Trap In TOF the murmur intensity reflects pulmonary stenosis severity inversely. Louder PS murmur = more flow through the RVOT = less right-to-left shunt = less cyanosis. A sudden quieter murmur during a tet spell is a bad sign, not a good one.

Aortic emergencies + subclavian steal + pericarditis FA 2025 pp. 308-311

Traumatic aortic rupture (blunt aortic injury) happens in deceleration trauma (high-speed MVA, fall from height). The tear is at the aortic isthmus, just distal to the L subclavian, where the ligamentum arteriosum tethers the mobile arch to the fixed descending aorta. CXR clues: widened mediastinum, indistinct aortic knob, deviated trachea, depressed L main bronchus, apical pleural cap. Confirm with CT angiography.

Aortic dissection: tear in the intima lets blood enter the media and create a false lumen. Sudden tearing chest or back pain radiating between the scapulae; pulse and BP asymmetry between arms; widened mediastinum on CXR. Strongest risk factor is chronic hypertension; connective tissue disease (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos, Loeys-Dietz), bicuspid aortic valve, cocaine, pregnancy, and iatrogenic injury also contribute. Stanford A involves the ascending aorta and is a surgical emergency. Stanford B begins distal to the L subclavian and is managed medically with aggressive BP control (IV beta-blocker first, then vasodilator).

Subclavian steal syndrome: atherosclerotic stenosis of the proximal subclavian artery (usually left), proximal to the vertebral artery takeoff. With ipsilateral arm exertion, the arm pulls blood backward (retrograde) down the vertebral, "stealing" from the posterior circulation. Symptoms are vertebrobasilar (dizziness, dysarthria, diplopia, drop attacks, ataxia) plus arm claudication; the giveaway sign is a >15 mmHg interarm SBP difference.

Acute pericarditis: sharp pleuritic chest pain, worse supine and better leaning forward; pericardial friction rub. ECG shows diffuse ST elevation with PR depression. Most common cause is viral (Coxsackie B, echovirus, adenovirus); other causes include uremia, post-MI (early peri-infarction pericarditis and the late Dressler syndrome at 2 to 8 weeks, autoimmune), connective tissue disease, malignancy, and bacterial. Effusion may progress to tamponade: Beck triad (hypotension, JVD, muffled sounds), pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg, low-voltage QRS with electrical alternans, RA late-diastolic collapse on echo.

Mnemonic · Beck's 3 D's Distant heart sounds, Distended neck veins, Decreased blood pressure. With pulsus paradoxus and electrical alternans, that is tamponade.
Trap Pulsus paradoxus is not paradoxical, it is exaggerated. SBP normally drops ~5 mmHg on inspiration; in tamponade the drop is >10 mmHg because the stiff pericardium forces the inspiratory increase in RV volume to bow the septum into the LV, cutting LV stroke volume.

Page 4 · Endocarditis + rheumatic disease

Infective endocarditis · Organism-by-situation FA 2025 pp. 318-319

Infective endocarditis is bacterial (or fungal) infection of the endocardium, usually on a valve leaflet. The organism that grows depends on the host situation: native valve vs prosthetic, IV drug use, recent procedure, underlying valve lesion, or colon cancer. Pair organism with situation and the question almost writes itself.

Rheumatic fever vs rheumatic heart disease FA 2025 p. 320

These are two different diseases on the same timeline. Acute rheumatic fever (ARF) is a post-streptococcal febrile illness in children 5 to 15, occurring 2 to 4 weeks after untreated group A streptococcal (S. pyogenes) pharyngitis. It is driven by molecular mimicry: antibodies against streptococcal M protein cross-react with cardiac myosin (and joint, brain, skin antigens). Rheumatic heart disease (RHD) is the chronic valvular scarring that follows, often years to decades later, classically affecting the mitral valve first (early MR from leaflet thickening, later MS from commissural fusion). The aortic valve is second most often involved; tricuspid and pulmonic are rarely affected.

Mnemonic · JONES criteria (acute RF, major) Joints (migratory polyarthritis of large joints), O (the heart shape, pancarditis), Nodules (subcutaneous, on extensor surfaces), Erythema marginatum (pink ring rash on trunk), Sydenham chorea. Need 2 major or 1 major + 2 minor (fever, arthralgia, elevated acute-phase reactants, prolonged PR), plus evidence of recent strep infection (positive throat culture, rising ASO or anti-DNase B titer).

Pathology pearls: ARF histology shows Aschoff bodies (granulomas with central fibrinoid necrosis surrounded by macrophages and lymphocytes) and Anitschkow cells (enlarged macrophages with caterpillar-shaped chromatin). RHD shows leaflet fibrosis, commissural fusion, and chordae tendineae thickening and shortening (the "fish-mouth" or "buttonhole" mitral orifice).

Treatment of ARF: penicillin (or amoxicillin) to eradicate residual strep; anti-inflammatory (NSAIDs, or corticosteroids if severe carditis). Prophylaxis after a first attack: long-acting penicillin (benzathine penicillin G IM monthly) for years to prevent recurrent strep infection and worsening valve damage. Duration depends on carditis and valve disease (5 years or until age 21 if no carditis; 10 years or until 21 if carditis without RHD; 10 years or until 40 if RHD; lifelong if prosthetic valve).

Trap Do not equate rheumatic fever with rheumatic heart disease. RF is the acute, febrile, multisystem syndrome (joints, heart, skin, brain) immediately after strep throat. RHD is the chronic valvular sequela, decades later, dominated by mitral disease. A patient can have one without (yet) the other.

Page 5 · Bridge into Physiology

From structure to function · The Physiology block ahead

Now that the anatomy and embryology are locked in, every clinical syndrome we meet from here forward is a functional consequence of one of the structures we have already placed. The next sessions move from anatomy to physiology, and every diagram you will draw rests on what we built this week.

  • The pressure-volume loop. A single rectangle that captures one heartbeat. Each corner is a valve event we just placed: MV opens (start of filling), MV closes (start of isovolumetric contraction), AV opens (start of ejection), AV closes (start of isovolumetric relaxation). Area inside the loop = stroke work.
  • The cardiac cycle & Wiggers diagram. Aligning pressure, volume, ECG, and heart sounds in time. S1 = MV+TV closure (start of systole). S2 = AV+PV closure (start of diastole). S3 = rapid passive filling (volume overload, normal in kids). S4 = atrial kick into a stiff ventricle (LVH, HOCM).
  • Frank-Starling mechanism. Higher LV end-diastolic volume (preload) stretches sarcomeres, increasing the next contraction's stroke volume. The shifted curves for heart failure (down and right) and inotropes (up and left) drive every congestive heart failure question.
  • Cardiac & nodal action potentials. Phase 0 of ventricular myocyte = fast Na+ in. Phase 2 plateau = slow Ca2+ in balanced by K+ out (the calcium that drives excitation-contraction coupling). SA node has no true resting potential; phase 4 funny current (If, Na+) is the pacemaker mechanism, and phase 0 of nodal cells is Ca2+-driven (which is why calcium channel blockers and adenosine slow the node).
  • Baroreflex & chemoreflex. The carotid sinus baroreceptor afferent travels via CN IX, the aortic arch baroreceptor afferent via CN X, because of the arch derivatives we mapped: the carotid sinus sits on the 3rd arch (CN IX), the aortic arch is the 4th L arch (CN X). The reflex is the body's beat-to-beat BP autocorrect.
  • Cardiac output equations. CO = SV x HR. SV = EDV - ESV. MAP = CO x SVR. Ejection fraction = SV / EDV. The Fick principle for CO measurement: CO = (rate of O2 consumption) / (arteriovenous O2 difference).
