Vascular wall patterns 6 cards
1Renal arteriole with onion-skin hyperplastic thickening + fibrinoid necrosis on biopsy. Underlying clinical state?
Malignant hypertension → malignant nephrosclerosis. Acute fibrinoid necrosis (plasma protein extravasation through dead endothelium) + chronic hyperplastic onion-skin pattern. Often with microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (schistocytes) from RBC fragmentation across the narrowed arterioles.
2Homogeneous pink protein deposits in intima of small arteries — pattern name + classic causes?
Hyaline arteriolosclerosis. Chronic benign hypertension and/or diabetes mellitus. Mechanism: plasma protein leakage into damaged vessel wall + ECM production.
3Concentric calcification in medial layer of medium-sized arteries with intact lumen — incidental on imaging. Diagnosis?
Monckeberg medial calcific sclerosis. Age-related; medial-only calcification rings; lumen NOT narrowed; not associated with ischemia. Easy to mistake for atherosclerosis on imaging.
4Bright pink amorphous material replacing arteriolar wall — pattern + 3 classic causes?
Fibrinoid necrosis. Causes: malignant hypertension, polyarteritis nodosa (PAN), preeclampsia/eclampsia. Mechanism: endothelial death → plasma proteins (esp. fibrinogen) flood vessel wall + necrotic smooth muscle.
5Vessel layer affected by atherosclerosis vs hypertensive arteriolosclerosis?
Atherosclerosis is fundamentally an intimal disease (lipid plaques in large + medium arteries). Hypertensive arteriolosclerosis (hyaline + hyperplastic) primarily affects the media of small arteries and arterioles.
6Why is malignant HTN associated with microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (schistocytes)?
Severely elevated BP damages arteriolar endothelium → fibrinoid necrosis + hyperplastic arteriolosclerosis narrow arteriolar lumens → RBCs fragment as they pass through → schistocytes on smear + thrombocytopenia. Same mechanism produces MAHA in TTP/HUS, DIC, but here the trigger is the BP itself.
Hypertension types & emergency 8 cards
7Primary vs secondary HTN: what proportion of patients have each?
Primary (~90%) — no identifiable cause; age, obesity, salt intake, family history. Secondary (~10%) — search for it in young patients, treatment-resistant cases, abrupt onset, or end-organ damage disproportionate to BP control.
8Most common cause of secondary HTN overall?
Renal parenchymal disease (chronic kidney disease). Renovascular disease (renal artery stenosis) is the second most common.
9Define hypertensive emergency vs urgency.
Both involve severely elevated BP (typically > 180/120). Emergency = with acute end-organ damage (encephalopathy, intracranial hemorrhage, ACS, pulmonary edema, AKI, papilledema, MAHA, eclampsia, dissection). Urgency = without acute end-organ damage.
10Why must BP be lowered gradually in hypertensive emergency?
Chronic HTN shifts the renal (and cerebral) autoregulation curve to the right — the body is calibrated to operate at higher pressures. Rapid normalization drops perfusion below the autoregulation floor → ischemic injury (e.g., ATN with granular casts, watershed strokes). Rule: lower MAP by no more than 20–25% in the first hour.
11Patient on ARB + thiazide for 3 weeks; BP unchanged; plasma renin activity nearly flat. Explanation?
Medication nonadherence. Both ARB and thiazide should drive PRA significantly higher (ARB removes angiotensin II negative feedback on JG cells; thiazide-induced volume contraction also raises renin). Flat PRA means the drugs are not in the patient's system.
12Thiazide diuretic — explain hypercalcemia + hypocalciuria.
Thiazides block Na/Cl cotransporter in DCT → ↓ intracellular Na → drives basolateral Na/Ca exchanger to extrude Ca²⁺ → ↑ Ca²⁺ reabsorption from urine → hypercalcemia + hypocalciuria. Contrast loop diuretics, which cause hypocalcemia + hypercalciuria (block Na/K/2Cl in TAL → lose paracellular Ca reabsorption). Mnemonic: "Loops Lose Ca, Thiazides Tow Ca back."
13Molecular driver of concentric LV hypertrophy in chronic HTN beyond mechanical stretch?
Angiotensin II (with endothelin as co-mediator). Local AngII has direct trophic effect on cardiomyocytes via AT1 receptors → hypertrophy independent of BP effects. This is why ACE-i / ARB / ARNi reduce LV remodeling more than other antihypertensives at equivalent BP control. β-myosin heavy chain expression is upregulated as the contractile-protein response.
14Sequence of cardiac changes in long-standing HTN leading to acute decompensated HF?
Chronic HTN → concentric LVH (sarcomeres in parallel, β-MHC up) → ↓ LV compliance + diastolic dysfunction → HFpEF → ↑ LA pressure → LA enlargement → AFib from atrial remodeling → AFib with rapid ventricular response loses atrial kick + shortens diastolic filling time → flash pulmonary edema → cardiogenic shock.
Renal artery stenosis 5 cards
15Two etiologies of renal artery stenosis and the typical patient for each?
Atherosclerotic plaque at the renal artery origin (> 90% in older patients with diffuse atherosclerosis). Fibromuscular dysplasia (typical "string-of-beads" on angiography at mid-distal renal artery) in younger women.
16Clinical clues to RAS?
Refractory HTN despite multiple drugs; recurrent flash pulmonary edema; abdominal bruit; AKI after starting ACE-i / ARB (efferent arteriolar constriction was holding GFR up); asymmetric kidney sizes.
17Pathology in the stenotic kidney vs the contralateral kidney in unilateral RAS?
Stenotic kidney → ischemic atrophy (cortical thinning, tubular atrophy, interstitial fibrosis, crowded glomeruli). Contralateral kidney → exposed to systemic HTN driven by stenotic-kidney renin → hyaline arteriolosclerosis. The stenotic kidney is protected from HTN injury but suffers hypoperfusion; the other is normally perfused but exposed to HTN.
18Why does ACE-i drop GFR in patients with bilateral RAS?
In RAS the kidneys depend on AngII-mediated efferent arteriolar constriction to maintain glomerular filtration pressure. ACE-i blocks AngII formation → efferent vessel dilates → glomerular hydrostatic pressure falls → GFR drops sharply → AKI. Same mechanism, milder, in single-kidney patients or volume-depleted CKD.
19RAS treatment principles?
Medical: optimize antihypertensives (avoid ACE-i / ARB if AKI or bilateral RAS), statin, antiplatelet, smoking cessation. Revascularization (stent) reserved for refractory HTN despite max medical therapy, flash pulmonary edema, or progressive renal dysfunction — does not improve survival in unselected RAS.
Aortic aneurysms 8 cards
20Primary mechanism of abdominal aortic aneurysm formation?
Chronic inflammation of the aortic wall — macrophages release matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) + elastases that degrade elastin and collagen → progressive medial weakening + dilation. Vasa vasorum atherosclerosis + intimal thickening worsen medial ischemia.
21USPSTF screening recommendation for AAA?
One-time abdominal ultrasound for men age 65–75 who have ever smoked.
22Counterintuitive risk factor that is mildly protective against AAA?
Diabetes mellitus. Mechanism unclear — possibly related to glycation effects on matrix remodeling. Other risks (age, male, smoking, HTN, family history) all increase AAA risk.
23AAA repair indications?
> 5.5 cm maximum diameter, expansion > 0.5 cm in 6 months, symptomatic, or ruptured. Asymptomatic 4.0–5.4 cm = surveillance imaging every 6–12 months.
24Triad of ruptured AAA + the two ecchymosis signs of contained retroperitoneal rupture?
Triad: sudden severe abdominal/back pain + hypotension/syncope + pulsatile abdominal mass. Retroperitoneal contained rupture: Grey-Turner sign (flank ecchymosis) + Cullen sign (periumbilical ecchymosis).
25Thoracic aortic aneurysm compression triad?
Cough (tracheal compression) + dysphagia (esophageal compression) + hoarseness (left recurrent laryngeal nerve compression as it loops under the aortic arch). All three signal an expanding TAA.
26Histology of TAA = cystic medial degeneration. Describe.
Fragmentation of elastic lamellae in the tunica media in a "basket-weave" pattern + cystic collections of mucopolysaccharide (myxoid material) + loss of medial smooth muscle. Same histology underlies aortic dissection. Causes: acquired (aging + HTN) and inherited (Marfan/FBN1, EDS vascular/COL3A1, Loeys-Dietz).
27Lathyrism — what enzyme is inhibited, and why does it cause aortic disease?
Sweet pea (Lathyrus) seeds contain β-aminopropionitrile, which inhibits lysyl oxidase — the copper-dependent enzyme that cross-links collagen and elastin in the aortic media. Without cross-links the media weakens → cystic medial degeneration → aneurysm. Same enzymatic defect underlies Menkes disease (copper transport defect) and Ehlers-Danlos kyphoscoliotic type.
Aortic dissection 6 cards
28Triggering event in aortic dissection?
Tear in the aortic intima — usually 1–5 cm, transverse or oblique. Pressurized blood enters the media → propagates proximally and/or distally → creates a false lumen alongside the true lumen.
29Strongest risk factor for aortic dissection?
Chronic hypertension. Other risks: connective tissue disease (Marfan, EDS vascular, Loeys-Dietz), bicuspid aortic valve, cocaine, pregnancy (3rd trimester), iatrogenic (catheterization, surgery), severe trauma.
30Stanford A vs B — definition and treatment?
Stanford A = involves the ascending aorta → surgical emergency. Stanford B = distal to the left subclavian only → medical management with aggressive BP control.
31Treatment ordering in acute dissection?
IV β-blocker (esmolol) FIRST to reduce dP/dt (shear stress on the aortic wall). Then add a vasodilator (nitroprusside) if BP still elevated. NEVER start a pure vasodilator alone — reflex tachycardia worsens shear and can propagate the dissection. Type A also needs urgent surgery.
32Five major complications of aortic dissection by extension territory?
Pericardial rupture → tamponade, acute AR (root extension), MI (coronary ostium occlusion), stroke (carotid extension), limb / renal / mesenteric ischemia (distal extensions).
33Marfan syndrome — gene + classic features?
Autosomal dominant FBN1 mutation → defective fibrillin-1 → loss of microfibril scaffolding for elastin. Features: tall stature with arm span > height, arachnodactyly, pectus excavatum/carinatum, joint hypermobility, ectopia lentis (upward and outward), mitral valve prolapse, aortic root dilation → AR and dissection. Contrast homocystinuria: marfanoid habitus but ectopia lentis is downward, plus mental retardation and thromboembolism.
Vasculitis differential 12 cards
3425-year-old Asian woman with constitutional symptoms, claudication, BP discrepancy between arms (160/90 R vs 120/80 L), subclavian bruit, ESR 110. Diagnosis?
Takayasu arteritis ("pulseless disease"). Large-vessel granulomatous vasculitis of the aorta + its major branches in young Asian women. Treatment: high-dose steroids + immunosuppression.
35Demographics and classic features of giant cell (temporal) arteritis?
Adults > 50, more common in women. Large-vessel granulomatous vasculitis affecting aortic arch + branches, especially the temporal artery. Features: new-onset unilateral headache, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, vision loss (anterior ischemic optic neuropathy, CRAO), polymyalgia rheumatica association, ESR > 100. Start steroids before biopsy to prevent vision loss.
364-year-old with 5 days of fever + bilateral nonexudative conjunctivitis + strawberry tongue + cracked lips + palmar erythema + cervical lymphadenopathy. Diagnosis and major complication?
Kawasaki disease (CRASH and burn: Conjunctivitis, Rash, Adenopathy, Strawberry tongue, Hand/foot changes + Fever ≥ 5 days). Medium-vessel vasculitis. Major complication: coronary artery aneurysm with risk of MI in children. Treatment: IVIG + high-dose aspirin (one of the few pediatric aspirin indications).
37PAN — three distinguishing features from the ANCA-associated vasculitides?
(1) ANCA-NEGATIVE. (2) Spares the pulmonary arteries. (3) Strong hepatitis B association (immune-complex mediated). Medium-vessel necrotizing vasculitis with fibrinoid necrosis + microaneurysms ("string-of-beads" on angiography). Multi-system: skin, renal (HTN, not GN), nerves (mononeuritis multiplex), GI (mesenteric ischemia, acalculous cholecystitis), musculoskeletal.
3830-year-old male heavy smoker with painful digital ulcers + Raynaud + tender erythematous superficial vein. Diagnosis + treatment?
Thromboangiitis obliterans (Buerger disease). Segmental small/medium vessel vasculitis triggered by tobacco. Histology: segmental inflammatory intraluminal thrombi with vessel wall sparing + extension into contiguous veins AND nerves. Smoking cessation is the only effective treatment (essentially curative if achieved; relapse with resumption).
39c-ANCA (PR3-ANCA) positive + sinusitis + lung cavitation + RPGN. Diagnosis?
Granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA, formerly Wegener's). Small-vessel ANCA-associated vasculitis. Classic triad: upper airway (sinusitis, saddle-nose, otitis) + lower airway (lung nodules, cavitation, hemoptysis) + renal (pauci-immune crescentic GN). Treatment: cyclophosphamide or rituximab + steroids.
40p-ANCA + asthma + eosinophilia + mononeuritis multiplex + granulomas on biopsy. Diagnosis?
Eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (EGPA, formerly Churg-Strauss). ANCA-associated small-vessel vasculitis. Distinctive combo: asthma + peripheral eosinophilia (often > 10%) + necrotizing granulomatous vasculitis + neuropathy.
41p-ANCA + renal + lung but NO granulomas + NO upper airway involvement. Diagnosis?
Microscopic polyangiitis (MPA). ANCA-associated small-vessel vasculitis with renal (pauci-immune crescentic GN) + pulmonary (alveolar hemorrhage) involvement. No granulomas and no upper airway involvement distinguish from GPA.
42Child with post-URI palpable purpura on lower extremities + abdominal pain + arthralgia + microhematuria with RBC casts. Diagnosis?
IgA vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein purpura). Non-ANCA small-vessel vasculitis. Tetrad: palpable purpura legs + abdominal pain (intussusception risk) + arthralgia + IgA nephropathy. Most kids recover with supportive care; renal involvement determines long-term outcome.
43Elderly man passes out repeatedly while buttoning a tight collar. Mechanism?
Carotid sinus hypersensitivity. Pressure on the carotid sinus → exaggerated baroreceptor firing → CN IX afferent → medulla → ↑ vagal output + ↓ sympathetic → bradycardia + vasodilation → syncope. Treatment: avoid triggers; pacemaker if cardioinhibitory predominates with recurrent syncope.
44What unique histologic feature distinguishes Buerger disease from PAN on biopsy?
Buerger involves contiguous veins AND nerves with the artery (segmental inflammatory thrombi extend across the neurovascular bundle). PAN is artery-only with transmural fibrinoid necrosis. PAN spares lungs; Buerger spares larger arteries and rarely involves visceral vessels.
45Which two arteritides cause aortic arch + branch involvement with granulomas, distinguished by age?
Both are large-vessel granulomatous vasculitides. Giant cell (temporal) arteritis in patients > 50 (temporal artery + arch). Takayasu arteritis in patients < 40, predominantly Asian women ("pulseless disease"). Histology overlaps; demographics + clinical features distinguish.
Atherosclerosis & PAD 8 cards
46Why is the abdominal aorta the site of the greatest atherosclerotic burden in the body?
Combination of factors: turbulent flow at bifurcations, branch points, and curves; tenuous vasa vasorum in the infrarenal segment increasing medial ischemia; chronic exposure to systemic risk factors. This also explains why AAA forms preferentially infrarenally.
4764-year-old smoker with thigh claudication + decreased sexual performance + diminished femoral pulses. Eponym?
Leriche syndrome = aortoiliac occlusive disease. Triad: bilateral lower-extremity claudication (hip/thigh/buttock) + erectile dysfunction + absent or diminished femoral pulses. Underlying lesion: lipid-filled intimal plaques in large arteries.
48Six Ps of acute limb ischemia?
Pain, Pallor, Poikilothermia (cold), Paresthesia, Paralysis, Pulselessness.
4935-year-old concert pianist with acute right leg ischemia → embolectomy reveals a gelatinous mass with stellate cells in mucopolysaccharide stroma. Source of the embolus?
Cardiac myxoma — most common primary cardiac tumor; ~80% arise in the left atrium attached to the interatrial septum near the fossa ovalis. Histology pathognomonic: stellate myxoma cells in a mucopolysaccharide-rich extracellular matrix. Can also cause ball-valve obstruction ("tumor plop"), constitutional symptoms (fever, weight loss, ↑ IL-6).
50Why does CK surge immediately after a successful femoral embolectomy?
Reperfusion injury. After restoration of blood flow to ischemic muscle: (1) oxygen free radical generation, (2) mitochondrial permeability transition, (3) inflammation/neutrophil recruitment, (4) complement activation — together accelerate cell death of marginally viable myocytes. CK leaks specifically because of cell membrane damage (loss of sarcolemmal integrity).
51Recurrent stroke + renal infarction + flank pain + wedge lesion on CT in a 64-year-old with HTN/T2DM. Most likely embolic source?
Atrial fibrillation (LA appendage thrombus → systemic embolism). The kidney is an end-organ — branch occlusion produces a coagulative wedge infarct. CK normal, but LDH elevated out of proportion to other markers; serum creatinine often normal because the contralateral kidney compensates.
52Coronary artery atheroma — which cell type drives migration from media to intima during plaque growth?
Smooth muscle cells. They migrate from the media into the intima under PDGF (platelet-derived growth factor) stimulation released by adherent platelets + macrophages + dysfunctional endothelium. SMCs then proliferate and secrete collagen → form the fibrous cap.
53Which two arterial beds are notably resistant to atherosclerosis?
Internal thoracic (internal mammary) arteries — used as the preferred CABG conduit (LIMA) because of 90% 10-year patency. Pulmonary arteries are also relatively spared in primary atherosclerosis (low pressure system). Mechanism not fully understood — likely endothelial differences + lack of vasa vasorum.
Venous disease 4 cards
54Underlying defect in varicose veins?
Incompetent venous valves. Chronic ↑ venous pressure from prolonged standing, pregnancy, obesity → vein dilation → valve incompetence → reflux → more dilation (vicious cycle). Risks: standing occupation, pregnancy, obesity, female sex, age, family history.
55Bronze-brown skin pigmentation of the legs in chronic venous insufficiency — mechanism?
Hemosiderin deposition. Chronic capillary hypertension → RBC extravasation into dermis → macrophages break down RBCs → iron stored as hemosiderin in dermis → brown discoloration.
56Venous vs arterial ulcer location?
Venous ulcers at the medial malleolus (gravity + stasis). Arterial ulcers at the lateral malleolus / toes / pressure points (poor distal perfusion). Venous ulcers moist with irregular borders + surrounding stasis dermatitis; arterial ulcers dry, punched-out, painful, with cool atrophic surrounding skin.
57Full feature set of chronic venous insufficiency?
Varicose veins + stasis dermatitis (erythema, scaling) + hemosiderin pigmentation + lipodermatosclerosis ("inverted champagne bottle" leg from chronic fibrotic induration) + stasis ulcers at medial malleolus + evening-worsening edema relieved by elevation.
Coarctation cross-reference 3 cards
5832-year-old with ruptured cerebral berry aneurysm + congenital heart defect. Most likely associated condition?
Coarctation of the aorta. Upper-body hypertension from the narrowing (proximal to the coarct) damages cerebral arteries → predisposes to berry aneurysm formation and rupture. Other berry aneurysm associations: ADPKD, Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos, smoking, HTN.
59Coarctation clinical features?
↑ BP + strong pulses in upper extremities; ↓ BP + weak pulses in lower extremities; brachial-femoral delay; rib notching from intercostal collaterals on CXR; lower-extremity claudication. Neonates with critical coarctation develop HF + shock when the ductus closes (PGE1 keeps it open until surgery).
60Major syndromic associations of coarctation?
Turner syndrome (45, X) — classic combination of coarctation + bicuspid aortic valve. Bicuspid aortic valve on its own. Berry aneurysm. Also seen in DiGeorge (22q11.2 deletion) in conjunction with conotruncal defects.
