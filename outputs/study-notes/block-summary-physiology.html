This wrap-up consolidates the three physiology sessions: pump mechanics, auscultation, and the conduction-plus-regulation duo. Use it as a single-sitting review before moving into pathology. Page anchors at the bottom point to the First Aid 2025 cardiovascular chapter.

1. Pump Mechanics (Day 4)

Core equations

  • CO = SV × HR (cardiac output is stroke volume times heart rate).
  • SV = EDV − ESV (stroke volume is what is left after ejection).
  • EF = SV / EDV (normal 55-70%).
  • MAP ≈ DBP + 1/3 (SBP − DBP) at rest. At high heart rates the fraction approaches 1/2.
  • Fick principle: CO = oxygen consumption / (arterial − venous oxygen content).

The three pump levers

LeverWhat it isRaised byLowered by
Preload End-diastolic ventricular volume; stretch on the sarcomere before contraction Venous return, fluid loading, sympathetic venoconstriction Venodilators (nitroglycerin), hemorrhage, raised intrathoracic pressure
Afterload Wall stress during ejection; approximated by MAP for LV Hypertension, aortic stenosis, vasoconstrictors ACE inhibitors, hydralazine, nitroprusside
Contractility Intrinsic strength of myocyte contraction independent of load Sympathetic stimulation, catecholamines, digoxin, hypercalcemia Beta-blockers, non-dihydropyridine CCBs, acidosis, hypoxia, heart failure

Laplace law

  • Wall stress = (Pressure × radius) / (2 × wall thickness).
  • Explains why a dilated heart works against more wall stress at the same blood pressure.
  • Concentric hypertrophy (thickened walls, normal cavity) is the heart's compensation for pressure overload (hypertension, AS).
  • Eccentric hypertrophy (dilated cavity, sarcomeres added in series) is the response to volume overload (AR, MR, chronic anemia, high-output states).

Frank-Starling relationship

  • Greater end-diastolic volume stretches sarcomeres toward optimal actin-myosin overlap, increasing stroke volume on the next beat.
  • The curve shifts down and right in heart failure (lower SV for any given preload).
  • The curve shifts up and left with positive inotropes (digoxin, dobutamine, exercise).

Pressure-volume loop landmarks

Going counterclockwise from filling:

  1. Mitral closes at the end of diastole at the EDV point. Begin isovolumetric contraction (volume constant, pressure rises rapidly).
  2. Aortic opens when LV pressure exceeds aortic pressure. Begin ejection (volume falls).
  3. Aortic closes at the ESV point when LV pressure drops below aortic. Begin isovolumetric relaxation.
  4. Mitral opens when LV pressure drops below LA pressure. Begin diastolic filling.
Pressure ^ | ___aortic close___ eject | / \ ---- | / iso. contraction \ iso. relaxation | | | |_|_____filling_________|___> Volume ESV EDV
  • The slope of the line connecting end-systolic points (ESPVR) reflects contractility. Steeper slope means stronger contraction.
  • The diastolic curve (EDPVR) reflects ventricular compliance. A stiff ventricle has a steeper EDPVR.

PV loop shapes in valvular disease

  • Aortic stenosis: very high LV pressure during ejection, narrow loop (SV reduced).
  • Aortic regurgitation: wide loop with high SV and rising end-diastolic volume; the isovolumetric relaxation segment is absent (continuous backflow into LV).
  • Mitral regurgitation: isovolumetric contraction segment disappears (LV starts losing volume backward into LA as soon as it contracts); large total SV but reduced forward SV.
  • Mitral stenosis: the LV side looks small (low EDV), with high left atrial pressures driving the abnormality.

2. Heart Sounds and Murmurs (Day 5)

Normal sounds

  • S1 is the closure of the mitral and tricuspid valves at the start of systole.
  • S2 is the closure of the aortic and pulmonic valves at the start of diastole. A2 normally precedes P2 because LV pressure falls before RV pressure.
  • S3 is an early diastolic, low-frequency sound from rapid filling into a volume-loaded or floppy LV. It can be normal in children and athletes; pathologic in HFrEF, MR, AR, high-output states.
  • S4 is a late diastolic sound from the atrium kicking blood into a stiff LV. Pathologic in HFpEF, hypertension, HCM, restrictive cardiomyopathy, acute MI; can be normal in older adults.

S2 splitting patterns

PatternMechanismClassic cause

Systolic murmurs

  • Aortic stenosis. Crescendo-decrescendo at the right upper sternal border, radiating to the carotids. Triad of angina, syncope on exertion, heart failure. Pulse is delayed and weak (parvus et tardus). Paradoxical S2 splitting in severe cases.
  • Mitral regurgitation. Holosystolic at the apex, radiating to the axilla. Worse with squatting and hand-grip.
  • Mitral valve prolapse. Mid-systolic click followed by a late systolic murmur. Click moves earlier with Valsalva or standing.
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy. Crescendo-decrescendo at the left lower sternal border. Louder with maneuvers that reduce LV volume (Valsalva strain, standing).
  • Ventricular septal defect. Harsh holosystolic at the left lower sternal border. Smaller defects make louder murmurs.
  • Tricuspid regurgitation. Holosystolic at the left lower sternal border, louder with inspiration (Carvallo sign).

Diastolic murmurs

  • Aortic regurgitation. Early decrescendo at the left lower sternal border (or Erb point). Wide pulse pressure. Signs: water-hammer pulse, head bobbing (de Musset), pistol-shot femorals, Quincke nail-bed pulsations.
  • Mitral stenosis. Opening snap after S2 followed by a low-pitched diastolic rumble at the apex, best heard in left lateral decubitus. Most commonly post-rheumatic.

Auscultation map

  • Aortic: right 2nd intercostal space.
  • Pulmonic: left 2nd intercostal space.
  • Erb point: left 3rd intercostal space (best for AR).
  • Tricuspid: left lower sternal border (4th to 5th intercostal space).
  • Mitral / apex: 5th intercostal space, midclavicular line.

3. Conduction and Regulation (Day 6)

Ventricular action potential

  • Phase 0: rapid Na+ influx, upstroke.
  • Phase 1: transient K+ efflux, brief notch.
  • Phase 2: plateau; Ca2+ entry through L-type channels is balanced by K+ efflux.
  • Phase 3: repolarization driven by K+ efflux as Ca2+ channels close.
  • Phase 4: resting membrane potential maintained by K+ leak.
  • Effective refractory period spans phases 0-3 and prevents tetany.

Pacemaker (nodal) action potential

  • Phase 4: spontaneous depolarization driven by the funny current (HCN channels, Na+ in) plus T-type Ca2+ influx. This is what makes nodal cells automatic.
  • Phase 0: slow upstroke from L-type Ca2+ entry (no fast Na+ channels here).
  • Phase 3: repolarization from K+ efflux.
  • No phase 1 or 2.
  • Drug targets: beta-blockers and non-DHP CCBs slow phase 4 (lower HR); adenosine hyperpolarizes the node (terminates AVNRT); ivabradine blocks the funny current.

Conduction system

  • SA node → atrial myocardium → AV node (key delay that lets atrial kick complete) → bundle of His → right and left bundle branches → Purkinje network → ventricular myocardium.
  • Conduction velocity order: Purkinje > atrial > ventricular > AV node.
  • Escape rate hierarchy: SA node 60-100, junctional 40-60, ventricular 20-40.

ECG basics

  • P wave: atrial depolarization.
  • PR interval: AV nodal delay (normal 120-200 ms). Prolongation is first-degree AV block.
  • QRS complex: ventricular depolarization (normal < 120 ms). Wide QRS suggests bundle branch block or ventricular origin.
  • ST segment: ventricular plateau (phase 2). Elevation suggests transmural injury; depression suggests subendocardial ischemia.
  • T wave: ventricular repolarization.
  • QT interval: total ventricular activity, rate-corrected (QTc). Prolongation predisposes to torsades de pointes.

Baroreflex (regulation of blood pressure beat-to-beat)

  • Stretch receptors in the carotid sinus signal through CN IX; stretch receptors in the aortic arch signal through CN X.
  • Both feed the nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS) in the medulla.
  • Rising BP: NTS triggers parasympathetic outflow (slows HR via vagus) and withdraws sympathetic tone (vasodilation, slower HR, lower contractility).
  • Falling BP: NTS allows sympathetic surge (vasoconstriction, faster HR, increased contractility). This is the orthostatic response.
  • Carotid sinus hypersensitivity, vasovagal syncope, and reflex bradycardia after sustained hypertension are all baroreflex phenomena.

Chemoreceptors

  • Peripheral chemoreceptors (carotid and aortic bodies) respond to falling PO2, rising PCO2, and falling pH. They drive ventilation and sympathetic outflow.
  • Central chemoreceptors (ventral medulla) respond to rising H+ in cerebrospinal fluid (from CO2 crossing the blood-brain barrier). They drive ventilation only, not sympathetic output.

Coronary autoregulation

  • The heart extracts more than 70% of delivered oxygen at rest, so any increased demand must be met by increased flow.
  • Mediators: adenosine (released by ischemic myocytes) and nitric oxide (endothelial).
  • Autoregulation maintains coronary flow across MAP 60-140 mmHg.
  • LV perfusion happens mostly during diastole because systolic compression squeezes intramural vessels. The subendocardium is the most vulnerable to ischemia.

MAP, SVR, CO relationship

  • MAP = CO × SVR. This is the single most useful equation in shock physiology.
  • Hypotension must come from a failing pump (low CO) or a failing vascular tone (low SVR), or both.
  • Shock framework that follows from this equation:
    • Hypovolemic: low preload → low CO → low MAP.
    • Cardiogenic: low contractility → low CO → low MAP.
    • Obstructive: mechanical block to filling or ejection → low CO → low MAP.
    • Distributive: catastrophically low SVR → low MAP despite high CO.

Quick Recall (Physiology Block)

  • CO = SV × HR. SV is set by preload, afterload, contractility.
  • EF normal 55-70%. HFrEF ≤ 40%, HFpEF ≥ 50% with diastolic dysfunction.
  • Laplace explains concentric (pressure) vs eccentric (volume) hypertrophy.
  • PV loop, four corners: mitral close, aortic open, aortic close, mitral open.
  • S3 = volume / floppy LV. S4 = pressure / stiff LV.
  • Fixed split S2 = ASD. Paradoxical split = LBBB or severe AS.
  • Maneuvers: HCM and MVP get LOUDER with reduced preload. Everything else gets softer.
  • Ventricular AP: Na in, K out, Ca plateau, K out, K leak.
  • Nodal AP: funny current + T-type Ca2+ (phase 4), L-type Ca2+ upstroke (phase 0).
  • Conduction speed: Purkinje > atrial > ventricular > AV node.
  • Baroreceptors: carotid via CN IX, aortic via CN X, both to NTS.
  • Coronary autoregulation: adenosine + NO, MAP 60-140, diastolic perfusion, subendocardium first to ischemia.
  • MAP = CO × SVR. Memorize. The whole shock framework lives here.
