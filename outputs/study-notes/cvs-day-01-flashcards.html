Embryology — heart tube, looping, septation 10 cards
1Which is the first organ to become functional in the human embryo, and at what week does it start beating?
The heart. It begins beating around week 4.
2What is the embryologic consequence of an L-loop of the heart tube?
Dextrocardia — the heart sits on the right side.
3Which septum is the last to close during atrial septation, and what defect results from its failure?
The foramen ovale closes last (functionally at birth; anatomic fusion over weeks). Failure = patent foramen ovale (PFO).
4Cryptogenic ischemic stroke. Bubble study shows bubbles in the left heart. Most likely mechanism?
Failed fusion between septum primum and secundum → patent foramen ovale; paradoxical embolism.
5Which interventricular septum portion closes last, and where do most VSDs occur?
The membranous IVS closes last; most VSDs occur there.
6Which embryologic structure, when it fails to fuse with the primum septum, produces a primum-type ASD? Classic syndrome?
The endocardial cushions. Classic in Down syndrome.
7Embryologic mechanism of transposition of the great arteries?
Failed spiraling of the aorticopulmonary septum. Aorta arises anteriorly from RV, pulmonary trunk posteriorly from LV.
8Embryologic mechanism of persistent truncus arteriosus?
Failure of the AP septum to form/divide — a single outflow vessel persists.
9Embryologic mechanism of tetralogy of Fallot?
Anterosuperior deviation of the infundibular septum due to abnormal neural crest cell migration. Yields VSD + overriding aorta + RV outflow obstruction + RV hypertrophy.
10Why does DiGeorge syndrome (22q11.2) cause conotruncal defects?
Outflow tract septation requires neural crest cells, which are abnormal. Also explains thymic aplasia and parathyroid hypoplasia.
Pharyngeal arch derivatives 5 cards
11Adult artery derived from the 3rd pharyngeal arch?
Common carotid + proximal internal carotid.
12Ductus arteriosus is the embryologic derivative of which arch?
The 6th (left) pharyngeal arch artery.
13Cranial nerve paired with the 6th pharyngeal arch?
CN X (vagus) — specifically, the recurrent laryngeal branch.
14Why does the left recurrent laryngeal nerve hook under the aortic arch?
It loops around the 6th arch derivative, which on the left becomes the ligamentum arteriosum, tethered to the arch.
15Which arch gives rise to the proximal right subclavian artery?
The 4th pharyngeal arch (right side).
Fetal circulation & neonatal flip 6 cards
16Which fetal vessel carries the highest oxygen saturation?
The umbilical vein (~80% SpO₂).
17Which large fetal vessel in the body carries the highest SpO₂ once mixed?
The IVC (after ductus venosus dumps oxygenated UV blood into it; ~67%).
18Three in utero shunts and their postnatal remnants?
Ductus venosus → ligamentum venosum · foramen ovale → fossa ovalis · ductus arteriosus → ligamentum arteriosum.
19Two physiologic changes that close the ductus arteriosus at birth?
↑ oxygen tension and ↓ prostaglandin E₂ (loss of placental PGE source).
20Drug that keeps a PDA open? Drug that closes one?
Keep open: PGE1 (alprostadil). Close: indomethacin.
21Three hemodynamic changes at birth?
↓ PVR (lungs inflate, ↑PaO₂); ↑ SVR (placenta removed); ↑ LV contribution to systemic flow.
Chambers & surface anatomy 8 cards
22Most anterior cardiac chamber? Clinical relevance?
Right ventricle. Penetrating chest wounds at left sternal border typically injure the RV → tamponade.
23Most posterior cardiac chamber? Symptoms when it enlarges?
Left atrium. Compresses esophagus (dysphagia) and left recurrent laryngeal nerve (hoarseness).
24On PA CXR, which chamber forms the right border of the cardiac silhouette?
The right atrium.
25Right middle lobe pneumonia obliterates which cardiac border? Name of the sign?
The right atrial (right heart) border — the silhouette sign.
26Stab wound at the left 5th ICS at the midclavicular line — what is most likely injured first?
The left lung. The cardiac apex is at this landmark but the lung overlies it.
27Where do ~90% of thrombi form in atrial fibrillation, and why?
The left atrial appendage — pectinate-muscle stasis with poor atrial contraction.
28Surface landmark for auscultating the tricuspid valve?
Left lower sternal border, 4th–5th ICS.
29IV drug user, new murmur, fever, cavitary lung lesions. Valve and organism?
Tricuspid endocarditis, most commonly Staphylococcus aureus.
Coronary arteries 10 cards
30Define coronary dominance.
The vessel that gives rise to the PDA is the dominant one. ~75–80% right · ~5–10% left · ~10–20% codominant.
31In left-dominant circulation, which artery supplies the AV node?
The left circumflex — whoever feeds the PDA feeds the AV node.
32Isolated RV infarct with a normal LV — vessel? dominance?
RCA in a left-dominant patient. RCA still supplies RV; LCx feeds the inferior LV via PDA so it is spared.
33Which papillary muscle has a single arterial supply, and from which vessel?
Posteromedial papillary muscle — supplied solely by the PDA.
34Acute severe MR + pulmonary edema 3–5 days post-inferior MI. Mechanism?
Posteromedial papillary muscle rupture from single PDA-derived supply.
35Where does the coronary sinus lie? Where does it drain?
In the posterior atrioventricular groove; drains into the right atrium.
36Embryologic origin of the coronary sinus? What does a dilated CS suggest?
Left horn of the sinus venosus. Dilated CS → suspect persistent left SVC draining into it.
37Trace a biventricular pacemaker lead from subclavian to the LV.
Subclavian vein → SVC → RA → CS ostium → posterior AV groove → lateral cardiac vein over LV free wall.
38ST elevation in I and aVL — culprit vessel?
Left circumflex (lateral wall infarction).
39ST elevation in II, III, aVF — culprit vessel?
Right coronary artery (inferior wall).
Conduction system 4 cards
40Location of the SA node?
Right atrium, near the junction with the SVC — subepicardial, at the upper end of the crista terminalis.
41Location of the AV node and the anatomic triangle that defines it?
Interatrial septum just above the septal leaflet of the tricuspid valve, near the coronary sinus ostium. Triangle of Koch = tricuspid annulus + tendon of Todaro + CS ostium.
42Speed order of cardiac conduction?
Purkinje > Atria > Ventricles > AV node (Park At Ventura, AV). Slow AV node = atrial kick.
43Inferior MI + Mobitz I AV block. Which artery?
The RCA (supplies AV node in ~90% of patients).
Procedures — anatomy applied 3 cards
44PCWP approximates which intracardiac pressure, and why?
Left atrial pressure. The inflated balloon creates a static column through pulmonary capillaries → pulmonary veins → LA.
45In transseptal LA catheterization, what structure is punctured?
The interatrial septum at the fossa ovalis — the thin remnant of the foramen ovale.
46CABG: surface landmarks for the great saphenous vein?
Distal: anterior to medial malleolus. Proximal: through the saphenous opening to join the femoral vein medial to the pubic tubercle.
Pericardium & effusion 4 cards
47Classic chest X-ray finding of a large pericardial effusion?
Enlarged "water-bottle" globular cardiac silhouette with clear lung fields.
48Two ECG findings that together suggest large pericardial effusion?
Low-voltage QRS (fluid insulates the leads) + electrical alternans (heart swings in the fluid).
49Most common viral causes of acute pericarditis?
Coxsackievirus B and echovirus.
50Triad of cardiac tamponade?
Beck's triad: hypotension + JVD + muffled heart sounds. Add pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg.
Aorta & great vessels 6 cards
51Branches of the aortic arch in order?
Brachiocephalic → left common carotid → left subclavian.
52Stanford classification of aortic dissection — management for each?
Type A: ascending aorta → emergency surgery. Type B: distal to L subclavian → medical (IV beta-blocker, then nitroprusside).
53Where does a Stanford type B dissection most commonly originate?
Near the origin of the left subclavian artery — site of highest shear stress.
54Where does blunt traumatic aortic injury most commonly occur, and why?
At the aortic isthmus (just distal to L subclavian), tethered by the ligamentum arteriosum.
55Path of an atheroembolus that causes monocular vision loss + cherry-red spot?
Internal carotid artery → ophthalmic artery → central retinal artery.
56Describe the flow in subclavian steal syndrome.
Proximal subclavian stenosis → arm activity → retrograde flow down the ipsilateral vertebral artery to perfuse the arm. Brain "loses" flow → vertebrobasilar symptoms + >15 mmHg interarm SBP difference.
Abdominal & pelvic vascular anatomy 6 cards
57Vertebral level of IVC formation, and from which veins?
L4–L5, by the union of the right and left common iliac veins.
58Why is a left-sided varicocele common but a right-sided varicocele in an adult alarming?
Left gonadal vein drains into the left renal vein, which can be compressed (nutcracker). Right gonadal drains directly into the IVC, so a right varicocele suggests IVC obstruction — classically renal cell carcinoma with tumor thrombus.
59Describe nutcracker syndrome.
Compression of the left renal vein between the aorta (posterior) and SMA (anterior) → ↑ pressure in left renal and left gonadal veins → left flank pain + hematuria ± left varicocele.
60Femoral triangle borders + NAVEL acronym?
Inguinal lig. (sup), sartorius (lat), adductor longus (med). N-A-V-E-L lateral → medial: Nerve, Artery, Vein, Empty space, Lymphatics. Vein is immediately medial to artery.
61Why is a femoral artery puncture above the inguinal ligament dangerous?
Can lacerate the external iliac artery or inferior epigastric artery → bleeding into the retroperitoneal space (not compressible). Post-cath hypotension without external bleeding.
62The inferior epigastric artery branches off which vessel and runs in which direction?
Off the external iliac just proximal to the inguinal ligament. Runs superomedially up the anterior abdominal wall.
Upper-body venous anatomy 3 cards
63Two veins that unite to form the brachiocephalic vein on each side?
The subclavian vein and the internal jugular vein.
64Unilateral right face and arm swelling but normal left side. Vessel? Classic cause?
Right brachiocephalic (innominate) vein — classically apical lung tumor (Pancoast) or central-line thrombus.
65Embryologic origin of the SVC?
The common cardinal veins.
