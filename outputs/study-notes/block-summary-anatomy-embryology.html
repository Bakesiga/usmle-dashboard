1. Heart Tube and Early Embryology (Day 1)

Origin of the heart

  • By day 18 of gestation, the cardiogenic field appears as a horseshoe of splanchnic mesoderm in front of the developing neural tube.
  • Lateral folding brings paired endocardial tubes together by day 22. They fuse on the midline to form a single primitive heart tube that begins beating almost immediately.
  • The first beat at day 22 is one of the earliest functional events in human embryology and is driven by spontaneous pacemaker activity even before any discrete conduction tissue exists.

Primitive dilations of the heart tube

The heart tube develops five sequential bulges. Each one gives rise to an adult structure.

Primitive dilationAdult derivative
Truncus arteriosusAscending aorta + pulmonary trunk
Bulbus cordisSmooth outflow of right ventricle (conus arteriosus) + smooth outflow of left ventricle (aortic vestibule)
Primitive ventricleTrabeculated left ventricle
Primitive atriumTrabeculated parts of both atria
Sinus venosusSmooth posterior wall of right atrium, coronary sinus, SVC

Cardiac looping

  • During week 4, the heart tube undergoes a rightward (dextral) D-loop. This brings the future ventricles caudal and the future atria cephalad.
  • When looping reverses (L-loop), the result is dextrocardia. If all viscera are also mirrored, the picture is situs inversus.
  • Kartagener syndrome (primary ciliary dyskinesia) is the classic association: situs inversus, recurrent sinusitis, bronchiectasis, infertility.

Sinus venosus and pulmonary vein incorporation

  • The right horn of the sinus venosus is absorbed into the wall of the right atrium and becomes its smooth posterior portion.
  • The left horn regresses, leaving behind the coronary sinus.
  • A single common pulmonary vein is absorbed into the back of the left atrium and splits into four pulmonary venous orifices. Failure of this step explains total anomalous pulmonary venous return.

Endocardial cushions and neural crest preview

  • The endocardial cushions are mesenchymal swellings that form the AV septum, contribute to ventricular septation (membranous part), and shape the AV valves.
  • Neural crest cells migrate from the dorsal neural tube into the outflow tract. They will build the aorticopulmonary septum on Day 2.
  • DiGeorge syndrome (22q11.2 deletion) is the clinical anchor for neural crest failure and conotruncal defects.

Critical timeline

  • Day 22: heart begins to beat.
  • Week 8: four-chambered heart is essentially complete.
  • Teratogen-sensitive window for cardiac malformations: roughly weeks 3 to 8 of gestation.

2. Septation (Day 2)

Atrial septum

  • The septum primum grows down from the roof of the common atrium toward the endocardial cushions. The remaining gap is the ostium primum.
  • Before the ostium primum closes, perforations appear in the upper septum primum and coalesce into the ostium secundum, preserving a right-to-left passage.
  • The septum secundum then grows down to the right of the septum primum, leaving an oval opening (the foramen ovale).
  • The overlapping septa create a one-way valve: oxygenated blood from the IVC pushes the septum primum aside and crosses into the left atrium during fetal life.

ASD subtypes

  • Ostium secundum ASD (most common): excessive septum primum resorption or deficient septum secundum.
  • Ostium primum ASD: endocardial cushion failure, often part of an AV canal defect and associated with Down syndrome.
  • Sinus venosus ASD: failure of incorporation near the SVC inlet; often paired with anomalous pulmonary venous return.
  • Patent foramen ovale: failure of fusion after birth; present in ~25% of adults; usually silent unless paradoxical embolus occurs.
  • All ASDs initially create a left-to-right shunt and the classic fixed split S2.

Ventricular septum

  • The muscular interventricular septum grows up from the apex.
  • The membranous interventricular septum forms last, when the aorticopulmonary septum and endocardial cushions descend and fuse with the muscular IVS.
  • Membranous VSD is by far the most common congenital VSD because that final fusion step is the most error-prone.
  • Muscular VSDs are smaller, often multiple, and frequently close on their own as the myocardium grows.

Conotruncal (aorticopulmonary) septum

  • Neural crest cells migrate into the truncus arteriosus and bulbus cordis and form a spiral septum.
  • This single septum simultaneously divides the truncus into aorta and pulmonary trunk and splits the bulbus cordis into the LV and RV outflow tracts.
  • Because it spirals, the aorta and pulmonary trunk twist around each other in the normal mature heart.

3. Fetal Circulation and Gross Anatomy (Day 3)

Fetal blood flow path

  • Oxygenated blood leaves the placenta through the single umbilical vein.
  • Deoxygenated blood returns to the placenta through two umbilical arteries (branches of the internal iliac arteries).
  • Three shunts redirect blood away from organs that are not yet functional.

Three fetal shunts

ShuntFunctionAdult remnant
Ductus venosusBypasses fetal liver (umbilical vein to IVC)Ligamentum venosum
Foramen ovaleShunts oxygenated blood right atrium to left atriumFossa ovalis
Ductus arteriosusShunts blood pulmonary artery to descending aorta past high-resistance fetal lungsLigamentum arteriosum

Postnatal transition

  • First breath fills the alveoli and drops pulmonary vascular resistance.
  • Cord clamping eliminates the low-resistance placental bed and raises systemic vascular resistance.
  • Left atrial pressure rises above right atrial pressure, pushing the septum primum against the septum secundum and functionally closing the foramen ovale.
  • Rising arterial oxygen and falling circulating prostaglandins close the ductus arteriosus.

Pharmacology of the ductus

  • Indomethacin or ibuprofen close a patent ductus by blocking prostaglandin synthesis.
  • Prostaglandin E1 (alprostadil) keeps the ductus patent in newborns with ductal-dependent lesions (transposition, critical coarctation, hypoplastic left heart) until surgical correction.

Gross cardiac anatomy

  • Right atrium forms the right heart border.
  • Right ventricle forms most of the anterior surface (closest to the sternum).
  • Left ventricle forms the apex and the left heart border.
  • Left atrium sits most posteriorly. Enlargement of the LA compresses the esophagus (seen as posterior displacement on barium swallow) and can cause dysphagia.

Coronary anatomy

  • Left main coronary artery branches into the left anterior descending (LAD) and the left circumflex (LCx).
    • LAD runs in the anterior interventricular groove. It supplies the anterior LV and the anterior two-thirds of the interventricular septum.
    • LCx runs in the left AV groove. It supplies the lateral LV.
  • Right coronary artery runs in the right AV groove. It supplies the right atrium, most of the RV, the SA node (in ~60% of people) and the AV node, and gives off the posterior descending artery (PDA) in right-dominant circulation (~85% of people).
  • The PDA supplies the inferior wall of the LV, the posterior third of the septum, and the posteromedial papillary muscle. That single-source supply is why posteromedial papillary muscle rupture after inferior MI is the classic post-MI mitral regurgitation lesion.
  • Coronary perfusion occurs predominantly during diastole because LV systolic pressure compresses intramural vessels. Tachycardia shortens diastole and worsens subendocardial ischemia.

Great vessel anatomy

  • Aortic arch branches in order: brachiocephalic trunk (which splits into right subclavian and right common carotid), then left common carotid, then left subclavian.
  • The ligamentum arteriosum tethers the aortic arch at the isthmus, just distal to the left subclavian. This is the classic location for traumatic aortic injury after rapid deceleration and the site of coarctation.
  • The pulmonary trunk bifurcates beneath the arch; the left main bronchus passes under the arch on its way to the lung.

Pericardium

  • Visceral and parietal layers; pericardial cavity normally holds 15-50 mL of serous fluid.
  • Rapid accumulation of even small volumes causes tamponade because the pericardium has not had time to stretch; chronic effusions can accumulate liters with minimal hemodynamic effect.
  • The phrenic nerve (C3-C5) travels over the fibrous pericardium, which is why pericardial pain refers to the shoulder.

Quick Recall (Anatomy & Embryology Block)

  • Heart beats at day 22. Four chambers by week 8.
  • Five primitive bulges: truncus, bulbus, ventricle, atrium, sinus venosus.
  • Atrial septation: two septa, two ostia, one foramen ovale.
  • Ventricular septation: muscular grows up; membranous closes last (most common VSD).
  • Conotruncal failures (neural crest, 22q11.2): truncus, transposition, tetralogy.
  • Three fetal shunts: ductus venosus, foramen ovale, ductus arteriosus.
  • PGE1 opens, indomethacin closes the ductus arteriosus.
  • Coronary dominance is defined by who gives the PDA. Right-dominant in ~85%.
  • Posteromedial papillary muscle has a single PDA supply → classic post-MI rupture.
  • Ligamentum arteriosum = coarctation site and traumatic aortic injury site.
