### Embryology — heart tube, looping, septation

**1.** Which is the first organ to become functional in the human embryo, and at what week does it start beating?
> The **heart**. It begins beating around **week 4**.

**2.** What is the embryologic consequence of an **L-loop** instead of the normal D-loop of the heart tube?
> **Dextrocardia** — the heart sits on the right side.

**3.** Which septum is the **last** to close during atrial septation, and what defect results from its failure?
> The **foramen ovale closes last** (functional closure at birth; anatomic fusion over weeks). Failure = **patent foramen ovale (PFO)**.

**4.** A patient with a cryptogenic ischemic stroke gets a transthoracic echo with agitated saline. Bubbles appear in the left heart. What's the most likely embryologic mechanism?
> **Failure of fusion between septum primum and septum secundum** → patent foramen ovale.

**5.** Which interventricular septum portion closes **last**, and which septum is the most common site of VSD?
> The **membranous** IVS closes last; most VSDs occur there.

**6.** Which embryologic structure, when it fails to fuse with the septum primum, produces a **primum-type ASD**, and what congenital syndrome is classically associated?
> The **endocardial cushions**. Classic in **Down syndrome**.

**7.** Which embryologic process produces **transposition of the great arteries (TGA)**?
> **Failed spiraling of the aorticopulmonary septum**. Result: aorta arises anteriorly from RV, pulmonary trunk arises posteriorly from LV.

**8.** Which embryologic process produces **persistent truncus arteriosus**?
> **Failure of the aorticopulmonary septum to form/divide** — one single outflow vessel persists.

**9.** Which embryologic process produces **tetralogy of Fallot**?
> **Anterosuperior deviation of the infundibular (conotruncal) septum** due to abnormal **neural crest cell** migration. Yields VSD + overriding aorta + RV outflow obstruction + RV hypertrophy.

**10.** Why does DiGeorge syndrome (22q11.2 deletion) cause conotruncal cardiac defects?
> Outflow tract septation requires **neural crest cells**, which are abnormal in 22q11.2 deletion. Also explains thymic aplasia and parathyroid hypoplasia.

### Pharyngeal arch derivatives

**11.** Which adult artery is derived from the **3rd** pharyngeal arch?
> **Common carotid artery + proximal internal carotid artery**.

**12.** What is the **ductus arteriosus** an embryologic derivative of?
> The **6th (left) pharyngeal arch artery**.

**13.** Which cranial nerve is paired with the 6th pharyngeal arch?
> **CN X (vagus)** — specifically, the **recurrent laryngeal nerve** branch.

**14.** Why does the **left recurrent laryngeal nerve** hook under the aortic arch?
> It loops around the **6th arch derivative**, which on the left becomes the ligamentum arteriosum, tethered to the aortic arch.

**15.** Which arch gives rise to the proximal **right subclavian artery**?
> The **4th** pharyngeal arch (right side).

### Fetal circulation and the neonatal flip

**16.** Which fetal vessel carries the **highest** oxygen saturation?
> The **umbilical vein** (~80% SpO₂).

**17.** Which large fetal vessel in the body carries the **highest** SpO₂ once mixed with deoxygenated systemic return?
> The **IVC** (after the ductus venosus dumps oxygenated UV blood into it; ~67% SpO₂).

**18.** Name the three in utero shunts and their postnatal remnants.
> **Ductus venosus** → ligamentum venosum; **foramen ovale** → fossa ovalis; **ductus arteriosus** → ligamentum arteriosum.

**19.** What two physiologic changes close the ductus arteriosus at birth?
> **↑ oxygen tension** and **↓ prostaglandin E₂** (loss of placental PGE source).

**20.** Drug that **keeps a PDA open**? Drug that **closes** one?
> Keep open: **prostaglandin E1 (alprostadil)**. Close: **indomethacin** (prostaglandin synthesis inhibitor).

**21.** Three hemodynamic changes at birth?
> **↓ pulmonary vascular resistance** (lungs inflate, ↑PaO₂), **↑ systemic vascular resistance** (placenta removed), **↑ LV contribution** to systemic flow.

### Chambers and surface anatomy

**22.** Which is the **most anterior** cardiac chamber, and what is the clinical relevance?
> **Right ventricle**. Penetrating chest wounds at the left sternal border typically injure the RV first → tamponade.

**23.** Which is the **most posterior** cardiac chamber, and what symptoms can occur when it enlarges?
> **Left atrium**. Compresses the esophagus (dysphagia) and the left recurrent laryngeal nerve (hoarseness).

**24.** On a PA chest X-ray, which chamber forms the **right border** of the cardiac silhouette?
> The **right atrium**.

**25.** A right middle lobe pneumonia obliterates which cardiac border on chest X-ray, and what is this phenomenon called?
> The **right atrial (right heart) border** — the **silhouette sign**.

**26.** A stab wound at the **left 5th ICS at the midclavicular line** most likely injures which structure first?
> The **left lung** (cardiac apex is here, but lung tissue overlies it).

**27.** In which atrial structure does ~90% of thrombi form in atrial fibrillation, and why?
> The **left atrial appendage** — trabeculated pectinate muscle creates stasis in the setting of poor atrial contraction.

**28.** Surface landmark for auscultating the **tricuspid valve**?
> **Left lower sternal border**, around the 4th–5th intercostal space.

**29.** IV drug user with new murmur, fever, and lung cavitations. Likely valve and organism?
> **Tricuspid endocarditis**, most commonly **Staphylococcus aureus**.

### Coronary arteries

**30.** Define **coronary dominance**.
> The artery that gives rise to the **posterior descending artery (PDA)** is the dominant one. ~75–80% right-dominant; ~5–10% left-dominant; ~10–20% codominant.

**31.** In a **left-dominant** patient, which artery supplies the **AV node**?
> The **left circumflex (LCx)** — whoever feeds the PDA feeds the AV node.

**32.** A patient has an isolated **RV infarct with a normal LV**. Which artery is occluded, and what does this imply about dominance?
> **RCA** in a **left-dominant** patient (RV is supplied by RCA regardless of dominance; in left-dominant, LCx supplies the inferior LV via PDA so it's spared).

**33.** Which papillary muscle has a **single** arterial supply, and what is the supplying vessel?
> **Posteromedial papillary muscle** — supplied solely by the **PDA**.

**34.** Acute severe mitral regurgitation + pulmonary edema 3–5 days after an inferior MI. Mechanism?
> **Posteromedial papillary muscle rupture** due to its single PDA-derived blood supply.

**35.** Where does the **coronary sinus** anatomically lie, and where does it drain?
> Runs in the **posterior atrioventricular (AV) groove**, drains into the **right atrium**.

**36.** What is the embryologic origin of the coronary sinus? What does a dilated coronary sinus suggest?
> **Left horn of the sinus venosus.** Dilated CS → suspect **persistent left superior vena cava** draining into it.

**37.** Trace the path of a **biventricular pacemaker lead** to the LV.
> Subclavian vein → SVC → right atrium → **coronary sinus ostium** → along **posterior AV groove** → into a lateral cardiac vein over the LV free wall.

**38.** ECG with ST elevation in I and aVL. Likely culprit vessel?
> **Left circumflex** (lateral wall infarction).

**39.** ECG with ST elevation in II, III, aVF. Likely culprit vessel?
> **Right coronary artery** (inferior wall).

### Conduction system

**40.** Where is the SA node located?
> In the **right atrium near its junction with the superior vena cava**, subepicardial, at the upper end of the crista terminalis.

**41.** Where is the AV node located, and what anatomic triangle defines it?
> In the **interatrial septum just above the septal leaflet of the tricuspid valve**, near the **coronary sinus ostium**. The **triangle of Koch** is bounded by the tricuspid annulus, the tendon of Todaro, and the coronary sinus ostium.

**42.** What is the speed order of cardiac electrical conduction?
> **Purkinje > Atrial > Ventricular > AV node** (Park At Ventura, AV). Slowest at the AV node is what creates the atrial kick.

**43.** Inferior MI patient develops Mobitz I AV block. Which artery is occluded?
> The **RCA** (supplies the AV node in ~90% of patients, since most are right-dominant).

### Procedures (anatomy applied)

**44.** Pulmonary capillary wedge pressure (PCWP) approximates which intracardiac pressure, and why?
> **Left atrial pressure**. The inflated balloon creates a static column of blood through the pulmonary capillaries and pulmonary veins to the LA.

**45.** In a **transseptal LA catheterization** (e.g., for AF ablation), which anatomic structure is punctured?
> The **interatrial septum at the fossa ovalis** — the thin remnant of the foramen ovale.

**46.** For CABG, the **great saphenous vein** is harvested. Name the two surface landmarks for its proximal and distal ends.
> **Distal**: anterior to the **medial malleolus**. **Proximal**: passes through the saphenous opening (fossa ovalis of the thigh) to join the femoral vein **medial to the pubic tubercle**.

### Pericardium and effusion

**47.** Classic chest X-ray finding of a large pericardial effusion?
> **Enlarged "water-bottle" globular cardiac silhouette** with clear lung fields.

**48.** Two ECG findings that together suggest large pericardial effusion?
> **Low-voltage QRS** (fluid insulates the leads) and **electrical alternans** (heart swings within the fluid).

**49.** Most common viral causes of acute pericarditis?
> **Coxsackievirus B** and **echovirus**.

**50.** Triad of cardiac tamponade?
> **Beck's triad**: hypotension, distended neck veins, muffled heart sounds. Add **pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg** on inspiration.

### Aorta and great vessels

**51.** Branches of the aortic arch in order?
> **Brachiocephalic → left common carotid → left subclavian**.

**52.** Stanford classification of aortic dissection — give the management for each.
> **Type A**: involves ascending aorta → emergency surgery. **Type B**: distal to L subclavian only → medical (IV beta-blocker, then nitroprusside).

**53.** Where does a **type B aortic dissection** most commonly originate?
> Near the origin of the **left subclavian artery**, where shear stress is highest.

**54.** Where does a **blunt traumatic aortic injury** most commonly occur, and why?
> At the **aortic isthmus** (just distal to L subclavian), tethered by the **ligamentum arteriosum** — the fixed point against which the mobile descending aorta shears.

**55.** Trace the path of an atheroembolus from the carotid that produces **monocular vision loss + cherry-red spot**.
> **Internal carotid artery → ophthalmic artery → central retinal artery**.

**56.** **Subclavian steal** — describe the flow.
> Proximal subclavian stenosis → arm activity → **retrograde flow down the ipsilateral vertebral artery** to perfuse the arm; brain "loses" flow. Symptoms: vertebrobasilar ischemia with arm use, >15 mmHg interarm SBP difference.

### Abdominal/pelvic vascular anatomy

**57.** At what vertebral level is the IVC formed, and from which veins?
> At **L4–L5**, by the union of the **right and left common iliac veins**.

**58.** Why is a **left-sided varicocele** common but a *right-sided* varicocele in an adult alarming?
> Left gonadal vein drains into the **left renal vein**, which can be compressed (nutcracker). Right gonadal drains directly into the IVC, so a right varicocele suggests **IVC obstruction** — classically **renal cell carcinoma** with tumor thrombus.

**59.** Describe **nutcracker syndrome**.
> Compression of the **left renal vein** between the **aorta (posterior)** and the **superior mesenteric artery (anterior)** → ↑ pressure in left renal and left gonadal veins → left flank pain + hematuria ± left varicocele.

**60.** Femoral triangle borders and the NAVEL acronym?
> Borders: inguinal ligament (sup), sartorius (lat), adductor longus (med). **N-A-V-E-L** lateral → medial: Nerve, Artery, Vein, Empty space (femoral canal), Lymphatics. **Vein lies immediately medial to the artery**.

**61.** Why is a **femoral artery puncture above the inguinal ligament** dangerous?
> Can lacerate the **external iliac artery** or **inferior epigastric artery** → bleeding into the **retroperitoneal space**, which is not compressible by direct pressure. Post-cath hypotension without external bleed.

**62.** The **inferior epigastric artery** branches off which vessel and runs in which direction?
> Off the **external iliac artery**, just proximal to the inguinal ligament. Runs **superomedially** up the anterior abdominal wall.

### Upper-body venous anatomy

**63.** What two veins unite to form the brachiocephalic vein on each side?
> The **subclavian vein** and the **internal jugular vein**.

**64.** A patient has unilateral right face and arm swelling but a normal left side. Which vessel is obstructed, and what's the classic cause?
> **Right brachiocephalic (innominate) vein** — classically an **apical lung tumor** (Pancoast) or thrombus from a central venous catheter.

**65.** Embryologic origin of the SVC?
> The **common cardinal veins**.
