Atherogenesis cellular cast 9 cards
1First step in atherogenesis?
Endothelial cell injury / dysfunction. Triggered by chronic hemodynamic stress (HTN), dyslipidemia, smoking, hyperglycemia. Causes ↑ vascular permeability + enhanced leukocyte adhesion (selectins, ICAM, VCAM upregulated).
2Predominant cell type in a fatty streak?
Macrophage foam cells. Monocytes adhere to dysfunctional endothelium, migrate into intima, differentiate into macrophages, and take up oxidized LDL via scavenger receptors (SR-A, CD36) — which are NOT downregulated by intracellular cholesterol → unrestricted lipid uptake → foam cells.
3At what age do fatty streaks first appear in the aorta?
Approximately age 10–20 (second decade of life). Atherosclerosis is a lifelong process beginning in childhood. Fatty streaks are reversible — they can regress with risk factor modification.
4Cell source of growth factors that drive smooth muscle migration into intima?
Platelets, which release platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF) after adhering to dysfunctional endothelium. PDGF drives SMC migration from media to intima where they proliferate and synthesize collagen.
5Cell type that builds the fibrous cap of a mature atheroma?
Smooth muscle cells. They migrate from media to intima under PDGF stimulation, proliferate, and lay down collagen → form the fibrous cap over the lipid/necrotic core.
6Two-component anatomy of a mature atheromatous plaque?
Lipid / necrotic core — built by foam cells (macrophages). Fibrous cap — built by smooth muscle cells. Plaque stability depends on the cap (SMC + collagen) being thicker and the core being smaller; vulnerability is the opposite.
7Enzyme released by plaque macrophages that ruptures the fibrous cap?
Matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) — collagenases, gelatinases, stromelysins. They degrade the collagen of the fibrous cap → cap thins → ruptures under shear stress → underlying lipid core + tissue factor exposed → thrombosis → ACS.
8Patient with near-total LAD occlusion but no necrosis on echo. Why is the LV intact?
Slow plaque growth allowed collateral circulation to develop. Chronic gradual stenosis is a stimulus for arteriogenesis — collateral vessels bypass the obstruction → maintain resting perfusion. Patient gets angina with exertion (collaterals can't keep up with increased demand) but no infarct at rest.
9Five vulnerable plaque features that predict rupture?
(1) Thin fibrous cap; (2) Large lipid/necrotic core; (3) Dense macrophage infiltrate with active inflammation; (4) Spotty calcification (vs stabilizing extensive calcification); (5) Rapid growth. Vulnerability — NOT degree of stenosis — drives rupture risk; many fatal STEMIs come from non-flow-limiting plaques.
Stable angina + stress testing 5 cards
10Pathologic definition of stable angina?
Fixed atherosclerotic stenosis ≥ 70% of coronary lumen, with NO thrombus. Reproducible exertional chest pain relieved by rest or sublingual nitroglycerin. ECG normal at rest; ST depression with stress.
11Why is the 70% stenosis threshold clinically significant?
Below 70% lumen obstruction, distal arteriolar vasodilation and collaterals can compensate at rest and even with moderate exertion. At ≥ 70%, the stenosis becomes flow-limiting during exertion → demand-supply mismatch → ischemia and angina with usual activity.
12Pharmacologic stress testing options when a patient can't exercise?
Dobutamine stress echo — β1 agonist mimics exercise (↑ HR + contractility + O₂ demand). Adenosine / dipyridamole / regadenoson nuclear — coronary vasodilators cause "coronary steal" (dilate healthy vessels; stenosed segments can't dilate further → flow diverts away → perfusion defect on nuclear imaging in the ischemic zone).
13Apical wall motion abnormality on stress echo localizes to which coronary territory?
LAD. Same localization as STEMI ECG: apex → LAD; inferior → RCA; lateral → LCx.
14Why does dobutamine produce a TRANSIENT wall motion defect in a patient with stable CAD, while plaque rupture produces a PERSISTENT defect?
Dobutamine creates a temporary demand-supply mismatch in the territory served by a fixed stenosis → ischemic but viable myocardium → contractile dysfunction that resolves once dobutamine is metabolized. Plaque rupture + thrombus produces an actual MI → necrosis → permanent loss of function (until/unless reperfused early enough that hibernating tissue recovers).
ACS spectrum 7 cards
15Distinguish UA vs NSTEMI vs STEMI by ECG + troponin.
UA: ST depression ± T inversion; troponin normal. NSTEMI: same ECG; troponin elevated. STEMI: ST elevation → Q waves; troponin elevated.
16Why does the subendocardium infarct first when a coronary artery is partially occluded?
The subendocardium is the deepest layer relative to the epicardial coronaries (worst-perfused), is perfused ONLY in diastole (LV intramural systolic pressure compresses vessels there), has the highest systolic wall stress, and the highest O₂ demand. Partial occlusion → only subendocardium dies (= NSTEMI). Complete sustained occlusion → wave of necrosis spreads to involve the full wall (= STEMI).
17STEMI ECG evolution sequence?
(1) Hyperacute peaked T waves (minutes); (2) ST elevation (minutes to hours); (3) Pathologic Q waves (hours to days — transmural necrosis marker, generally permanent); (4) T-wave inversion (days); (5) ST returns to baseline (weeks). Persistent ST elevation > 6 weeks suggests LV aneurysm.
18ECG → coronary territory: II, III, aVF?
Inferior MI; culprit usually the RCA in right-dominant hearts (~85% of patients). Often associated with AV nodal dysfunction (RCA also supplies the AV node in right-dominant hearts).
19STEMI door-to-balloon time goal?
< 90 minutes from first medical contact to PCI. If PCI cannot be performed within 120 minutes, give fibrinolytics (alteplase or tenecteplase) if no contraindications.
20Standard STEMI medical regimen at the time of presentation?
Aspirin loading dose 325 mg + P2Y12 inhibitor loading (clopidogrel, ticagrelor, or prasugrel) + anticoagulant (heparin/enoxaparin/bivalirudin) + statin (high-intensity) + β-blocker if hemodynamically appropriate. Sublingual or IV nitroglycerin if symptomatic and not contraindicated.
21What pathologic state corresponds to "ST elevation + Q waves" on ECG?
Transmural infarction from complete coronary occlusion (ruptured atherosclerotic plaque + occlusive thrombus). The Q waves represent loss of viable myocardium across the full thickness of the wall.
MI histology timeline 7 cards
22Light microscopy of myocardium 2 hours after onset of fatal MI?
Essentially normal — minimal change, possibly subtle wavy fibers at the infarct margin. Light-microscopic changes don't develop until ~4 hours post-onset. The diagnosis at 2 hours is clinical + ECG + troponin (rises ~3 h) + autopsy findings of the coronary lesion itself.
23Predominant inflammatory cell in MI tissue at day 2–3?
Neutrophils. They peak at day 2–3, then macrophages take over from day 3–7 to phagocytose necrotic debris. Granulation tissue + neovascularization develops by 10–14 days.
24Cytokine that drives post-MI scar formation?
TGF-β (transforming growth factor-beta). Released by macrophages in the proliferation phase (days to weeks post-MI). Differentiates fibroblasts to myofibroblasts, stimulates collagen synthesis (initially type III, later type I), and downregulates inflammation. Master mediator of fibrosis everywhere (heart, liver, kidney, lung).
25When is the LV wall at greatest risk of free-wall rupture?
Day 5–14 post-MI. This is when macrophages have phagocytosed the necrotic tissue but granulation tissue + collagen scar have not yet matured → the wall is structurally weakest. Especially after transmural anterior MI (LAD territory).
26Most common cause of death in the first hour after MI onset?
Ventricular fibrillation. Ischemic myocardium becomes electrically heterogeneous → reentrant circuits → VT → degenerates to VF → no cardiac output → death within minutes. ~50% of MI deaths occur out-of-hospital in this first hour. Early defibrillation (public AEDs, EMS) saves these patients.
27What ICD indication applies post-MI?
Left ventricular ejection fraction < 35% at ~40 days post-MI despite optimal medical therapy. Reduces sudden arrhythmic death risk. Patients with sustained VT/VF documented post-MI get secondary-prevention ICD.
28Persistent ST elevation more than 6 weeks after MI — what to consider?
LV aneurysm (or true ventricular aneurysm from a transmural anterior MI). Aneurysmal segment shows dyskinesis, may harbor mural thrombus, and predisposes to ventricular arrhythmias + chronic HF. Cardiac MRI / echo confirm.
RV infarction 5 cards
29Why does the RV recover after MI more readily than the LV?
The RV has (1) less muscle mass, (2) lower resting O₂ extraction = more reserve, (3) coronary perfusion throughout the cardiac cycle (vs LV which is perfused only in diastole because its high systolic pressure compresses its own vessels), (4) more developed collateral circulation, and (5) higher capacity for ischemic preconditioning.
30RV infarction cath profile: CVP / PCWP / CO / SVR?
CVP ↑, PCWP ↓, CO ↓, SVR ↑. The ↑ CVP + ↓ PCWP combination is pathognomonic for primarily right-sided failure (with clear lungs). LV is under-filled because the failing RV can't push blood through the pulmonary circuit.
31Why is nitroglycerin contraindicated in RV infarction?
RV infarction makes the RV severely preload-dependent. Nitroglycerin drops venous preload → ↓ RV filling → catastrophic ↓ in RV output → cardiogenic shock and arrest. Same logic applies to diuretics, morphine, and other preload reducers. Give IV fluids instead to maintain RV preload.
32Clinical triad of RV infarction in inferior STEMI?
Jugular venous distension + clear lungs + hypotension. The clear lungs distinguish from LV cardiogenic shock (which has pulmonary edema + crackles). Confirm with V4R lead — ST elevation in V4R confirms RV involvement.
33Definitive treatment of RV infarction?
Emergent PCI of the proximal RCA. While preparing for PCI: IV fluids for preload, inotropes (dobutamine) if persistent shock despite fluids, avoid preload-reducers, treat bradyarrhythmias (atropine, temporary pacing if needed because RCA supplies AV node).
Mechanical complications 6 cards
34Day 5 of an anterior STEMI + sudden hypotension + JVD + clear lungs + no murmur + PEA arrest. Diagnosis?
Free wall rupture → cardiac tamponade. LAD-territory transmural anterior MI between days 5–14 is the classic setting. The wall is at its weakest from macrophage clearance + immature collagen. Almost universally fatal — emergent pericardiocentesis + surgical repair attempted in some cases.
35Why does the posteromedial papillary muscle rupture more often than the anterolateral?
The posteromedial papillary muscle has single coronary supply (PDA only), whereas the anterolateral has dual supply from LAD + LCx. RCA occlusion → inferior MI → posteromedial papillary necrosis → rupture → acute MR → flash pulmonary edema + cardiogenic shock.
36Cath finding that distinguishes interventricular septum rupture from papillary muscle rupture post-MI?
Both produce a new murmur + cardiogenic shock 3–5 days post-MI. IVS rupture shows an O₂ step-up from RA to RV on right heart catheterization (oxygenated LV blood shunts through the defect into the RV). Papillary rupture does not cause an O₂ step-up; instead, large V waves in the PCWP tracing reflect acute severe MR.
3763-year-old with MI + new systolic murmur + basilar crackles. Revascularized → next morning murmur and crackles gone. What happened?
Papillary muscle dysfunction (NOT rupture). Ischemic but viable papillary muscle → hypokinesis + outward displacement → MV chordae can't coapt → transient MR. Restoring blood flow restores papillary function → MR resolves. Contrast with papillary rupture — necrotic, physically torn, irreversible, requires surgery.
382-week-post-MI patient with new chest pain + fever + pericardial rub. Diagnosis?
Dressler syndrome — autoimmune pericarditis 2–10 weeks post-MI, presumed antibody-mediated response to exposed pericardial antigens. Pericardial friction rub, low-grade fever, pleurisy, ± effusion. Treat with NSAIDs ± colchicine; steroids if refractory.
391–3 days post-transmural anterior MI with new pericardial friction rub. Diagnosis?
Fibrinous pericarditis ("post-infarction pericarditis"). Inflammation from underlying transmural necrosis irritates the visceral pericardium → friction rub. Self-limited; treat with high-dose aspirin (avoid other NSAIDs and steroids early post-MI — they impair scar formation).
Ischemic injury timeline 6 cards
40How quickly does contraction stop after coronary occlusion?
Within ~60 seconds. Aerobic glycolysis stops, anaerobic begins, lactate accumulates, and ATP at high-demand sites (Na/K ATPase, SERCA, contractile machinery) depletes rapidly → contraction ceases. This is the earliest observable consequence of ischemia, well before any visible histologic change.
41Why do ischemic cardiomyocytes swell?
Loss of ATP → Na/K ATPase fails → ↑ intracellular Na⁺ + ↓ intracellular K⁺. SERCA + PMCA also fail → ↑ cytosolic Ca²⁺. Increased intracellular solute → osmotic water influx → cell + organelle swelling. Mitochondrial Ca²⁺ overload eventually triggers mitochondrial permeability transition → cell death (irreversible threshold).
42EM ultrastructural marker of IRREVERSIBLE ischemic injury?
Mitochondrial vacuolization with flocculent (amorphous) phospholipid-Ca²⁺-protein densities. Once mitochondrial permeability transition occurs, the pore opens in the inner mitochondrial membrane and the cell cannot generate ATP again. Cell swelling, ER swelling, myofibril relaxation, polysome disaggregation, triglyceride droplet accumulation = REVERSIBLE features.
43How does ischemia-reperfusion injury cause cell death?
When blood flow is restored, marginally viable cells die through (1) oxygen free radical generation (O₂ rushes back; xanthine oxidase + neutrophils produce ROS), (2) mitochondrial permeability transition, (3) inflammation + neutrophil infiltration, (4) complement activation. CK leaks via cell membrane damage (loss of sarcolemmal integrity).
44What does the post-PVC beat have more of than a normal beat?
Greater end-diastolic volume from the compensatory pause (longer filling time) → greater stroke volume by Frank-Starling. The patient feels the post-PVC beat as a palpitation (the "thump") — not the PVC itself.
45Why does troponin start rising at ~3 hours when light microscopy shows no change yet?
Troponin is a sensitive marker of even microscopic cell membrane damage and intracellular protein leak. By 3 hours post-occlusion, some myocytes are undergoing irreversible injury and leaking troponin into blood — even though gross light microscopy of these isolated cells won't show changes until ~4 hours. Troponin peaks at ~24 hours and remains elevated for days.
Stunned · hibernating · scarred 4 cards
46Define stunned myocardium.
Post-ischemic contractile dysfunction following an ACUTE brief ischemic insult (5–20 min) with subsequent reperfusion. Caused by oxidative stress + Ca²⁺ dyshomeostasis. Spontaneously reversible over hours to days. Seen after brief coronary occlusions (e.g., transient vasospasm, post-PCI).
47Define hibernating myocardium.
Adaptive ↓ contractility + ↓ metabolism in response to CHRONIC underperfusion from severe coronary stenosis. Cells are alive but dormant. Reversible with revascularization (PCI/CABG) — function returns within days to weeks after blood flow is restored. Q12 vignette: EF 35% → CABG → EF 50% at 10 days.
48Why is viability testing important before revascularization in a patient with multivessel CAD + reduced EF?
Distinguishes hibernating myocardium (will recover with revascularization → CABG/PCI is worth the risk) from scarred myocardium (will not recover → revascularization brings risk without benefit). Modalities: PET, dobutamine stress echo (low-dose looks for recruitment of contractile reserve), cardiac MRI with delayed gadolinium (transmurality of scar).
49What does ischemic preconditioning protect against?
Brief episodes of ischemia BEFORE a prolonged ischemic insult activate protective signaling pathways (KATP channel opening, kinase activation, mitochondrial preservation) that make the myocardium more resistant to subsequent prolonged ischemia. This explains why some patients with crescendo angina prior to STEMI have smaller infarcts than expected. Distinct from stunning + hibernation — it's a protective adaptation, not a dysfunction.
Special angina syndromes 6 cards
5034-year-old woman with severe nighttime chest pain that resolves spontaneously; ST elevation during episode; angiography shows no obstructive CAD. Diagnosis?
Vasospastic (Prinzmetal) angina. Hyperreactivity of coronary smooth muscle → episodic intense focal vasoconstriction → transient transmural ischemia → ST elevation that resolves spontaneously. Demographics: young, female, minimal CAD risk factors (smoking is one risk factor that does associate). Migraine + Raynaud are commonly coexistent vasospastic disorders.
51First-line treatment of vasospastic angina, and which drug class to avoid?
Calcium channel blockers (diltiazem, amlodipine) — first-line. Long-acting nitrates. AVOID β-blockers — unopposed α activity worsens coronary vasoconstriction. Also avoid cocaine, sumatriptan, ergot derivatives.
52Mechanism of cocaine-induced chest pain?
Cocaine blocks presynaptic norepinephrine reuptake → α1 + β1 overstimulation. α1 → peripheral + coronary vasoconstriction (↓ supply). β1 → ↑ HR + contractility (↑ demand). Demand-supply mismatch → ischemia. Sympathomimetic exam findings: dilated pupils, agitation, sweating, low-grade fever (peripheral vasoconstriction impairs heat loss).
53Treatment of cocaine-induced chest pain — which drug class to avoid?
Benzodiazepines (sedation + ↓ central sympathetic outflow) + nitroglycerin (coronary vasodilation + ↓ preload). AVOID pure β-blockers — unopposed α activity worsens coronary vasoconstriction. Labetalol (α + β blocker) or phentolamine (α-blocker) are acceptable if HTN needs more aggressive control.
54Coronary territory at greatest risk during exercise stress?
Whichever territory has the most severe fixed stenosis. Generally LAD because anterior wall + apex have the highest myocardial mass and highest O₂ demand. Subendocardium is the most vulnerable layer within any territory because it perfuses only in diastole and bears the highest systolic wall stress.
55How does cigarette smoking promote atherogenesis at the molecular level?
(1) Direct endothelial toxicity from oxidant chemicals + free radicals → ↑ vascular permeability + ↓ NO bioavailability. (2) Pro-coagulant effect → ↑ fibrinogen + platelet activation. (3) Pro-inflammatory cytokines induced systemically. (4) ↓ HDL → ↓ reverse cholesterol transport. Net: smoking is the single strongest modifiable risk factor for coronary, peripheral, and abdominal aortic atherosclerosis.
Embolic + watershed 5 cards
5672-year-old with severe abdominal pain shortly after cardiac catheterization → bowel resection → histology shows needle-shaped intravascular clefts with multinucleated giant cells. Diagnosis?
Atheroembolic disease (cholesterol crystal embolism). Iatrogenic dislodgement of atherosclerotic plaque material during invasive vascular procedures → cholesterol-rich debris showers downstream → lodges in small arteries → coagulative ischemia + foreign-body giant cell reaction. The needle-shaped clefts are empty spaces left by the cholesterol dissolving during tissue processing.
57Five clinical syndromes from atheroembolism by destination?
Mesenteric — abdominal pain, bowel ischemia. Renal — AKI with eosinophilia + hypocomplementemia (lab clue). Skin — blue toe syndrome, livedo reticularis. Retinal — Hollenhorst plaques (bright yellow refractile particles on fundoscopy). Brain — TIA, embolic stroke.
58Distinguish atheroembolism from cardioembolism histologically?
Atheroembolism: needle-shaped cholesterol clefts + multinucleated giant cells (foreign body reaction); cause = invasive vascular procedure or severe atherosclerosis. Cardioembolism: larger fibrin-rich clots (no cholesterol clefts); cause = AFib LA appendage thrombus, LV mural thrombus post-MI, endocarditis vegetation, atrial myxoma.
59Patient develops bilateral hippocampal necrosis + colonic infarct at the splenic flexure after sustained hypotension. Mechanism?
Watershed ischemia. Splenic flexure is the colonic watershed (border zone between distal SMA + IMA territories) → first to infarct with sustained low MAP. Hippocampal CA1 neurons are selectively vulnerable to global ischemia because of high metabolic demand. Same physiology produces "man-in-the-barrel" syndrome (proximal arm weakness from parasagittal cortex watershed infarcts between ACA + MCA + PCA territories).
60Why does an aged patient with cachexia develop yellow-brown granules in cardiomyocytes despite no significant CAD?
Lipofuscin ("wear-and-tear" pigment) — lysosomal breakdown products of lipid peroxidation. Accumulates with age in low-turnover cells (heart, liver, CNS). Malnutrition (e.g., cachexia from esophageal cancer) accelerates formation. Distinguishes from hemosiderin (iron, golden-brown) and melanin (tyrosine oxidation product).
