Days 16 to 19 lecture summary · First Aid 2025 pp. 690 to 703 · All wording original

1 · Upper Airway FA p. 690

  • Rhinosinusitis: the maxillary sinus is involved most often. Usual organisms are nontypeable Haemophilus influenzae, Streptococcus pneumoniae, and Moraxella catarrhalis.
  • Epistaxis: anterior bleeds from the Kiesselbach plexus are the most common. Posterior bleeds from the sphenopalatine artery are less common but more dangerous.

2 · Obstructive Lung Disease FA pp. 692 to 693

  • Hallmark: airflow obstruction. The FEV1 falls more than the FVC, so the FEV1/FVC ratio drops below 70%. Air trapping raises RV, FRC, and TLC.
  • COPD has two faces. Chronic bronchitis (the "blue bloater"): a productive cough for at least 3 months a year over 2 years, an increased Reid index, with hypoxia and hypercapnia. Emphysema (the "pink puffer"): alveolar wall destruction, loss of elastic recoil, and a barrel chest. Centriacinar emphysema comes from smoking; panacinar comes from alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency.
  • Asthma: reversible bronchoconstriction triggered by allergens, viral infections, or stress. Sputum shows Curschmann spirals and Charcot-Leyden crystals. Aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease is the triad of asthma, nasal polyps, and aspirin or NSAID sensitivity.
  • Bronchiectasis: permanently dilated bronchi from chronic infection or impaired clearance. Associated with cystic fibrosis, Kartagener syndrome, and allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis.

3 · Restrictive Lung Disease FA pp. 694 to 695

  • Hallmark: reduced FVC and TLC with a normal or increased FEV1/FVC ratio.
  • Extrapulmonary causes (poor mechanics): neuromuscular weakness (myasthenia gravis, ALS, Guillain-Barre) and structural problems (scoliosis, severe obesity).
  • Interstitial (pulmonary) causes: idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (a usual interstitial pneumonia pattern with honeycomb lung), pneumoconioses, sarcoidosis, hypersensitivity pneumonitis, and drug toxicity (bleomycin, amiodarone, methotrexate).

4 · Sarcoidosis FA p. 695

  • Noncaseating granulomas, bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, an elevated ACE level, and hypercalcemia (activated macrophages make 1-alpha-hydroxylase, which raises vitamin D). More common in Black women.
  • Other findings: uveitis, erythema nodosum, lupus pernio, and Bell palsy. Often restrictive on pulmonary function testing.

5 · Mesothelioma FA p. 695

  • A malignancy of the pleura strongly tied to asbestos exposure. Psammoma bodies appear on histology. It is not caused by smoking.

6 · Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome FA p. 697

  • Causes: sepsis (most common), aspiration, trauma, pancreatitis, burns, and near-drowning.
  • Diffuse alveolar damage raises capillary permeability, producing protein-rich edema and hyaline membranes. The result is stiff lungs (low compliance), V/Q mismatch, diffuse bilateral infiltrates, and a low PaO2 to FiO2 ratio.
  • Management: low tidal volume ventilation and PEEP.

7 · Sleep Apnea FA p. 697

  • Obstructive: the airway collapses despite effort (obesity, large tonsils). Features include daytime sleepiness, hypertension, and atrial fibrillation.
  • Central: no respiratory effort (CNS injury, heart failure, opioids).
  • Both cause nocturnal hypoxia, leading to pulmonary hypertension and polycythemia.

8 · Pulmonary Hypertension FA p. 698

  • Defined by a mean pulmonary artery pressure of 20 mm Hg or more.
  • Five groups: arterial or idiopathic (BMPR2 mutation), left heart disease, chronic lung disease or hypoxia, chronic thromboembolic, and multifactorial.
  • Vessels develop medial hypertrophy and plexiform lesions, ending in right ventricular failure (cor pulmonale).

9 · Exam Findings and Pleura FA pp. 698 to 699

  • Pleural effusion: dull to percussion, with decreased breath sounds and fremitus. Transudate (heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome) versus exudate (infection, malignancy, inflammation), separated by the Light criteria. (Callback: this is the heart-failure effusion case from the CVS block.)
  • Pneumothorax: hyperresonant, with decreased breath sounds and fremitus. The spontaneous type hits tall thin young men (a ruptured bleb). Tension pneumothorax (trauma or mechanical ventilation) pushes the mediastinum away from the affected side and is an emergency needle decompression.
  • Consolidation or lobar pneumonia: dull percussion, increased fremitus, bronchophony, egophony, and late inspiratory crackles.

10 · Lung Cancer FA pp. 702 to 703

  • The leading cause of cancer death. Complications mnemonic SPHERE: SVC syndrome, Pancoast tumor, Horner syndrome, Endocrine paraneoplastic, Recurrent laryngeal (and phrenic) nerve involvement, and Effusions.

  • Pancoast tumor (lung apex, usually non-small cell) compresses local structures, causing Horner syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) and SVC syndrome (facial plethora, jugular distension).

Key rules

  1. Obstructive = low FEV1/FVC.
  2. Restrictive = low volumes, normal or high FEV1/FVC.
  3. Asbestos = lower lobes plus mesothelioma.
  4. Silica and coal = upper lobes.
  5. Small cell and squamous = central and smoking.
  6. Adeno and large = peripheral.
  7. Tension pneumothorax pushes the trachea away.
