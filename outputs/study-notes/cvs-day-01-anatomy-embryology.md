# Day 1 · Cardiovascular Anatomy & Embryology

> This sheet covers everything the Q-bank actually asks on Day 1: heart embryology, pharyngeal-arch arteries, fetal circulation, the chambers, the coronaries, the conduction system, the pericardium, the aorta and great vessels, the abdominal/pelvic vessels, and the upper-body venous tree. All wording is original; the high-yield facts themselves appear in every standard anatomy text.

## 1 · The 30-second map

The heart is the **first** organ to function (it beats by week 4). Day 1 lives on three axes:

1. **Embryology** — how a single tube becomes four chambers, and why outflow tract defects cluster together.
2. **Cardiac anatomy** — chambers, valves, coronaries, conduction, pericardium.
3. **Vascular anatomy** — aorta and branches, IVC and tributaries, upper-body veins, fetal/neonatal transition.

Almost every question on this day is a clinical wrapper around a structural fact in one of those buckets.

## 2 · Embryology — timeline

| Week | Event | Why it's tested |
|---|---|---|
| 3 | Cardiogenic mesoderm forms the **primitive heart tube** | Pericardial origin |
| 4 | Tube **loops to the right** (D-loop) | L-loop → **dextrocardia** |
| 4 | Heart starts to beat | "Earliest functional organ" trivia |
| 5–8 | **Septation** of atria, ventricles, outflow tract | Origin of 90% of congenital defects |

### Septation, sequenced

**Atrial septum.** Septum primum grows down → leaves ostium primum (closes), then ostium secundum opens. Septum secundum grows down to the right of primum → leaves the **foramen ovale**. After birth, ↑LA pressure + ↓RA pressure push the two septa together; over weeks they fuse. Failure = **patent foramen ovale** (PFO).

**AV cushions** fuse to split the common AV canal into tricuspid and mitral inlets and contribute to the lower atrial and upper ventricular septa. Failure = **endocardial cushion defect** (classic in Down syndrome).

**Ventricular septum.** Muscular portion grows up. The **membranous portion** is last to close — most common location for **VSD** (the most common congenital heart defect overall).

**Outflow tract.** Neural-crest-derived **aorticopulmonary (AP) septum** spirals down through the truncus arteriosus and bulbus cordis, splitting them into aorta + pulmonary trunk.

| Failure mode | Resulting defect |
|---|---|
| AP septum **fails to spiral** (forms straight wall) | **Transposition of the great arteries (TGA)** — aorta anterior off RV, PA posterior off LV |
| AP septum **fails to form/divide** at all | **Persistent truncus arteriosus** (single outflow vessel) |
| AP septum **malaligned anterosuperiorly** (deviation of the infundibular septum) | **Tetralogy of Fallot** (VSD, overriding aorta, RVOT obstruction, RV hypertrophy) |

> **Neural crest is the asterisk.** Outflow tract anomalies → neural-crest problem. Look for DiGeorge (22q11.2 deletion): conotruncal defects + thymic aplasia + hypocalcemia + cleft palate.

### Atrial septal defects, in plain English

- **Secundum ASD** (~75%) → arrested growth of septum **secundum**. Most common ASD.
- **Primum ASD** → failed fusion of septum **primum** with endocardial cushions. Often co-occurs with AV cushion defects (Down syndrome).
- **PFO** is *not* a true ASD — it's a probe-patent flap. Clinical relevance: **cryptogenic stroke** (paradoxical embolism). Diagnose with agitated-saline bubble study + Valsalva.

### Primitive embryologic venous systems

| System | Becomes |
|---|---|
| **Vitelline veins** | Portal system (portal vein, hepatic veins, IVC portion) |
| **Umbilical veins** | Ductus venosus → ligamentum venosum; left UV → ligamentum teres hepatis |
| **Cardinal veins** | Systemic venous return — **SVC** (from common cardinals), azygos, IVC portions |

## 3 · Pharyngeal arch arteries (and their nerves)

| Arch | Adult artery derivative | Associated cranial nerve |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | Maxillary artery (portion) | **CN V** (trigeminal) |
| 2nd | Stapedial, hyoid (largely regresses) | **CN VII** (facial) |
| 3rd | **Common carotid + proximal internal carotid** | **CN IX** (glossopharyngeal) |
| 4th | **Aortic arch (L); proximal right subclavian (R)** | **CN X** (vagus — superior laryngeal br.) |
| 5th | Regresses entirely | — |
| 6th | **Proximal pulmonary arteries; ductus arteriosus (L)** | **CN X** (vagus — recurrent laryngeal br.) |

> The recurrent laryngeal nerve hooks around the 6th arch derivative on each side. On the left, that derivative becomes the ductus arteriosus → ligamentum arteriosum, which is why the **left recurrent laryngeal nerve loops under the aortic arch** while the right hooks around the right subclavian (4th arch).

## 4 · Fetal circulation & the neonatal flip

```
Placenta ─ umbilical vein (SaO₂ ~80%) ─► ductus venosus ─► IVC ─► RA
                                                                  │
                                            ┌─────────────────────┴─────────────────────┐
                                            ▼                                           ▼
                                    foramen ovale ─► LA ─► LV ─► aorta         RV ─► pulmonary trunk
                                                                                        │
                                                                       ductus arteriosus│
                                                                                        ▼
                                                                              descending aorta
```

### Three in utero shunts

| Shunt | Purpose in utero | Postnatal remnant |
|---|---|---|
| **Ductus venosus** | Bypasses fetal liver | Ligamentum venosum |
| **Foramen ovale** | Bypasses lungs (RA → LA) | Fossa ovalis |
| **Ductus arteriosus** | Bypasses lungs (PA → aorta) | Ligamentum arteriosum |

### Fetal SpO₂ gradient (who has the highest oxygenation?)

Umbilical vein **(~80%, the highest of any fetal vessel)** → ductus venosus → **IVC (~67%, still the highest of any large vessel sampled in the body)** → RA → preferentially streamed through foramen ovale → LA → LV → ascending aorta (~65%, supplying brain/upper body). The descending aorta is lower (~58%) because RV blood through the ductus mixes in. The umbilical artery is the lowest.

### The neonatal flip — three simultaneous changes at birth

| Change | Mechanism | Result |
|---|---|---|
| ↓ Pulmonary vascular resistance | Lungs inflate → arterioles vasodilate; ↑PaO₂ relieves hypoxic constriction | Pulmonary flow rises |
| ↑ Systemic vascular resistance | Placenta detaches (low-resistance bed removed) | LA & aortic pressure rise |
| ↑ LV contribution to systemic flow | Both ventricles now pump in **series**, not parallel | Adult-pattern circulation |

**Closure signals.**
- **Foramen ovale** closes functionally as ↑LA pressure pushes septum primum against septum secundum.
- **Ductus arteriosus** closes via **↑O₂ + ↓PGE₂** (placental loss removes prostaglandin source). Functional closure in hours; anatomic closure over days.

**Pharmacology lock.**
- **Keep DA open** (e.g., TGA before surgery, ductal-dependent lesions): **prostaglandin E1**.
- **Close a PDA**: **indomethacin** (prostaglandin synthesis inhibitor).

## 5 · Cardiac chambers, surfaces, and X-ray borders

### Spatial relationships

- **Most anterior** chamber: **right ventricle**. Penetrating wounds at the left sternal border (3rd–5th ICS) most commonly injure the RV. Layers traversed in such a wound: skin → pectoralis major → external/internal intercostal muscles → internal thoracic vessels → transversus thoracis → parietal pleura → pericardium → RV myocardium.
- **Most posterior** chamber: **left atrium**. LA enlargement → dysphagia (esophageal compression), hoarseness (compresses left recurrent laryngeal n. against the aortic arch).
- **Apex**: left 5th ICS, midclavicular line. *But* the lungs overlie it: a lateral stab at the 5th ICS along the MCL usually hits the **left lung** first, not the LV. A more medial, deeper trajectory is required to reach the LV.

### PA chest X-ray borders

| Border | Structure |
|---|---|
| Right upper | SVC |
| Right lower | **Right atrium** |
| Left upper | Aortic knob |
| Left, descending | Pulmonary artery → LA appendage (concavity) → **LV** |
| Inferior anterior | RV |
| Inferior right corner | IVC entering RA |

**Silhouette sign:** lung consolidation that obliterates a cardiac border tells you which lobe is involved.
- **Right middle lobe** opacity silhouettes the **right heart border (RA)**.
- **Lingula** opacity silhouettes the **left heart border (LV)**.
- **Right lower lobe** opacity silhouettes the **right hemidiaphragm**, not the heart.

### Inner anatomy: atria

- **RA**: pectinate muscles anteriorly (from primitive atrium), smooth posteriorly (from sinus venosus). Boundary = **crista terminalis**.
- **LA**: smooth body (incorporated pulmonary veins); pectinate muscle only in the **appendage**. The LA appendage is the single most common site of thrombus formation in **atrial fibrillation** (~90% of LA clots) — stasis from poor contraction + trabeculated walls.

### Auscultation areas (surface landmarks)

| Valve | Best heard at |
|---|---|
| Aortic | Right 2nd ICS, sternal border |
| Pulmonic | Left 2nd ICS, sternal border |
| Tricuspid | Left lower sternal border (4th–5th ICS) |
| Mitral | Cardiac apex (5th ICS, MCL) |

**Tricuspid endocarditis in IV drug users** → *Staphylococcus aureus* most common; bacteria first hit the right heart from the venous side. Lung exam may show cavitary nodules (septic pulmonary emboli).

## 6 · Coronary arteries

### The three vessels

| Vessel | Territory | Common trap |
|---|---|---|
| **RCA** | Inferior wall of LV, RV, SA node (~60%), AV node (~90% of patients — *right-dominant*), PDA in ~70–80% | Inferior MI → bradycardia, AV block |
| **LAD** | Anterior wall LV, anterior 2/3 of IVS, apex | "Widow-maker"; most commonly occluded |
| **LCx** | Lateral wall LV; supplies PDA in left-dominant circulation (~5–10%) | Lateral MI → LCx |

- Coronary blood flow occurs **during diastole** (aortic valve closes → blood is forced into the sinuses of Valsalva and the coronary ostia).
- The **subendocardium** is the most vulnerable layer to ischemia.

### Coronary dominance — anchored rule

The **dominant coronary** is whichever vessel gives off the **posterior descending artery (PDA)**.

| Pattern | PDA from | AV nodal artery from | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Right dominant | RCA | RCA | ~70–80% |
| Left dominant | LCx | **LCx** | ~5–10% |
| Codominant | both | RCA | ~10–20% |

> **Anchor:** *whoever feeds the PDA feeds the AV node.* So in a left-dominant heart, the AV nodal artery comes from the LCx — and an isolated RV infarct with a normal LV occurs when the RCA is occluded in a left-dominant patient (RCA still supplies RV, but the inferior LV is spared because the LCx → PDA territory is intact).

### ECG-lead-to-coronary territory map

| Region | Leads | Vessel |
|---|---|---|
| Inferior | II, III, aVF | **RCA** (right-dominant) |
| Lateral | I, aVL, V5–V6 | **LCx** |
| Anterior | V1–V4 | **LAD** |
| Septal | V1–V2 | **LAD** (septal branches) |
| Posterior | Tall R in V1–V2 (reciprocal) | **RCA or LCx** |

### Posteromedial papillary muscle — fragile by design

The mitral valve has two papillary muscles, anterolateral and posteromedial. The **anterolateral** has dual supply (LAD + LCx). The **posteromedial** has **single supply** from the **PDA**. After an inferior MI, the posteromedial papillary muscle is the one that ruptures — sudden new severe **mitral regurgitation + pulmonary edema 3–5 days post-MI**.

### Coronary sinus and biventricular pacing

The **coronary sinus** runs in the **posterior atrioventricular (AV) groove** and empties into the **right atrium**. Embryologic origin: **left horn of the sinus venosus** (so a dilated CS on echo → suspect **persistent left SVC** draining into it).

For a **biventricular pacemaker** (cardiac resynchronization in heart failure), three leads are placed:
1. Right atrial lead → RA appendage.
2. Right ventricular lead → RV apex.
3. Left ventricular lead → subclavian vein → SVC → RA → **coronary sinus ostium** → along the **posterior AV groove** → into a lateral cardiac vein over the LV free wall.

That's why chest X-ray questions place the third lead in the AV groove.

## 7 · Conduction system

**Order of activation:** SA node → AV node → bundle of His → R/L bundle branches → Purkinje fibres → ventricular myocytes.

**Anatomic landmarks.**
- **SA node**: subepicardial in the **right atrium at the junction with the SVC**, near the upper end of the crista terminalis.
- **AV node**: in the **interatrial septum just above the septal leaflet of the tricuspid valve**, near the **coronary sinus ostium**. Together with the tendon of Todaro and the tricuspid annulus, this region defines the **triangle of Koch** — the target for AV-node ablation in AF with rapid ventricular response.
- **Bundle of His**: traverses the membranous IVS; the only normal electrical bridge between atria and ventricles.

**Speed order (fast → slow): Park At Ventura, AV.**
- **P**urkinje > **A**tria > **V**entricles > **AV** node.

The **AV nodal delay (~100 ms)** is what allows the **atrial kick** — atrial contraction finishes before ventricular systole. RCA supplies the AV node in ~90%, which is why **inferior MI → bradyarrhythmias** (Mobitz I, sinus brady).

## 8 · Cardiac procedures — anatomy in action

### Pulmonary artery (Swan-Ganz) catheterization

Path: peripheral vein → SVC → **RA → RV → pulmonary trunk → branch PA**. The balloon at the tip is inflated to wedge in a small PA branch. With forward flow occluded, the pressure measured distal to the balloon equals the static column of blood through the **pulmonary capillaries → pulmonary veins → LA**. So **PCWP ≈ LA pressure** (and ≈ LVEDP in the absence of mitral disease).

### Transseptal LA catheterization (e.g., AF ablation)

Venous access (typically right femoral vein) → IVC → **RA → puncture the interatrial septum at the fossa ovalis** (thin membrane = closed foramen ovale) → LA. Allows direct LA pressure measurement and access to the pulmonary vein ostia (the usual ectopic AF foci).

### CABG conduits

| Conduit | Anatomic landmarks |
|---|---|
| **Left internal mammary (thoracic) artery (LIMA)** | Branch of subclavian; preferred for LAD because it stays patent decades |
| **Great saphenous vein** | Longest superficial vein in the body. Proximal end: passes through the **saphenous opening (fossa ovalis of the thigh)** and joins the **femoral vein medial to the pubic tubercle**. Distal end: **anterior to the medial malleolus**. |
| **Radial artery** | Used selectively |

## 9 · Pericardium & effusion

The pericardium is a fibroserous sac with two layers (parietal and visceral) separated by a small amount of serous fluid (~15–50 mL normally). **Pericardial effusion → cardiac tamponade** when accumulation outpaces stretch.

**Causes (high-yield):**
- **Viral pericarditis** (coxsackievirus B, echovirus) — preceded by URI.
- Uremia, post-MI (Dressler), TB, malignancy, rheumatologic disease.

**Classic findings:**
- Chest X-ray: **enlarged, globular "water-bottle" cardiac silhouette** with clear lung fields.
- ECG: **low-voltage QRS** (effusion insulates the leads) + **electrical alternans** (heart swinging in fluid).
- Physical: **Beck's triad** of tamponade — hypotension, distended neck veins, muffled heart sounds. **Pulsus paradoxus** (>10 mmHg drop in systolic BP with inspiration).

## 10 · Aorta in the adult — branches, dissection, trauma

### Arch branches in order

1. **Brachiocephalic** (→ right common carotid + right subclavian)
2. **Left common carotid**
3. **Left subclavian** — the landmark on every dissection question.

### Abdominal aortic branches (cranial → caudal)

- **Celiac** (T12)
- **Superior mesenteric (SMA)** (L1)
- **Renal arteries** (L1–L2)
- **Gonadal** (L2)
- **Inferior mesenteric (IMA)** (L3)
- **Common iliacs** (L4 bifurcation)

### Stanford classification — dissection

| Stanford | Where the plane lies | Management |
|---|---|---|
| **Type A** | **Involves the ascending aorta** (any extension) | Surgical emergency — replace ascending aorta |
| **Type B** | Distal to the **left subclavian artery** only | Medical first — IV beta-blocker, then nitroprusside |

> **Q-bank pivot.** Sudden severe midback/interscapular pain + hypertension + smoker, TEE shows flap distal to L subclavian → **Stanford type B**. The intimal tear most often starts **near the origin of the L subclavian**, where shear stress is highest. Type A tears typically begin at the **sinotubular junction** of the ascending aorta.

Risk factors in the stems: **hypertension** (#1), Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos, bicuspid aortic valve, cocaine, third-trimester pregnancy.

### Blunt traumatic aortic injury (BAI)

Mechanism: rapid deceleration (MVC, fall from height). The aorta tears at the **isthmus** — the transition between the relatively mobile descending aorta and the arch tethered by the **ligamentum arteriosum** (the postnatal remnant of the ductus, attached just distal to the L subclavian).

Clues on the chest X-ray: **widened mediastinum**, depressed left mainstem bronchus, blurred aortic knob. Examination: **pulse discrepancy** between upper and lower extremities (pseudocoarctation), interscapular murmur.

### Atheroemboli — the carotid → eye cascade

Internal carotid artery atherosclerosis can throw an embolus that travels: **ICA → ophthalmic artery → central retinal artery**. Result: sudden painless monocular vision loss with a **cherry-red spot** at the macula on fundoscopy. Ipsilateral **carotid bruit** is the smoking gun.

### Subclavian steal syndrome

Proximal subclavian artery stenosis (most often left, atherosclerosis #1 cause) → arm activity creates a pressure sink → **retrograde flow down the ipsilateral vertebral artery** to perfuse the arm. The brain "loses" that flow during exertion.

Stem: vertebrobasilar symptoms (dizziness, drop attacks, vertigo) **with arm use** + **>15 mmHg systolic BP difference between arms**.

### TEE landmarks (cardiac vs aortic windows)

The esophagus sits **directly behind the LA** in the midthorax. In the midesophagus:
- **Anterior view** → **left atrium** is closest to the probe (LA is the most posterior chamber).
- **Posterior rotation** → the probe now images the **descending thoracic aorta**, which runs anterior to the vertebral column behind the esophagus.

## 11 · Abdominal & pelvic vascular anatomy

### Inferior vena cava (IVC)

- **Formed** by the union of the **right and left common iliac veins** at **L4–L5**, just below the aortic bifurcation.
- Lies to the **right of the aorta**, **anterior to the vertebral bodies**, **medial to the descending part of the duodenum**.
- Passes through the diaphragm at the **caval hiatus (T8)** → empties into the right atrium.
- Drains lower extremities, pelvic and abdominal viscera, and the portal system via the hepatic veins.

**IVC filters** are placed for DVT when anticoagulation is contraindicated; positioned in the infrarenal IVC, typically around L2–L3.

### Renal veins — the asymmetry that wins exam points

| Right renal vein | Left renal vein |
|---|---|
| Short, drains straight into IVC | **Long.** Crosses the midline **anterior to the aorta** and **posterior to the SMA** before reaching IVC |
| Receives only the right renal | **Also receives left gonadal, left adrenal, and a left phrenic branch** |

### Gonadal vein drainage — the most asked vein in CVS anatomy

- **Right gonadal → IVC** directly.
- **Left gonadal → left renal vein → IVC**.

### Nutcracker syndrome

The left renal vein gets compressed between the **aorta (posterior)** and the **SMA (anterior)** — the nutcracker. Back-pressure raises pressure in the left renal vein and the left gonadal vein draining into it.

| Clue in the stem | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Left flank pain + hematuria | Renal vein entrapment |
| Left-sided **varicocele** | Backpressure into the left pampiniform plexus via the left gonadal vein |

> **Right-sided varicocele in an adult = rule out renal cell carcinoma** with tumor thrombus invading the IVC (the right gonadal vein drains directly into the IVC at an angle, so right-sided varicocele points to IVC obstruction).

### Femoral triangle & cardiac catheterization access

Borders:
- **Superior**: inguinal ligament
- **Lateral**: sartorius
- **Medial**: adductor longus

Contents (lateral → medial): **NAVEL** — femoral **N**erve, **A**rtery, **V**ein, **E**mpty space (femoral canal), **L**ymphatics.

Practical points:
- **Femoral vein** is **immediately medial to the femoral artery** — that's the cannulation target for central venous access if subclavian/IJ are unavailable.
- The **inferior epigastric artery** branches off the external iliac just proximal to the inguinal ligament and runs **superomedially** up the anterior abdominal wall.
- A femoral artery puncture **above the inguinal ligament** can lacerate the inferior epigastric vessels or the external iliac and bleed into the **retroperitoneal space** (psoas/ileal fascia) — hypotension with no obvious external hemorrhage after cardiac cath. Puncture **below** the ligament limits bleeding to a compressible space.

## 12 · Upper-body venous anatomy

### Embryologic origin

The **SVC** is derived from the **common cardinal veins** (which drained into the sinus venosus of the embryonic heart). Cardinal → systemic venous return.

### Adult anatomy (right side mirrors left)

Subclavian vein **+** internal jugular vein → **brachiocephalic vein**.
Right brachiocephalic **+** left brachiocephalic → **superior vena cava** → right atrium.
External jugular vein drains the scalp and face; it joins the subclavian.

### Obstruction patterns — laterality tells you the lesion

| Pattern | Lesion |
|---|---|
| **Unilateral** face + arm swelling | **Brachiocephalic (innominate) vein** obstruction on that side (classic: apical lung tumor, indwelling central line thrombus) |
| **Bilateral** face + arm + neck swelling | **SVC obstruction (SVC syndrome)** — lung cancer, lymphoma, fibrosing mediastinitis |

On axial chest CT, the SVC is identified to the right of the heart, posterolateral to the ascending aorta, anterior to the right PA, just below the carina.

## 13 · Top 15 question-bank patterns to drill

1. **VSD in the membranous IVS.** Most common congenital heart defect. Many close spontaneously.
2. **PFO + cryptogenic stroke + bubble study.** Failed septum primum/secundum fusion.
3. **Tetralogy of Fallot.** Anterosuperior deviation of the infundibular septum (neural crest). Cyanotic; squatting relieves spells.
4. **Transposition of great arteries.** Failed spiraling of the AP septum. Keep duct open with **PGE1**.
5. **PDA = 6th aortic arch derivative (left).** Continuous "machinery" murmur. Close with **indomethacin**.
6. **Coronary sinus runs in the posterior AV groove.** Biventricular pacing lead path. Dilated CS = persistent left SVC.
7. **Left-dominant circulation → LCx supplies the AV node.** Right-dominant → RCA. Whoever feeds the PDA feeds the AV node.
8. **Posteromedial papillary muscle rupture 3–5 days post-MI.** Single PDA supply.
9. **Stanford type B aortic dissection** = distal to the **left subclavian artery**. Type A = ascending aorta. Management diverges sharply.
10. **Blunt aortic injury at the isthmus** — tethered by the ligamentum arteriosum.
11. **IVC formed at L4–L5 by union of common iliac veins.** Anterior to vertebral body, right of aorta.
12. **Left gonadal → left renal → IVC.** Nutcracker = left flank pain + hematuria ± left varicocele.
13. **Right-sided varicocele in an adult → renal cell carcinoma** invading IVC.
14. **Subclavian steal** = retrograde flow in vertebral artery + >15 mmHg interarm BP difference.
15. **LA appendage** = thrombus source in AF (pectinate stasis); transseptal puncture at the **fossa ovalis** for ablation.

## 14 · 90-second self-test

1. The most common congenital heart defect arises from failure of which structure to fuse?
2. Where in the heart will a thrombus most likely form in chronic atrial fibrillation, and why?
3. A patient has left-dominant coronary circulation. Which artery supplies the AV node?
4. Why is the posteromedial papillary muscle the one that ruptures after an inferior MI?
5. Where does the lead of a biventricular pacemaker lie when threaded through the coronary sinus?
6. Stanford type B aortic dissection most commonly originates near the origin of which artery?
7. At what vertebral level is the IVC formed, and from which two veins?
8. Which gonadal vein drains directly into the IVC? Why is a left varicocele common but a *right* varicocele alarming?
9. Why does an apical lung tumor cause swelling of only the right face and arm rather than both sides?
10. In subclavian steal, which artery has reversed (retrograde) flow?

*Answers (cover and uncover):*
1. **Membranous interventricular septum** → VSD.
2. **LA appendage** — pectinate muscle stasis in the setting of poor atrial contraction.
3. **Left circumflex** (in left-dominant circulation, LCx gives off the PDA → AV nodal artery).
4. **Single arterial supply** from the PDA, vs. dual supply (LAD + LCx) of the anterolateral papillary muscle.
5. In the **posterior atrioventricular groove**, then forward along a lateral cardiac vein over the LV free wall.
6. **Left subclavian artery** (isthmus / just distal to it).
7. **L4–L5**, by the union of the **right and left common iliac veins**.
8. **Right** gonadal vein → IVC directly. Left drains into the renal vein, which gets compressed (nutcracker) → routine left varicocele. A *new right* varicocele bypasses the usual anatomy → **IVC obstruction**, classically **renal cell carcinoma** with tumor thrombus.
9. The right brachiocephalic vein is obstructed (apical tumor compresses it). The left side drains via its own intact brachiocephalic and SVC route, so it doesn't swell.
10. **Ipsilateral vertebral artery** (steals from the brainstem to feed the arm).
