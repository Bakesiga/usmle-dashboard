Question 1

Correct answer: E

Vignette in brief: An 8-month-old previously healthy infant is brought to the urgent care clinic with three days of vomiting and watery diarrhea.

Correct answer: E. Hematocrit increased, serum albumin increased, serum uric acid increased

Acute gastroenteritis in an infant produces rapid loss of fluid through vomiting and diarrhea, depleting intravascular and extracellular volume. Three integrated lab findings follow. First, hemoconcentration: as plasma volume falls but red cell mass stays the same, the proportion of blood occupied by cells (hematocrit) rises. Second, serum proteins including albumin become concentrated in the smaller plasma volume, raising measured albumin levels even though no new protein has been made. Third, volume depletion reduces renal perfusion and lowers glomerular filtration rate. Since uric acid is normally cleared by glomerular filtration and proximal tubular handling that is sensitive to volume status (in volume contraction, more sodium and uric acid get reabsorbed in the proximal tubule), serum uric acid rises. The triad of elevated hematocrit, elevated albumin, and elevated uric acid is therefore a fingerprint of pure dehydration. Treatment is fluid replacement, which restores all three values toward baseline.

Why the other choices are wrong

A, B, C
Decreased hematocrit would indicate dilution (fluid overload, transfusion, anemia), not dehydration
D
Albumin moves WITH hematocrit in dehydration (both concentrate together); they cannot diverge
F
Hematocrit and albumin rise together in dehydration; uric acid also rises from reduced renal clearance

Question 2

Correct answer: A

Vignette in brief: A 43-year-old man is evaluated for occasional retrosternal chest pressure that develops with moderate exertion and sometimes occurs at rest.

Correct answer: A. Arginine

In a healthy coronary endothelium, acetylcholine binds endothelial muscarinic (M3) receptors and activates endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS). eNOS catalyzes the conversion of L-arginine and molecular oxygen into nitric oxide (NO) and L-citrulline. NO diffuses across the basement membrane into the underlying vascular smooth muscle, where it activates soluble guanylate cyclase. The resulting rise in cyclic GMP triggers smooth muscle relaxation and vasodilation. The dilation observed with acetylcholine infusion therefore requires intact endothelium plus available L-arginine substrate. This question highlights a clinical paradox: in patients with significant atherosclerotic disease and dysfunctional endothelium, acetylcholine produces vasoCONSTRICTION instead of vasodilation, because the damaged endothelium fails to generate NO and the direct muscarinic effect on vascular smooth muscle (which is constrictive in the absence of NO release) predominates. The fact that this patient's vessels still dilated indicates relatively preserved endothelial function despite the family history. The same eNOS-NO pathway underlies endothelium-dependent flow-mediated dilation tested in research vascular studies. Other vasodilator pathways include adenosine (acting via A2 receptors on smooth muscle), prostacyclin (PGI2 from cyclooxygenase activity in endothelium), and shear-stress-induced NO release.

Why the other choices are wrong

B
Aspartate is a non-essential amino acid involved in the urea and aspartate-malate shuttle cycles, no direct role in vasodilation
C
Glutamate is a major CNS excitatory neurotransmitter and TCA cycle intermediate, not a substrate for endothelial vasodilator synthesis
D
Tryptophan is the precursor for serotonin and niacin synthesis, not vasodilation
E
Tyrosine is the precursor for catecholamines (dopamine, norepinephrine, epinephrine, all VASOCONSTRICTORS), not for endothelial vasodilators

Question 3

Correct answer: D

Vignette in brief: A 32-year-old woman comes to the office for a preconception medical evaluation. She feels well, reports no health issues, and has regular menstrual cycles.

Correct answer: D. Unchanged EF, increased LV cavity size, increased max CO

Endurance training imposes chronic volume overload on the heart because cardiac output must remain elevated for prolonged exercise sessions. The myocardium adapts via eccentric hypertrophy: new sarcomeres are added in SERIES (end to end) rather than in parallel, lengthening individual myocardial fibers and enlarging the LV chamber dimension. Stroke volume rises substantially because the larger ventricle can accept and eject more blood per beat. Resting heart rate falls (the marked bradycardia of trained endurance athletes, here 60 per minute) because the increased stroke volume meets the same resting cardiac output demand with fewer beats. EF (= SV / EDV) stays in the normal range or rises slightly because both numerator and denominator grow proportionally. Maximum cardiac output during peak exercise is substantially increased compared with a sedentary peer (often 35 to 40 L/min in elite endurance athletes versus 20 to 25 L/min in untrained controls), because peak SV is dramatically higher even though peak HR is similar. This contrasts with concentric hypertrophy seen with chronic pressure overload (sustained hypertension, aortic stenosis, resistance training), where sarcomeres are added in PARALLEL, the wall thickens without chamber enlargement, EF stays normal or rises modestly, but chamber size is unchanged or decreased.

Why the other choices are wrong

A
Decreased EF would suggest dilated cardiomyopathy, not the healthy adaptive eccentric hypertrophy of endurance training
B, C
Decreased LV cavity size is the pattern of concentric hypertrophy from pressure overload (chronic HTN, AS), not endurance training
E
Decreased EF and unchanged cavity size do not fit any healthy or trained cardiac adaptation

Question 4

Correct answer: E

Vignette in brief: A 48-year-old man with diet-controlled type 2 diabetes mellitus is being evaluated for occasional retrosternal chest pain.

Correct answer: E. Total systemic vascular resistance

During exercise, working skeletal muscle releases local metabolic vasodilators (adenosine, lactate, K+, CO2, H+) that produce profound arteriolar dilation in the active muscle beds. This regional vasodilation drops the resistance in the largest vascular bed of the body. Even though sympathetic activation produces compensatory vasoconstriction in non-exercising beds (splanchnic, renal), the net effect is a substantial decrease in total systemic vascular resistance. Despite the SVR drop, mean arterial pressure rises modestly during exercise (here 95 to 112) because cardiac output increases dramatically (HR roughly doubles to 132, and stroke volume also rises). The relationship MAP = CO x SVR holds: a large CO increase overpowers the SVR fall, raising MAP. Pulmonary artery systolic pressure rises with the increased CO crossing the pulmonary bed. Systemic systolic pressure also rises. LVEDP typically stays close to baseline or rises slightly with increased venous return. So the only parameter that drops during peak exercise is total SVR.

A
Cardiac output rises substantially during exercise (HR x SV both increase)
B
LVEDP is roughly unchanged at submaximal exercise, may rise slightly at peak
C
Pulmonary artery systolic pressure rises modestly to accommodate the higher CO crossing the lung
D
Systemic systolic BP rises during exercise, consistent with the MAP increase seen here

Question 5

Correct answer: D

Vignette in brief: A 54-year-old man is being evaluated for exertional dyspnea that started 6 months ago. The patient has no associated cough or wheezing.

Correct answer: D. Rate of oxygen consumption

The Fick principle is the foundational method for calculating cardiac output from gas exchange data. It states that the rate at which a tissue takes up a substance equals blood flow through that tissue multiplied by the arteriovenous concentration difference of that substance. Applied to the lungs and using oxygen as the substance, cardiac output equals oxygen consumption divided by the arteriovenous oxygen content difference: CO = VO2 / (CaO2 - CvO2). Given the cath data: arterial oxygen content (CaO2) is 20 mL/100 mL, mixed venous oxygen content (CvO2) is 15 mL/100 mL, so the AV difference is already calculable as 5 mL/100 mL. The only missing parameter is VO2 (rate of oxygen consumption, typically measured by a metabolic cart or a Douglas bag). Once VO2 is obtained, CO follows directly. The other parameters listed are either already derivable from the given data (oxygen-binding capacity and Hb saturation underlie the oxygen content values), already provided, or irrelevant (respiratory quotient is for CO2/O2 metabolism ratios, not directly used in Fick CO calculation).

Why the other choices are wrong

A
AV difference in oxygen content is already calculable from the given CaO2 (20) and CvO2 (15) values: 20 - 15 = 5 mL/100 mL
B
Oxygen binding capacity of Hb is incorporated into the oxygen content values already given, so not separately needed
C
Hemoglobin saturation is also incorporated into the oxygen content values
E
Respiratory quotient (CO2 produced / O2 consumed) is used in indirect calorimetry, not in the Fick equation for CO

Question 6

Correct answer: B

Vignette in brief: A 32-year-old woman is recruited for a research study. She is evaluated in the hospital as part of the study protocol.

Correct answer: B. Exercise

The hallmark of normal exercise physiology is matched scaling of cardiac output and minute ventilation to metabolic demand. During exercise, the working muscles drive massive increases in oxygen consumption and CO2 production. The cardiovascular response includes sympathetic activation that raises heart rate (chronotropy), stroke volume, and contractility (inotropy), producing a substantial rise in cardiac output (from ~5 L/min at rest up to 25 L/min or more in trained individuals at peak). Simultaneously, the respiratory system increases ventilation through neural and chemoreceptor feedback to perfectly match CO2 elimination to production and oxygen uptake to consumption. As a result, arterial PaO2 and PaCO2 remain essentially normal across the full range of submaximal to maximal exercise. This is what distinguishes exercise from the other listed conditions: each of them disrupts gas exchange in a characteristic way that would show on the ABG. Carotid sinus massage triggers a vagal response and BRADYCARDIA (opposite of increased HR). High altitude lowers PaO2 (the inspired partial pressure drops). Panic attack with hyperventilation drops PaCO2 (respiratory alkalosis). Pulmonary artery occlusion (e.g. PE) often produces hypoxemia from V/Q mismatch and hypocapnia from compensatory hyperventilation, plus signs of right heart strain.

C

D

Question 7

Correct answer: B

Vignette in brief: A 64-year-old man presents to the office with chest discomfort and mild shortness of breath that started one hour ago after an argument with his wife.

Correct answer: B. Left ventricular hypertrophy

A presystolic heart sound, occurring just before S1, is the fourth heart sound (S4) or atrial gallop. S4 is generated when the atria contract forcefully to push blood into a ventricle that has become stiff and non-compliant. The atrial kick (active filling phase of diastole, contributing ~25% of LV filling in normal hearts) collides with the resistant LV wall, producing the audible low-frequency thump immediately before S1. The most common cause is concentric LV hypertrophy from chronic pressure overload: long-standing hypertension, severe aortic stenosis, or hypertrophic cardiomyopathy. The thickened muscular wall has reduced compliance, so any given filling volume produces a higher intracardiac pressure rise (steeper end-diastolic pressure-volume relationship). The S4 is best heard with the bell of the stethoscope at the cardiac apex, with the patient in the left lateral decubitus position to bring the LV apex closest to the chest wall, and on held expiration to reduce competing breath sounds. This patient's hypertension, diabetes, and current hypertensive episode (180/100) all support concentric LVH as the underlying cause. An S4 also occurs in acute MI as ischemic tissue becomes acutely stiff, and in active angina from transient diastolic dysfunction. In contrast, S3 is heard in EARLY diastole (during passive rapid filling), reflects LV cavity enlargement and reduced compliance to volume overload, and is common in dilated cardiomyopathy, severe MR, severe AR, and high-output states.

Why the other choices are wrong

A
Papillary muscle dysfunction acutely causes mitral regurgitation, producing a holosystolic murmur, not a presystolic sound
C
Increased preload alone would produce an S3 (during rapid passive filling), not an S4. S4 reflects reduced LV compliance, not increased filling
D
AR produces a diastolic decrescendo murmur, not a presystolic sound; though AR can secondarily cause LV dilation and an S3
E
Mitral leaflet fusion is mitral stenosis, which produces an opening snap and diastolic rumble, not a presystolic sound (though severe MS may have a loud S1)

Question 8

Correct answer: B

Vignette in brief: A 44-year-old man with progressive dyspnea is diagnosed with dilated cardiomyopathy. Despite optimal medical therapy, he continues to have symptoms and disease progression is noted.

Correct answer: B. Sodium-calcium exchanger (NCX)

During each cardiac cycle, cytoplasmic calcium rises to trigger contraction and must then fall to allow relaxation. The rise comes from two sources: a small influx through voltage-gated L-type calcium channels during phase 2 of the action potential, and a much larger release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum through ryanodine receptors (calcium-induced calcium release). To enable relaxation, this calcium must be cleared from the cytoplasm rapidly. Two main extrusion pathways do this: (1) SERCA (sarcoplasmic/endoplasmic reticulum Ca-ATPase) pumps calcium back into the SR using ATP, accounting for roughly 70 percent of calcium removal in a healthy ventricular myocyte. (2) The Na/Ca exchanger (NCX) in the sarcolemma extrudes calcium across the cell membrane to the extracellular space, using the inward sodium gradient as its energy source, accounting for roughly 30 percent of calcium removal. Among the listed options, only NCX actively moves calcium OUT of the cytoplasm. SERCA is not in the answer choices. The other proteins either bring calcium into the cytoplasm (RyR releases from SR; L-type channels bring in extracellular Ca) or bind calcium for signaling purposes (calmodulin, troponin C). In failing dilated cardiomyopathic hearts, SERCA expression and activity are typically reduced, so NCX takes on a relatively larger share of calcium clearance, and overall calcium handling is slower, contributing to impaired relaxation (lusitropic failure) and reduced contractility.

Why the other choices are wrong

A
Calmodulin binds calcium to enable downstream signaling (CaMK activation), but does not transport calcium across membranes
C
Ryanodine receptors RELEASE calcium from SR into cytoplasm (raising cytoplasmic Ca), opposite of what is being asked
D
Troponin C binds calcium to trigger thin filament conformational changes that enable cross-bridge cycling; it does not move calcium
E
L-type calcium channels bring Ca INTO the cytoplasm during phase 2 of the AP, opposite direction; they are not extruders

Question 9

Correct answer: C

Vignette in brief: A large pharmaceutical company is conducting research on an experimental drug that affects blood flow in diseased tissue.

Correct answer: C. Left ventricular myocardium

Coronary blood flow is unique in the body for being PHASIC, dropping markedly during systole and rising during diastole. The reason is mechanical: during systole, the contracting LV wall generates intramural pressure that approaches or even exceeds aortic systolic pressure. This high intramural pressure compresses the small intramyocardial coronary arterioles, occluding flow through them despite the high systemic pressure pushing forward. As the LV relaxes during diastole, intramyocardial pressure drops to near zero, the arterioles re-open, and flow surges (driven by aortic diastolic pressure that has been steadily falling but is still well above myocardial venous pressure). Roughly 80 percent of LV coronary blood flow occurs during diastole. This phasic pattern is most extreme in the LEFT coronary distribution because LV systolic pressure is much higher than RV systolic pressure (approximately 120 mm Hg vs 25 mm Hg). RV coronary flow shows much less systolic dip because RV systolic pressure (~25 mm Hg) only partially compresses RV coronary vessels. Brain, kidney, and other organs have continuous flow throughout the cardiac cycle with only minor pulse pressure variation; they do not show the systolic blackout pattern shown. Clinically, this explains why coronary perfusion pressure equals aortic diastolic pressure minus LV end-diastolic pressure, and why tachycardia (which shortens diastole more than systole) compromises coronary supply.

Why the other choices are wrong

A
Adrenal medulla has continuous arterial flow, no systolic occlusion pattern
B
Brain cortex receives continuous flow modulated by autoregulation, not phasic systolic interruption
D, E
Renal cortex and medulla have continuous flow modulated by autoregulation
F
RV myocardium shows much less systolic dip because RV systolic pressure is only about 25 mm Hg, not enough to fully compress RV coronary vessels
G
Subcortical brain nuclei have continuous flow

Correct answer: C. Stroke volume

The dashed PV loop shows increased contractility (positive inotropy), the classic signature being a leftward shift of the end-systolic point at unchanged end-diastolic volume, accompanied by a higher peak ejection pressure. Mechanically, increased contractility means the ventricle ejects more vigorously and ejects to a smaller residual volume during each cycle. The slope of the end-systolic pressure-volume relationship (ESPVR) line, which connects the end-systolic points across various loading conditions, becomes STEEPER with positive inotropy and SHALLOWER with negative inotropy. Drugs producing this pattern include digoxin (in HFrEF), dobutamine, milrinone, and endogenous sympathetic surge. In this CABG patient with acute decompensation, the most likely drug is one of these positive inotropes. Correct answer: D

Vignette in brief: A 56-year-old man arrives in the emergency department reporting that his heart feels fast and irregular.

Correct answer: D. Atrial fibrillation: chaotic fibrillatory baseline with no discernible P waves and irregularly irregular narrow QRS intervals

The scenario describes holiday heart syndrome: acute atrial fibrillation precipitated by binge alcohol intake. AF on ECG has three hallmark features: absent organized P waves replaced by chaotic fibrillatory undulations, irregularly irregular R-R intervals with no underlying repeating pattern, and a typically rapid uncontrolled ventricular response above 100 per minute. Mechanistically, foci of rapid atrial electrical activity (often originating near the pulmonary venous ostia) fire at 400 to 600 per minute. The AV node filters and transmits these impulses unpredictably to the ventricles. Common predisposing and triggering factors include age-related atrial remodeling, hypertension, valvular disease (especially mitral stenosis), hyperthyroidism, sympathetic surge from sepsis or stress, and excessive alcohol intake.

Question 13

Correct answer: C

Vignette in brief: A 65-year-old woman is brought to the hospital with sudden chest heaviness and shortness of breath that began one hour earlier.

Correct answer: C. Decreased contractility, increased SVR, increased PA systolic pressure

Acute lateral wall STEMI with hypotension and cold clammy extremities indicates cardiogenic shock from left ventricular pump failure. Three concurrent hemodynamic changes follow. First, infarcted myocardium contracts poorly, so cardiac contractility falls. Second, the resulting drop in blood pressure unloads the carotid and aortic baroreceptors, removing tonic inhibition of sympathetic outflow. The sympathetic surge produces intense peripheral arteriolar vasoconstriction, raising systemic vascular resistance markedly (this is what makes the extremities cold and clammy). Third, the failing LV cannot eject enough blood forward, so LV end-diastolic pressure climbs and transmits retrograde through the left atrium into the pulmonary venous system, raising pulmonary capillary wedge pressure. To continue moving blood forward across the now higher pulmonary venous pressure, pulmonary artery systolic pressure must also rise. If the RV cannot generate this higher pressure, right ventricular failure follows.

Correct answer: E

Vignette in brief: A 45-year-old woman enrolled in a cardiovascular health study wears a continuous 24-hour ambulatory ECG monitor.

Correct answer: E. Z > X > Y (post-PVC beat highest, PVC lowest)

Beat Y is a premature ventricular contraction, recognized on ECG by its wide QRS, oppositely-directed T wave, and the brief pause that follows. PVCs originate in ectopic ventricular foci with enhanced automaticity and depolarize the ventricles ahead of schedule, before the LV has finished its usual diastolic filling time. The EDV for beat Y is therefore reduced compared with a baseline sinus beat. The compensatory pause that follows occurs because the SA node continues firing on its own schedule, but the next sinus impulse arrives during the ventricular refractory period and is blocked. The next successfully conducted beat (Z) therefore has a longer diastolic filling interval than usual, producing a larger EDV. The forceful contraction generated by this larger preload is sometimes felt as a palpitation. The correct ranking is Z (post-PVC, longest fill time) greater than X (sinus, normal fill time) greater than Y (PVC, shortened fill time).

Correct answer: B

Vignette in brief: A 44-year-old man is hospitalized after a motor vehicle collision with multiple injuries. A traumatic arteriovenous fistula has formed as a result of vascular trauma.

Correct answer: B. PV loop showing widened stroke volume with slightly lower peak pressure and leftward shift of end-systolic point (reduced afterload with increased preload)

An arteriovenous fistula creates a low-resistance shortcut from the arterial side directly to the venous side, bypassing the high-resistance arteriolar bed. Three hemodynamic consequences follow. First, the parallel low-resistance pathway lowers overall systemic vascular resistance, so the LV ejects against less afterload. Second, blood returns rapidly to the right heart through the venous limb, raising venous return and increasing LV preload (the EDV grows). Third, the combination of less afterload and more preload increases stroke volume, widening the PV loop horizontally and producing a slightly lower peak ejection pressure. Baseline contractility is initially unchanged (the slope of the end-systolic pressure-volume relationship is preserved), although a sustained reflex may eventually adjust it. Large chronic AV fistulas can drive high-output heart failure if the LV cannot keep pace with the elevated venous return demand.

Question 16

Correct answer: D

Vignette in brief: A 4-week-old boy is scheduled for cardiac catheterization to close a patent ductus arteriosus.

Correct answer: D. Pulmonary artery

After birth, pulmonary vascular resistance normally falls and aortic pressure exceeds pulmonary arterial pressure throughout the cardiac cycle. When the ductus arteriosus fails to close (as is common in premature infants), this pressure gradient drives oxygenated blood from the descending aorta backward through the PDA into the main pulmonary artery, both during systole and diastole. The deoxygenated blood that the right ventricle normally ejects into the pulmonary artery (PaO2 around 40 mm Hg) gets mixed with this incoming oxygenated aortic blood, raising the measured PaO2 of pulmonary artery samples above the normal value. All other compartments retain their usual oxygen content: the aorta receives normally oxygenated blood from the LV, the left atrium continues to receive blood from the pulmonary veins (which is fully oxygenated regardless of shunt presence), and the right atrium receives systemic venous return that is upstream of the PDA shunt point. If the shunt later reverses (Eisenmenger physiology with elevated pulmonary pressures), aortic PaO2 could drop and the patient would become cyanotic, which is not described here.

The ascending aorta is upstream of the PDA shunt; its blood is normally oxygenated by the LV and is not affected

Descending aortic blood proximal to the shunt is also fully oxygenated, unchanged from normal

E

Question 19

Correct answer: E

Vignette in brief: Following the cardiac evaluation that suggested heart failure, the same 52-year-old man is examined further.

Correct answer: E. Low lactate dehydrogenase content

Pleural effusions are classified as transudates or exudates using the Light criteria, which compare pleural protein and LDH to serum levels. A transudate forms when an imbalance in hydrostatic or oncotic forces drives fluid across an otherwise intact capillary membrane. In heart failure, elevated pulmonary venous and capillary hydrostatic pressure pushes a protein-poor and LDH-poor ultrafiltrate into the pleural space, so the fluid has low protein and low LDH relative to serum. An exudate forms when increased capillary permeability (infection, malignancy, inflammation) allows protein and large molecules to leak into the space, producing high protein and high LDH levels. Heart failure is the most common cause of transudative effusion; pneumonia, malignancy, tuberculosis, and rheumatologic disease are common causes of exudate. The Light criteria flag an effusion as exudative if pleural-to-serum protein ratio exceeds 0.5, pleural-to-serum LDH ratio exceeds 0.6, or pleural LDH is more than two-thirds of the upper limit of normal serum LDH.

Why the other choices are wrong

A
