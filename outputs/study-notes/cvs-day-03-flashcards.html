Ventricular action potential 6 cards
1List the five phases of the ventricular myocyte action potential and the dominant ion movement of each.
Phase 0 = rapid depolarization, Na⁺ influx; phase 1 = initial repolarization, transient K⁺ out / Na⁺ close; phase 2 = plateau, L-type Ca²⁺ in balanced by K⁺ out; phase 3 = rapid repolarization, K⁺ efflux dominates; phase 4 = resting at −90 mV via K⁺ leak.
2What unique property of the cardiac action potential gives rise to phase 2?
Simultaneous opening of L-type Ca²⁺ channels and partial closure of K⁺ channels, producing the prolonged plateau. This is the distinctive feature absent in skeletal and smooth muscle APs.
3During which phases of the ventricular AP is the cell in its effective refractory period?
Phases 0–3 — Na⁺ channels are inactivated and cannot re-open until late phase 3 / early 4. This prevents tetanic contraction.
4An ECG shows a wide QRS complex with opposite-direction T wave and a brief compensatory pause. Identify the beat and the LV-volume sequence (less than vs more than normal) of the surrounding beats.
Premature ventricular contraction (PVC). The PVC itself has decreased LV end-diastolic volume (less filling time). The post-PVC beat has increased EDV from the compensatory pause → ↑ SV → palpitation.
5How do Vaughan-Williams class IB and class III drugs affect AP duration?
Class IB (lidocaine, mexiletine) shorten AP duration; class III (amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide, ibutilide) prolong AP duration via K⁺ channel block.
6Class IC antiarrhythmics (flecainide, propafenone) — main caution?
Avoid in structural heart disease (post-MI, HFrEF, severe LVH) — they can be pro-arrhythmic and increase mortality in those patients. Use only in healthy hearts ("pill-in-the-pocket" AFib).
Pacemaker action potential 6 cards
7Describe the three relevant phases of an SA-nodal pacemaker action potential.
Phase 4 (spontaneous depolarization) = HCN "funny" Na⁺ current early → T-type Ca²⁺ → L-type Ca²⁺ approaching threshold. Phase 0 = slow upstroke from L-type Ca²⁺ influx. Phase 3 = repolarization via K⁺ efflux.
8Which current is responsible for the slope of phase 4 in pacemaker cells?
The HCN ("funny") current — slow Na⁺ inflow activated by hyperpolarization. Steeper slope → faster pacemaker rate.
9How does verapamil alter the pacemaker action potential?
Blocks L-type Ca²⁺ channels → slows the late phase 4 climb (less Ca²⁺ contribution) and slows phase 0 upstroke → ↓ pacemaker firing rate + ↓ AV conduction + prolonged AV refractory period.
10Mechanism of ivabradine?
Selective HCN ("funny") channel blocker. Slows phase 4 depolarization in the SA node → ↓ HR with no effect on contractility, conduction, or BP.
11How does adenosine terminate AVNRT?
Activates A1 adenosine receptors → opens K⁺ channels → hyperpolarizes AV nodal cells + prolongs AV refractoriness → breaks the reentrant circuit. Very short half-life (~10 s).
12Which ionic current dominates AV-nodal phase 0, and what drugs target it for rate control in AFib?
L-type Ca²⁺ current. Targeted by non-dihydropyridine CCBs (verapamil, diltiazem); β-blockers also indirectly reduce its activity. Both prolong AV refractoriness → slower ventricular rate.
Excitation–contraction coupling 4 cards
13Walk through cardiomyocyte EC coupling in order.
AP → L-type Ca²⁺ opens → small Ca²⁺ influx → Ca²⁺-induced Ca²⁺ release from SR via ryanodine receptor → cytosolic Ca²⁺ binds troponin C → tropomyosin moves → actin-myosin cross-bridge cycling → contraction.
14How is cytosolic Ca²⁺ cleared during relaxation?
SERCA pumps Ca²⁺ back into the SR (consumes ATP). Sarcolemmal NCX (Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger) extrudes Ca²⁺ across the cell membrane using the Na⁺ gradient. A smaller amount via plasma-membrane Ca²⁺ ATPase.
15Mechanism by which digoxin increases contractility?
Blocks the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase → ↑ intracellular Na⁺ → reduces the Na⁺ gradient that drives NCX → NCX extrudes less Ca²⁺ → ↑ intracellular Ca²⁺ → ↑ contractility.
16Why does hypokalemia potentiate digoxin toxicity?
K⁺ and digoxin compete for the same binding site on Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase. Low extracellular K⁺ allows digoxin to bind more readily, intensifying its inhibition and toxic effects.
Conduction system 5 cards
17Order cardiac conduction velocities from fastest to slowest.
Park-At-Ventura, AV — Purkinje > atrial myocardium > ventricular myocardium > AV node.
18Why is AV-nodal delay important?
It separates atrial systole from ventricular systole so that atrial kick can finish filling the ventricles before they contract. It also filters supraventricular tachycardias to a survivable ventricular rate.
19Intrinsic firing rate hierarchy for the cardiac pacemakers?
SA node 60–100/min; AV junction / His ~40–60/min (narrow-QRS escape); Purkinje / ventricular ~20–40/min (wide-QRS escape).
20In third-degree AV block, what determines whether the escape rhythm has a narrow or wide QRS?
Location of the block. Above the bundle bifurcation → narrow QRS escape from the His bundle (better — more stable, 40–60/min). Below the bifurcation → wide QRS ventricular escape (unstable, 20–40/min).
21What anatomic structure determines the ventricular response rate in atrial fibrillation?
The AV node refractory period. Chaotic atrial impulses (400–600/min) bombard the AV node; only those arriving outside refractoriness conduct. Rate-control drugs (β-blockers, non-DHP CCBs, digoxin) prolong AV refractoriness.
ECG basics 5 cards
22What does each interval measure: PR, QRS, QT?
PR = atrial depolarization + AV delay (normal 0.12–0.20 s). QRS = ventricular depolarization (normal < 0.12 s). QT = ventricular depolarization + repolarization (rate-corrected QTc < 0.44 s in men, < 0.46 s in women).
23How is electrical axis assessed at a glance?
Look at lead I and lead aVF. Both positive = normal axis. I positive, aVF negative = left axis deviation (–30° to –90°). I negative, aVF positive = right axis deviation (+90° to +180°).
24Distinguish 1°, Mobitz I, Mobitz II, and 3° heart blocks on ECG.
1° = constant long PR (> 0.20 s); all P waves conduct. Mobitz I (Wenckebach) = progressive PR lengthening until a dropped beat (AV-nodal, benign). Mobitz II = constant PR with intermittent dropped beats (infranodal, pacemaker indicated). 3° = complete dissociation of P waves from QRS; escape rhythm.
25Wide notched "p-mitrale" vs tall "p-pulmonale" — what do they signify?
P-mitrale (wide notched P in II, biphasic in V1) = left atrial enlargement, classically from mitral stenosis. P-pulmonale (tall peaked P in II ≥ 2.5 mm) = right atrial enlargement, classically from pulmonary hypertension or COPD.
26What localises an inferior MI on ECG, and which artery is usually responsible?
ST elevation in leads II, III, and aVF. Usually the right coronary artery (RCA). Often accompanied by AV-nodal block (Mobitz I or transient 3°) because the RCA also supplies the AV node in most patients.
Arrhythmia recognition 8 cards
27An irregularly irregular rhythm with no organized P waves, narrow QRS, and rate 138/min in a healthy 56-year-old after a night of heavy drinking — diagnosis and label?
Atrial fibrillation — "holiday heart syndrome." Acute alcohol can trigger AFib in otherwise healthy adults via autonomic shifts and atrial irritation.
28Sawtooth ECG pattern at atrial rate 300/min with ventricular response 150/min — diagnosis and conduction ratio?
Atrial flutter with 2:1 AV conduction. Atrial rate ~300/min is divided by the AV node into ventricular responses of 150 (2:1), 100 (3:1), or 75 (4:1).
29Anatomic origin of paroxysmal atrial fibrillation triggers, and corresponding curative ablation target?
Ectopic foci most often arise in the pulmonary veins. Curative ablation = pulmonary vein isolation (circumferential RF lesions around the PV ostia).
30Anatomic substrate of typical atrial flutter and ablation target?
Macroreentrant circuit around the tricuspid annulus with the slow zone at the cavotricuspid isthmus (between TV annulus and IVC in the right atrium). RF ablation across this isthmus is curative.
31A 23-year-old with sudden palpitations and a regular narrow tachycardia at 160/min terminates with right carotid sinus massage. Mechanism?
Paroxysmal SVT — most commonly AVNRT. Carotid sinus pressure ↑ baroreceptor firing → ↑ vagal output (via CN IX afferent) → prolonged AV node refractory period → reentrant circuit breaks.
32An older man passes out in the garage. ECG shows complete dissociation of P waves and QRS with a narrow-QRS escape rhythm at 45/min. Where is the escape pacemaker?
Above the bundle bifurcation — the His bundle (junctional escape). Narrow QRS implies the escape uses the normal conduction system below the block.
33An older man passes out, complete heart block with a wide-QRS escape rhythm at 25/min — where is the escape pacemaker?
Below the bundle bifurcation — Purkinje / ventricular escape. Wide QRS, slow rate, less reliable. Needs immediate pacing.
34Adolescent fainted while standing in heat; supine BP 105/60, standing BP 110/65 (no orthostatic drop), normal ECG, brief unresponsiveness with rapid recovery. Mechanism of syncope?
Vasovagal (reflex) syncope. Brainstem misfires both autonomic limbs: cardioinhibitory (↑ vagal → ↓ HR + ↓ contractility) and vasodepressor (↓ sympathetic → vasodilation + ↓ preload) → ↓ MAP → cerebral hypoperfusion.
Antiarrhythmic + ablation map 4 cards
35Match anatomic ablation targets to arrhythmias.
AFib → pulmonary veins; atrial flutter → cavotricuspid isthmus; AVNRT → slow pathway of AV node; WPW → accessory pathway (bundle of Kent); RVOT VT → right ventricular outflow tract.
36Vaughan-Williams classes — one-liner per class.
IA quinidine/procainamide (Na⁺ block, ↑ AP); IB lidocaine/mexiletine (Na⁺ block, ↓ AP, ischemic); IC flecainide/propafenone (strong Na⁺ block, no AP change, healthy hearts only); II β-blockers; III amiodarone/sotalol/dofetilide/ibutilide (K⁺ block, ↑ AP/QT); IV non-DHP CCBs; plus adenosine, magnesium, digoxin.
37Why is amiodarone used cautiously despite broad efficacy?
Multi-organ toxicity profile from its iodine content and lipophilicity: thyroid (hypo or hyper), lung (pulmonary fibrosis), liver (transaminitis), skin (blue-gray discoloration, photosensitivity), eye (corneal microdeposits), heart (bradycardia, QT prolongation).
38First-line rate control agents for AFib in a patient without HFrEF?
β-blocker (metoprolol, atenolol) or non-dihydropyridine CCB (diltiazem, verapamil). Both work by prolonging AV nodal refractoriness. Digoxin is added if rate remains uncontrolled, especially with concomitant HFrEF.
Coronary and vascular biology 5 cards
39Why does the heart require flow increases rather than extraction increases to meet ↑ demand?
Resting myocardial O₂ extraction is already > 70% — the highest of any tissue. There is little reserve in the arteriovenous O₂ difference, so meeting ↑ demand requires ↑ coronary blood flow.
40Two main mediators of coronary autoregulation, and the MAP range over which it operates?
Adenosine and nitric oxide. Effective over MAP 60–140 mmHg. Below 60, ischemia; above 140, autoregulation fails and flow rises with pressure.
41Why is LV myocardium perfused mainly in diastole?
During systole the high intramyocardial pressure (especially in the subendocardium) compresses coronary arterioles. In diastole the LV is relaxed → vessels reopen → blood flows. The subendocardium is most vulnerable to ischemia because it experiences the highest systolic pressure.
42Substrate and mediator of endothelial-derived NO?
Substrate = L-arginine. Endothelial NOS converts arginine + O₂ + NADPH → NO + L-citrulline. NO diffuses into smooth muscle → activates guanylate cyclase → ↑ cGMP → MLCK dephosphorylation → vasodilation.
43Which vessel in the body has the lowest oxygen saturation?
The coronary sinus. Because cardiac O₂ extraction is > 70% at rest, the venous return draining the heart is more desaturated than mixed venous blood anywhere else.
Baroreceptors and chemoreceptors 5 cards
44Afferent cranial nerves for baroreceptors at the carotid sinus vs aortic arch?
Carotid sinus → CN IX (glossopharyngeal). Aortic arch → CN X (vagus). Mnemonic: carotid → IX, aortic → X.
45Same question for peripheral chemoreceptors (carotid body vs aortic body)?
Same pairing — carotid body → CN IX, aortic body → CN X.
46An elderly man passes out repeatedly while buttoning a tight shirt collar. Mechanism?
Carotid sinus hypersensitivity. Pressure on the sinus → exaggerated baroreceptor firing → CN IX → medulla → ↑ vagal output + ↓ sympathetic → bradycardia + vasodilation → syncope.
47Hemorrhagic shock baroreflex response — describe baroreceptor firing, contractility, and ANP release.
↓ BP → ↓ arterial stretch → ↓ baroreceptor firing → ↑ sympathetic + ↓ vagal → ↑ HR, ↑ contractility, vasoconstriction. Atrial filling is reduced → ↓ ANP release.
48Phenylephrine is given to a normal volunteer. Predict the HR change and the responsible reflex.
Phenylephrine (α₁ agonist) ↑ SVR → ↑ MAP. Baroreceptor stretch ↑ → vagal output ↑ → reflex bradycardia. This is the classic test of intact baroreflex.
Shock physiology 7 cards
49Hemodynamic profile (CVP, PCWP, CO, SVR, SvO₂) of hypovolemic shock?
CVP ↓, PCWP ↓, CO ↓, SVR ↑, SvO₂ ↓. Skin cold and clammy. Causes: hemorrhage, GI losses (diarrhea, vomiting), burns.
50Hemodynamic profile of cardiogenic shock?
CVP ↑, PCWP ↑, CO ↓, SVR ↑ (compensatory vasoconstriction), SvO₂ ↓. Skin cold. Classic cause: large anterior STEMI with LV failure.
51Hemodynamic profile of distributive shock (septic)?
CVP ↓, PCWP ↓, CO usually ↑, SVR ↓, SvO₂ usually ↑. Skin warm. Inflammatory cytokines cause vasodilation; cardiac output rises to maintain perfusion until late decompensation.
52Why is the PCWP paradoxically elevated in cardiac tamponade despite intrinsically low LV preload?
Pericardial fluid externally compresses the LV; the measured wedge pressure reflects extrinsic compression rather than true LV filling. The LV cavity itself is small (low preload), but the catheter "sees" the high pericardial pressure transmitted through the LA.
53A 2-year-old with 4 days of diarrhea is lethargic with BP 65/42, dry mucous membranes, sunken eyes, hyperactive bowel sounds, and Kussmaul respirations. Acid-base picture and underlying mechanism?
Lactic acidosis from tissue hypoperfusion + bicarbonate loss in stool = metabolic acidosis. Body compensates with ↑ ventilatory rate → compensatory respiratory alkalosis (Kussmaul breathing).
54A trauma patient receives 2 L of normal saline after a hemorrhagic insult. Which intracellular parameter rises first to improve cardiac output?
End-diastolic sarcomere length. ↑ Venous return → ↑ EDV → ↑ sarcomere stretch → ↑ SV via Frank-Starling. HR, contractility, SVR, and PRA are already maximal from baroreflex.
55Cardinal physical findings of cardiac tamponade?
Beck's triad — hypotension, elevated JVP, muffled heart sounds — plus pulsus paradoxus (> 10 mmHg SBP drop with inspiration). Echo shows right atrial late-diastolic collapse. Treatment: pericardiocentesis.
Heart failure cascade and pharmacology 10 cards
56Source cells of ANP and BNP, and the stimulus that triggers their release.
ANP is secreted by atrial cardiomyocytes, BNP by ventricular cardiomyocytes. The stimulus is myocardial wall stretch from volume overload (or pressure overload that distends the chamber).
57Downstream effects of ANP/BNP on the kidney?
↑ GFR (afferent dilation + efferent constriction), ↓ proximal Na⁺ reabsorption, ↓ renin secretion → natriuresis and diuresis. Also vasodilation systemically.
58Why is intravenous saline infusion in a healthy subject expected to ↑ GFR?
Volume load stretches atria and ventricles → ANP and BNP release → activate guanylate cyclase via natriuretic peptide receptors → afferent arteriolar dilation + efferent constriction → ↑ glomerular hydrostatic pressure → ↑ GFR + natriuresis.
59Mechanism of sacubitril and why it is paired with valsartan in HFrEF?
Sacubitril inhibits neprilysin, the metalloprotease that degrades ANP and BNP → ↑ endogenous natriuretic peptide activity. Sacubitril alone would also reduce angiotensin II breakdown, so it is paired with an ARB (valsartan) to block the RAAS arm — together they form an "ARNi."
60List the four-pillar guideline-directed medical therapy for HFrEF.
(1) β-blocker (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol). (2) ACE-i, ARB, or ARNi (sacubitril/valsartan). (3) MRA (spironolactone or eplerenone). (4) SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin).
61Why is sympathetic activation in HFrEF considered "maladaptive over time"?
Early ↑ sympathetic tone supports BP via ↑ HR, contractility, and vasoconstriction. Chronically, the same activation drives adverse cardiac remodeling, β-receptor downregulation, arrhythmias, and progressive pump failure. Blocking this arm with β-blockers improves survival.
62First-line vasodilator in acute decompensated heart failure with hypertensive crisis?
Sodium nitroprusside — balanced venous and arterial vasodilator. Drops both preload and afterload while keeping SV essentially preserved. Allows pulmonary venous decompression without dropping CO.
63Why does a chronic AV fistula eventually cause high-output heart failure?
The fistula provides a low-resistance pathway → chronic ↑ preload (↑ venous return) + ↓ effective SVR. Initially CO rises and SV widens (eccentric remodeling). Over years the volume overload exceeds the heart's adaptive capacity → high-output failure with ↑ filling pressures and pulmonary congestion.
64Predict the autonomic response when a tight collar is tightened around the neck of a person with carotid sinus hypersensitivity.
Pressure on the carotid sinus → ↑ baroreceptor stretch → ↑ CN IX firing to medulla → exaggerated ↑ vagal + ↓ sympathetic → bradycardia + vasodilation → cerebral hypoperfusion and syncope.
65Vasovagal vs orthostatic vs cardiogenic syncope — one distinguishing feature each.
Vasovagal: prodromal warmth, pallor, nausea; trigger (heat, pain, standing); rapid recovery; no orthostatic BP drop. Orthostatic: SBP drops ≥ 20 or DBP ≥ 10 within 3 min of standing. Cardiogenic: abrupt without prodrome; abnormal ECG (arrhythmia or structural finding); higher mortality risk.
