Ventricular action potential 6 cards
1Name the five phases of the ventricular action potential.
0 rapid depolarization (Na⁺ influx) · 1 initial repolarization (Na inactivation + transient K⁺) · 2 plateau (L-type Ca²⁺ balanced by K⁺) · 3 rapid repolarization (delayed-rectifier K⁺) · 4 rest at −90 mV (inward-rectifier K⁺ leak).
2Which ion carries the upstroke of the ventricular AP?
Na⁺, through fast voltage-gated Na channels.
3Which ion creates the plateau of the ventricular AP?
L-type Ca²⁺, balanced by K⁺ efflux. Cardiac-specific feature that lengthens the AP and supplies the trigger Ca²⁺ for EC coupling.
4Define the effective refractory period.
The interval (phases 0–3) during which Na channels are inactivated and another full AP cannot be triggered — built-in protection against tetany.
5How do Vaughan-Williams class IB and class III drugs differ in their effect on AP duration?
Class IB (lidocaine, mexiletine) shortens the AP and binds preferentially to ischemic tissue. Class III (amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide, ibutilide) blocks K⁺ channels and prolongs the AP, ERP, and QT.
6Why is the post-PVC sinus beat felt as a palpitation rather than the PVC itself?
The compensatory pause after a PVC gives the LV extra filling time → larger EDV → larger SV via Frank-Starling → the next beat is forceful enough for the patient to feel.
Pacemaker action potential 5 cards
7Name the three phases of the SA/AV nodal action potential.
4 spontaneous depolarization (HCN funny current → T-type Ca → L-type Ca) · 0 slow upstroke (L-type Ca²⁺) · 3 repolarization (K⁺ efflux). No stable resting potential.
8What does the "funny" (HCN) current do, and where?
Hyperpolarization-activated cyclic-nucleotide-gated channels carry a slow Na⁺ inward current that begins phase 4 depolarization in pacemaker cells. PKA (via β₁-driven cAMP) raises its slope → faster firing.
9Which drug selectively blocks the HCN current?
Ivabradine — lowers heart rate without changing contractility or AV conduction. Used in HFrEF when β-blockade is not tolerated.
10Why does verapamil slow the SA and AV nodes?
Non-DHP CCB blocks L-type Ca²⁺, which is responsible for both pacemaker phase 4 (late ascent to threshold) and phase 0 (slow upstroke). Result: slower firing + slower AV conduction + prolonged AV refractoriness.
11How does adenosine terminate AVNRT?
Activates A₁ receptors on AV nodal cells → opens K⁺ channels → hyperpolarization → transient AV nodal block that interrupts the reentrant circuit. Half-life ~10 seconds.
EC coupling & digoxin 4 cards
12Outline the five steps of excitation-contraction coupling in ventricular myocytes.
1 AP depolarizes sarcolemma · 2 L-type Ca²⁺ channel opens (phase 2) · 3 trigger Ca activates ryanodine receptor → CICR from SR · 4 cytosolic Ca binds troponin C → tropomyosin shifts → cross-bridge cycling · 5 relaxation by SERCA (Ca back into SR) + NCX (Ca out through sarcolemma using the Na gradient).
13Mechanism by which digoxin raises contractility.
Blocks Na/K ATPase → ↑ intracellular Na → reduces the Na gradient that powers NCX → less Ca extrusion per beat → ↑ cytosolic Ca for cross-bridge cycling → positive inotropy.
14Why does hypokalemia potentiate digoxin toxicity?
Low extracellular K⁺ leaves more digoxin-binding sites on Na/K ATPase open, increasing inhibition. Common pitfall when loop diuretics are added without K⁺ repletion.
15Classic clinical features of digoxin toxicity?
GI (nausea, vomiting, anorexia), visual (yellow-green halos = xanthopsia), and almost any arrhythmia (bradycardia, AV block, atrial tachycardia with block). Treat: stop drug, correct K⁺ carefully, digoxin-specific Fab fragments for severe cases.
Conduction velocity & escape rhythms 4 cards
16Conduction velocity order from fastest to slowest?
Purkinje > atrial myocardium > ventricular myocardium > AV node. Mnemonic: Park At Ventura, AV. AV node slowest (~0.05 m/s) by design.
17Two physiologic roles of AV nodal delay?
1 Lets atrial systole finish filling the ventricle (preserves atrial kick). 2 Filters supraventricular tachycardias (AFib, flutter) so the ventricles see a manageable rate.
18Intrinsic rates of the SA node, AV junction, and ventricular pacemakers?
SA 60–100/min · AV junction / His 40–60/min · Purkinje / ventricular 20–40/min.
19Complete heart block with a narrow QRS escape at 45/min — origin of the escape and what does narrow vs wide tell you?
Narrow QRS → escape originates in the AV junction / His bundle (above bifurcation), generally more stable. Wide QRS escape → ventricular pacemaker (below bifurcation), slower and less reliable → immediate pacing needed.
ECG basics 3 cards
20What does each ECG component represent: P, PR, QRS, QT?
P atrial depolarization. PR conduction from atria through the AV node into the ventricles. QRS ventricular depolarization. QT total ventricular depolarization + repolarization.
21Distinguish Mobitz I from Mobitz II.
Mobitz I (Wenckebach): progressive PR lengthening → dropped beat. Site = AV node. Usually benign. Mobitz II: constant PR with intermittent dropped beats. Site = infranodal (His-Purkinje). Pacemaker indicated due to high risk of progression to complete block.
22Sokolow-Lyon criterion for LVH on ECG?
S in V₁ + R in V₅ or V₆ > 35 mm.
Arrhythmia recognition 6 cards
23ECG hallmarks of atrial fibrillation?
Irregularly irregular ventricular response, absent organized P waves, narrow QRS, fibrillatory baseline. Most ectopic foci originate around the pulmonary veins.
24Rate-control drug classes for AFib and their shared mechanism?
β-blocker, non-DHP CCB (verapamil, diltiazem), digoxin — all prolong AV nodal refractoriness so fewer atrial impulses reach the ventricles.
25ECG and ablation target of typical atrial flutter?
ECG: sawtooth flutter waves, atrial rate ~300/min, ventricular response often 150 (2:1). Macroreentrant circuit around the tricuspid annulus with the slow zone at the cavotricuspid isthmus (ablation target).
26Acute termination strategy for AVNRT?
Vagal maneuvers first (carotid sinus massage, Valsalva, cold-water face immersion) to prolong AV refractoriness and break the reentrant circuit. If that fails, IV adenosine push.
27Why does "holiday heart" lead to AFib?
Binge alcohol acutely alters atrial conduction and shortens atrial refractoriness, predisposing to ectopic foci and AFib in otherwise structurally normal hearts.
28ECG of complete (third-degree) AV block?
Complete AV dissociation — P waves and QRS complexes march independently. QRS rate and width depend on the escape pacemaker (junctional vs ventricular).
Ablation targets & antiarrhythmics 5 cards
29Ablation target for AFib?
Pulmonary vein isolation — electrically disconnects the ectopic foci most often arising from the pulmonary vein ostia.
30Ablation target for AVNRT?
The slow pathway of the AV node. Eliminates one limb of the reentrant circuit.
31Ablation target for WPW?
The accessory pathway (bundle of Kent) connecting atrium to ventricle outside the AV node.
32Why is flecainide avoided in patients with structural heart disease?
Class IC drugs strongly block Na channels and are pro-arrhythmic in scarred or ischemic myocardium (CAST-trial principle).
33Multi-organ toxicities of amiodarone?
Thyroid (hypo and hyper), pulmonary fibrosis, hepatitis, blue-gray skin + photosensitivity, corneal microdeposits, bradycardia / QT prolongation. Inhibits CYP3A4 + 2C9 → raises warfarin INR.
Coronary autoregulation & NO biology 5 cards
34Two main mediators of coronary autoregulation?
Adenosine (from local ATP breakdown, acts at A₂A → vasodilation) and nitric oxide from endothelium.
35Why does the subendocardium fail first under ischemic stress?
It is the most compressed layer during systole (highest intramural pressure) and therefore is perfused only in diastole. Any drop in diastolic perfusion pressure or shortening of diastole (tachycardia) hits the subendocardium first.
36Substrate, enzyme, and second messenger of vascular NO?
Substrate: L-arginine. Enzyme: endothelial NOS (eNOS), with O₂ and NADPH. Diffuses into smooth muscle → activates guanylate cyclase → ↑ cGMP → MLCK dephosphorylation → relaxation.
37Why is sildenafil contraindicated with nitrates?
Both raise vascular smooth-muscle cGMP — sildenafil by blocking PDE5, nitrates by donating NO. Combined use causes catastrophic, refractory hypotension.
38What is the earliest functional defect in atherogenesis?
Loss of endothelial NO bioavailability from smoking, dyslipidemia, hypertension, hyperglycemia. The endothelium loses its anti-inflammatory, anti-thrombotic, vasodilator phenotype.
Baroreflex · chemoreceptors · syncope 5 cards
39Afferent CN for the carotid sinus baroreceptor and the aortic arch baroreceptor?
Carotid sinus → CN IX (glossopharyngeal). Aortic arch → CN X (vagus). Mnemonic: carotid IX, aortic X.
40Baroreflex response to acute hemorrhage?
↓ Stretch → ↓ baroreceptor firing → ↓ vagal + ↑ sympathetic outflow → ↑ HR + ↑ contractility + vasoconstriction. ANP falls because atrial stretch is reduced.
41How does phenylephrine produce reflex bradycardia?
Pure α₁ agonist → vasoconstriction → ↑ MAP → baroreceptor firing rises → vagal tone to the SA node increases → HR falls. Classic teaching demonstration of an intact baroreflex.
42What is the dual mechanism of vasovagal syncope?
A simultaneous brainstem misfire of both autonomic limbs: cardioinhibitory (↑ vagal → bradycardia and ↓ contractility) and vasodepressor (↓ sympathetic → peripheral vasodilation and ↓ venous return).
43Carotid sinus hypersensitivity — classic trigger and presentation?
Tight collar, shaving, or head-turning in an older adult → mechanical pressure on the carotid sinus → exaggerated reflex bradycardia and hypotension → syncope. Pacemaker if cardioinhibitory predominates and the episodes recur.
Shock physiology 5 cards
44Hemodynamic profile of hypovolemic shock?
↓ CVP, ↓ PCWP, ↓ cardiac index, ↑ SVR (compensatory vasoconstriction), ↓ mixed venous O₂, cool extremities.
45Hemodynamic profile of cardiogenic shock?
↑ CVP, ↑ PCWP, ↓ cardiac index, ↑ SVR, ↓ SvO₂, cool extremities. SVR is up, not down — common trap.
46Why does PCWP appear paradoxically high in cardiac tamponade?
External pericardial pressure is transmitted into all chambers, so measured LA pressure reads high — but effective LV filling is actually low (the pericardium constrains diastolic expansion).
47Why is cardiac output usually high in early septic shock?
Systemic vasodilation from inflammatory mediators (TNFα, IL-1, IL-6, iNOS-derived NO) drops SVR. Tachycardia and venoconstriction maintain — or briefly elevate — CO. Mitochondrial dysfunction also limits O₂ extraction, so SvO₂ runs high.
48How do you distinguish neurogenic from other distributive shock?
After spinal cord injury, loss of sympathetic outflow produces vasodilation plus paradoxical bradycardia (unopposed vagal tone). Most other distributive shocks have compensatory tachycardia.
Heart failure cascade & ARNi 5 cards
49Four neurohormonal arms activated in decompensated HFrEF?
1 Sympathetic (↑ HR, contractility, vasoconstriction) · 2 RAAS (AngII + aldosterone + remodeling) · 3 ADH (water retention, hyponatremia) · 4 ANP/BNP counter-regulation.
50Source cells of ANP and BNP, and their effects?
ANP from atrial myocytes (atrial stretch), BNP from ventricular myocytes (ventricular stretch). Both activate guanylate cyclase → ↑ cGMP → natriuresis, diuresis, vasodilation, ↓ aldosterone, ↓ renin.
51How does sacubitril/valsartan work?
Sacubitril inhibits neprilysin (the protease that degrades ANP/BNP) → raises endogenous natriuretic peptide activity. Valsartan blocks the AT₁ receptor to neutralize the parallel rise in angiotensin II that sacubitril would otherwise allow.
52Four-pillar regimen for HFrEF with mortality benefit?
β-blocker (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol) · ACE-i / ARB / ARNi · MRA (spironolactone, eplerenone) · SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin). Loop diuretics relieve congestion but do not extend life.
53Which vasodilator is preferred for acute decompensated HF with hypertension, and why?
Sodium nitroprusside — balanced venous and arterial NO release drops both preload and afterload simultaneously, lowering wall stress while keeping stroke volume preserved.
