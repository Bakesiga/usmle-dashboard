HF clinical presentation left vs right

  • HFrEF (left): orthopnea, PND, exertional dyspnea, fatigue, S3, bibasilar crackles, displaced apical impulse (inferolateral PMI).
  • Right HF / cor pulmonale: JVD, hepatomegaly, peripheral pitting edema, cardiac ascites (transudate, SAAG > 1.1), Kussmaul sign.
  • #1 cause of right HF = left HF. #2 = chronic lung disease (cor pulmonale).
  • Pulmonary edema histology: pink acellular intra-alveolar transudate; chronic LV failure → hemosiderin-laden alveolar macrophages ("HF cells", Prussian blue +).
  • Lymphatic buffer: ~10–20× normal capacity must be saturated before clinical edema → explains why early ↑ filling pressures may be silent.

ADHF chest X-ray pulmonary edema signs

  • Bilateral patchy airspace opacities (alveolar edema).
  • Kerley B lines — short horizontal interlobular septal thickening at lung bases.
  • Bat-wing perihilar opacification.
  • Fissure thickening (sub-pleural fluid).
  • Blunted costophrenic angles (pleural effusions).
  • Cardiomegaly + cephalization of pulmonary vessels.
  • Cardiac/vascular curves: cardiac shifts down-right (↓ contractility) + vascular shifts right (volume retention) → new intersection at ↑ RA pressure with maintained/slightly ↓ CO.

BNP biomarker wall stress · NPV

  • BNP — released by ventricular cardiomyocytes in response to wall stress (Laplace: T = Pr/2h).
  • ANP — from atrial cardiomyocytes in response to atrial stretch.
  • Both → guanylate cyclase → ↑ cGMP → natriuresis + vasodilation + ↓ aldosterone + ↓ renin.
  • Clinical use: normal BNP rules out acute HF (high NPV); elevated supports HF.
  • Non-specific: also ↑ in AFib, CKD, PE, sepsis, advanced age, female sex.
  • Falsely low in obesity (adipose neprilysin clears BNP).
  • On ARNi, BNP is unreliable for monitoring → use NT-proBNP.

Vicious cycle of decompensated HF bad arms vs good arm

↓ CO → ↓ baroreceptor stretch + ↓ renal perfusion │ ┌───────────┴───────────┐ ▼ ▼ SNS activation RAAS activation [BAD ARMS] (↑ HR, ↑ contractility, (AngII → vasoconstriction vasoconstriction) + aldosterone → Na/H₂O retention) │ │ └───────────┬───────────┘ ▼ ↑ SVR (afterload) + ↑ preload ▼ ↑ wall stress → worsens HF → loop repeats │ │ countered (but eventually overwhelmed) by ▼ ANP (atrial stretch) + BNP (ventricular stretch) [GOOD ARM] → cGMP → natriuresis + vasodilation + ↓ aldosterone
  • Stage B clue: all three neurohormones (AngII, NE, ANP) are already ↑ even before symptoms appear → rationale for starting ACE-i + β-blocker at Stage B.
  • 4-pillar logic: β-blocker breaks the SNS arm; ACE-i/ARB/ARNi breaks RAAS (ARNi also augments ANP/BNP via neprilysin block); MRA blocks aldosterone; SGLT2-i adds an independent mortality benefit.

HFrEF four-pillar regimen mortality benefit · start at Stage C (β-blocker + ACE-i at Stage B)

PillarExamplesMechanism / pearl
1. β-blockerCarvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprololBlocks SNS arm; reverses remodeling. Start LOW, GO SLOW. Do not start in acute decompensation.
2. ACE-i / ARB / ARNiLisinopril · losartan · sacubitril/valsartanBlocks RAAS. ARNi superior to ACE-i in stable HFrEF (PARADIGM-HF). ACE-i → dry cough + angioedema (bradykinin); ARB doesn't. Wash out ACE-i 36 h before starting ARNi.
3. MRASpironolactone · eplerenoneBlocks aldosterone → anti-fibrotic + K⁺/Mg-sparing. Monitor K⁺ + Cr. Spiro = gynecomastia; eplerenone selective.
4. SGLT2 inhibitorDapagliflozin · empagliflozinWorks regardless of diabetes; mechanism not fully understood. Reduces HF hospitalization + CV mortality.
Symptom add-on: loop diuretic (furosemide) — congestion relief, no mortality benefit.
Devices: ICD if EF ≤ 35 % after ≥ 3 mo of optimized GDMT · CRT if QRS ≥ 150 ms + LBBB · LVAD / transplant at Stage D.

HFpEF management stiff LV

  • SGLT2-i (only class with consistent mortality/hospitalization benefit in HFpEF).
  • MRA (spironolactone) — symptom + hospitalization benefit, especially EF 45–55 %.
  • Loop diuretic for congestion.
  • Aggressive BP control — HTN is the dominant driver.
  • Treat comorbidities: AF, OSA, obesity, CKD.
  • β-blockers + ACE-i/ARB are NOT mortality-proven in HFpEF (used for comorbidities).

Right HF · cor pulmonale RV pressure overload

  • COPD path: chronic alveolar hypoxia → hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction + remodeling → pulmonary HTN → RV pressure overload → RV dilation → tricuspid regurgitation → RV failure.
  • Cardiac ascites: ↑ portal capillary hydrostatic pressure from systemic venous congestion + normal oncotic + normal permeability → transudate, SAAG > 1.1 g/dL.
  • Distinguish from cirrhotic ascites: both have SAAG > 1.1, but cardiac ascites total protein > 2.5 g/dL (sinusoidal congestion preserves protein leak).
  • Reversible DCMs: alcohol (abstinence) · peripartum (often resolves) · tachycardia-induced · stress (Takotsubo).
  • Anthracycline (doxorubicin) cardiotoxicity: dose-dependent irreversible DCM presenting years–decades later. Mechanism: free-radical mitochondrial damage + topoisomerase IIβ inhibition. Prevent with dexrazoxane.

  • Massive PE pathophysiology: clot in PA → ↑ RV afterload → RV dilation + ↑ wall tension → RV ischemia (supply-demand mismatch) → ↓ LV preload → cardiogenic collapse. Echo: enlarged RV cavity, septal flattening.
  • Septic cold phase: CO drops, SvO₂ falls, SVR may rise → looks like cardiogenic shock — context matters.
  • Tamponade quirk: PCWP often looks ↑ (transmitted pericardial pressure), but LV is actually under-filled.

Vasopressor / inotrope cheat-sheet match to shock

  • Norepinephrine (α₁ >> β₁) — first-line in septic shock and most undifferentiated shock with low SVR.
  • Epinephrine (α₁ + β₁ + β₂) — IM in anaphylaxis; IV in cardiac arrest + refractory shock.
  • Dobutamine (β₁ >> β₂) — inotrope of choice in cardiogenic shock with adequate BP.
  • Phenylephrine (pure α₁) — neurogenic shock, anesthesia-induced hypotension; reflex bradycardia.
  • Vasopressin — adjunct in septic shock when norepinephrine alone insufficient.
  • Dopamine (dose-dependent) — largely fallen out of favor (arrhythmias).

High-output HF pearl low SVR + ↑ CO

  • Etiologies: hyperthyroidism (Graves), severe anemia, large AV fistula, wet beriberi (thiamine deficiency), Paget disease of bone, pregnancy.
  • Findings: ↓ SVR + ↑ CO + wide pulse pressure + warm extremities + pitting edema + S3.
  • Graves treatment: propranolol (blocks β + peripheral T4→T3 conversion) + definitive Rx (methimazole, radioactive iodine, thyroidectomy).
  • Treat the cause — diuretics + standard HF meds alone do not fix it.

Top 15 traps

  1. S3 = floppy LV (volume); S4 = stiff LV (pressure).
  2. HFrEF ≤ 40 %; HFpEF ≥ 50 %; 41–49 % = HFmrEF.
  3. Start ACE-i + β-blocker at Stage B — don't wait for symptoms.
  4. 4 pillars: β-blocker + ARNi (or ACE-i/ARB) + MRA + SGLT2-i.
  5. Loop diuretic = symptoms only, NO mortality benefit.
  6. ICD if EF ≤ 35 % after optimized GDMT; CRT if QRS ≥ 150 ms + LBBB.
  7. ACE-i cough/angioedema = bradykinin; switch to ARB.
  8. ARNi: wash out ACE-i 36 h to avoid angioedema.
  9. SGLT2-i works even without diabetes.
  10. Septic shock first-line vasopressor = norepinephrine.
  11. Anaphylaxis = IM epinephrine, not IV (unless arrest).
  12. Cardiogenic shock inotrope = dobutamine; revascularize early.
  13. Massive PE → ↑ CVP + ↓ PCWP + RV dilation on echo.
  14. Cardiac ascites: SAAG > 1.1, total protein > 2.5 g/dL.
  15. Hemosiderin-laden macrophages = chronic LV failure → Prussian blue +.
