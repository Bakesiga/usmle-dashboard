Core equations 7 cards
1Write the equation for cardiac output in two forms.
CO = SV × HR and CO = VO₂ / (CaO₂ − CvO₂) (Fick principle).
2EDV is 125 mL, ESV is 75 mL, HR is 100/min. Compute SV, EF, and CO.
SV = EDV − ESV = 50 mL. EF = SV / EDV = 40%. CO = SV × HR = 5 L/min.
3What ejection fraction defines HFrEF?
EF < 40%. Borderline 40–49% is sometimes called "HF mid-range EF." Preserved EF (HFpEF) = ≥ 50%.
4Two physiologic determinants of pulse pressure?
(1) Stroke volume — larger SV widens PP. (2) Aortic compliance — stiffer aorta widens PP at the same SV.
5Why does isolated systolic hypertension dominate in the elderly?
Aortic compliance falls with age. The same SV pushed into a stiffer aorta creates a larger systolic pressure swing → high SBP with relatively unchanged DBP → wide pulse pressure.
6Poiseuille's law: which variable dominates resistance and why?
Radius. R ∝ ηL / r⁴ — so halving the radius raises resistance 16-fold. This is why arteriolar tone is the main BP regulator.
7What approximates mean arterial pressure from systolic and diastolic values?
MAP ≈ DBP + 1/3 (SBP − DBP). Also MAP ≈ CO × SVR.
Preload, afterload, contractility 8 cards
8Define preload, afterload, and contractility in one line each.
Preload = end-diastolic volume (or pressure) — depends on venous return. Afterload ≈ aortic DBP — the pressure the LV must exceed to open AV. Contractility = intrinsic myocardial force at a given preload.
9State the Frank-Starling law.
Within physiologic range, ↑ end-diastolic sarcomere length → ↑ force of contraction → ↑ stroke volume.
10A patient with hypovolemic shock receives 2 L of normal saline. Which intracellular change in cardiomyocytes most likely follows?
↑ End-diastolic sarcomere length. Fluid raises venous return → ↑ EDV → ↑ stretch → ↑ SV by Frank-Starling.
11A drug delivers nitric oxide selectively to venous smooth muscle and has no direct effect on cardiomyocytes. Effect on cardiomyocyte end-diastolic sarcomere length and SV?
Venodilation → ↑ venous capacitance → ↓ preload → ↓ EDV → ↓ end-diastolic sarcomere length → ↓ SV via Frank-Starling. Intrinsic contractility unchanged.
12What gene is upregulated in concentric LV hypertrophy from chronic hypertension, and why?
β-myosin heavy chain. Pressure overload adds sarcomeres in parallel; the heart thickens to lower wall stress (Laplace). Concentric LVH → ↓ LV compliance → S4 → HFpEF.
13Eccentric vs concentric LVH — how are sarcomeres added and what triggers each?
Eccentric = volume overload (AR, MR, AV fistula); sarcomeres added in series → chamber dilates. Concentric = pressure overload (chronic HTN, AS); sarcomeres added in parallel → walls thicken.
14Why is angiotensin II elevated in chronic systemic hypertension that has progressed to HFpEF?
Stiff LV → ↓ stroke volume → ↓ forward CO → kidney senses low perfusion → activates RAAS → ↑ angiotensin II (vasoconstriction + aldosterone). Initially compensatory, then maladaptive.
15Frank-Starling explains why post-PVC beats feel like palpitations. Why?
The compensatory pause after a PVC gives the LV extra diastolic filling time → ↑ EDV → ↑ SV → larger ejection on the next beat, which the patient perceives as the "thump."
PV loops — points and shifts 10 cards
16Label the four corners of the normal LV pressure-volume loop.
Point 1: MV closes (S1, end-diastole). Point 2: AV opens (start of ejection). Point 3: AV closes (S2, end-systole). Point 4: MV opens (start of passive filling).
17An opening snap is heard. Which PV-loop point corresponds to its origin?
Point 4 — MV opens. In mitral stenosis the stiff leaflets snap open against the LA-LV gradient just after isovolumetric relaxation.
18A young patient with a bicuspid aortic valve has a soft systolic ejection click. Which valve event does the click coincide with on the LV pressure tracing?
Aortic valve opening at the end of isovolumetric contraction = point B on the LV tracing (equivalent to point 2 on the PV loop).
19Predict the PV-loop change when phenylephrine is given (pure α₁ agonist).
↑ Afterload → loop top shifts higher and to the right at the ESPVR line → ↑ ESV, ↓ SV, EDV slightly ↑ from venoconstriction.
20Predict the PV-loop change when dobutamine is given.
↑ Contractility → ESPVR steepens (top-left line tilts up) → ↓ ESV, ↑ SV. EDV unchanged.
21Compare PV-loop effects of nitroglycerin vs nitroprusside.
Nitroglycerin (venous >> arterial) → ↓ EDV, narrow loop, ↓ SV, ↓ wall stress. Nitroprusside (balanced) → loop smaller at both ends but SV preserved at lower wall stress. Best for acute HF with hypertension.
22PV-loop signature of acute AV fistula?
↑ Preload (more venous return) + ↓ afterload (low-resistance shunt) → loop shifts right and slightly lower; ↑ EDV, ↑ SV. Chronic = high-output cardiac failure.
23What unique PV-loop feature distinguishes mitral regurgitation from other valve lesions?
There is no true isovolumetric contraction phase — regurgitant flow into the LA starts at point 1 (S1), so the LV begins to lose volume immediately rather than only at AV opening.
24How does the PV loop in mitral stenosis differ from normal?
The LV loop is normal or slightly small because the LV is under-filled by the obstruction. The pathology lives upstream — the LA is hypertensive. Cath confirms ↑ PCWP with normal LV pressures.
25Aortic stenosis vs aortic regurgitation — PV-loop comparison.
AS: peak LV pressure rises well above aortic systolic; tall, narrow loop with persistent LV-aorta gradient. AR: low DBP (regurgitant backflow), ↑ EDV (regurgitant return), wide loop with eccentric LVH.
Bedside maneuvers 7 cards
26A systolic murmur softens with handgrip and gets louder with Valsalva strain. Diagnosis?
HCM (or sometimes MVP). The opposite of AS. Maneuvers that ↓ LV blood volume (Valsalva strain, standing) accentuate dynamic LVOT obstruction; maneuvers that ↑ volume (squat, handgrip, leg raise) relieve obstruction → softer murmur.
27A murmur is louder on inspiration. Side of the heart?
Right-sided (Carvallo sign). Inspiration drops intrathoracic pressure → ↑ venous return → ↑ RV preload → louder right-sided murmurs (TR, TS, PR, PS, RV S3/S4).
28How does the murmur of mitral valve prolapse change with Valsalva?
Both the click and murmur move earlier in systole with Valsalva (↓ preload makes the redundant leaflets prolapse sooner). The opposite happens with squat.
29A tetralogy-of-Fallot patient improves cyanosis by squatting. Mechanism?
Squat ↑ SVR → ↑ aortic resistance → ↓ R→L shunt across the VSD → more blood through the pulmonary circuit → ↑ O₂ saturation.
30Why does pulsus paradoxus occur in cardiac tamponade?
Pericardial fluid prevents free RV expansion. Inspiratory ↑ venous return is accommodated by the septum bowing into the LV → ↓ LV EDV → ↓ LV SV during inspiration → SBP drop > 10 mmHg.
31Beck's triad?
Hypotension + elevated JVP + muffled heart sounds — classic for cardiac tamponade.
32Distinguish water-hammer pulse from pulsus parvus et tardus.
Water-hammer / Corrigan = rapid rise, high amplitude, rapid collapse — aortic regurgitation. Pulsus parvus et tardus = small amplitude, slow rise — aortic stenosis. The exam vignette usually contrasts the two.
Cardiac & vascular function curves 5 cards
33What does the cardiac function curve plot, and what shifts it up?
It plots CO against CVP (or RAP). Shifts up with ↑ contractility (catecholamines, exercise, digoxin), down with ↓ contractility (β-blockers, ischemia, HFrEF).
34What does the vascular function curve plot, and what shifts it right?
It plots CVP against CO. Shifts right with ↑ blood volume or venoconstriction (saline bolus, exercise, AV fistula). Shifts left with hemorrhage or venodilation.
35Predict the operating-point shift in acute hemorrhage.
Vascular curve shifts left → intersection moves left and down → ↓ CO with ↓ CVP. Compensatory sympathetic tone can partially restore CO but at the cost of vasoconstriction.
36Operating-point shift during steady-state exercise?
Cardiac curve shifts up (↑ contractility), vascular curve shifts right (venoconstriction + skeletal muscle pump). Intersection moves up → ↑ CO with roughly unchanged CVP.
37Operating-point shift in chronic AV fistula?
Vascular curve shifts right (effective volume expanded, capacitance falls) + cardiac curve shifts up (chronic preload-driven hypertrophy) → ↑ CO with mildly ↑ CVP. Chronic state = high-output cardiac failure.
Wiggers and JVP 6 cards
38Name the events at S1 and S2.
S1 = mitral + tricuspid valve closure (start of systole). S2 = aortic + pulmonic valve closure (start of diastole).
39What does the JVP "a wave" represent and when does it disappear?
Atrial contraction. Disappears in atrial fibrillation (no organized atrial systole).
40What produces cannon "a" waves?
Complete (3°) AV block. Atria contract against a closed tricuspid valve when the ventricles happen to be in systole, producing giant a waves intermittently in the neck veins.
41Big v waves on JVP — likely lesion?
Tricuspid regurgitation. Atrial filling occurs against a closed TV during ventricular systole; with TR the regurgitant jet adds to atrial pressure → towering v wave.
42On Doppler mitral inflow, what do the E and A waves represent, and which one corresponds to S3?
E wave = early passive ventricular filling. A wave = late atrial-contraction filling. S3 sound, when present, occurs at the end of the rapid passive filling (E wave) — sudden deceleration in a non-compliant or volume-overloaded LV.
43When is S3 normal vs pathologic?
Normal in children and adults under ~40 (compliant LV). Pathologic in older adults with heart failure or volume-overloaded LV.
Heart sounds and S2 splitting 5 cards
44S4 — timing, location, and the structural problem it implies.
Late diastolic, low-pitched, heard with the bell at the apex. Atrial kick into a stiff, non-compliant LV. Causes: HCM, HFpEF, severe HTN, aging.
45Physiologic splitting of S2 — what is it?
On inspiration, ↑ venous return delays P2 (RV takes longer to empty) while A2 moves slightly earlier → audible A2–P2 split. On expiration, they fuse.
46Why is S2 splitting "fixed" in atrial septal defect?
The constant L→R shunt equalises RA and LA pressures and volumes throughout the respiratory cycle, so P2 is delayed by the same amount regardless of breathing → unchanging A2–P2 gap.
47What causes paradoxical splitting of S2?
A2 is delayed past P2 — usually LBBB or severe AS. On expiration ↓ RV return advances P2; gap widens. Inspiration brings them together.
48Wide (but still respiration-variable) S2 splitting — common causes?
Anything that delays P2 — RBBB, pulmonic stenosis, severe MR (early A2 because LV empties faster into the LA + aorta).
Valvular disease recognition 10 cards
49Classic triad of severe aortic stenosis?
Angina, syncope, heart failure. The high LV pressure required to push blood across the narrowed valve eventually causes ischemia, low CO with exertion, and pump failure.
50A 33-year-old with prior endocarditis has bounding peripheral pulses and a systolic-diastolic bruit over the femoral artery on partial compression. Diagnosis and named sign?
Aortic regurgitation. The femoral finding is Duroziez sign. Other AR signs: water-hammer / Corrigan pulse, de Musset (head bobbing), Quincke (nail capillary pulsations), Müller (uvula pulse), Traube ("pistol-shot" femoral), Hill (popliteal SBP > brachial by > 60 mmHg).
51Cath data: LVEDP 10, LV peak systolic 110, PCWP 36. Diagnosis?
Mitral stenosis. PCWP reflects LA pressure; a large LA-LV gradient with normal LV pressures means obstruction at the mitral valve.
52How does mitral stenosis affect LV diastolic pressure, afterload, and contractility?
All three are normal. The obstruction is upstream of the LV, so the LV is simply under-filled. Pulmonary hypertension develops by retrograde transmission; the LV muscle itself stays unaffected.
53A 35-year-old with IVDU history has a holosystolic murmur at the lower left sternal border that grows louder on inspiration. Diagnosis?
Tricuspid regurgitation (Carvallo sign positive). IVDU classically infects the tricuspid valve.
54Distinguish the holosystolic murmurs of MR, TR, and VSD by location and respiratory response.
MR: apex → radiates to axilla; no inspiratory change. TR: LLSB; louder with inspiration. VSD: middle LSB (3rd–4th ICS); often with thrill; no respiratory change.
55Acute MR 3–5 days post-MI with pulmonary edema — anatomic explanation?
Rupture of the posteromedial papillary muscle, which is supplied by the PDA alone (single coronary supply makes it vulnerable to inferior MI). Acute regurgitation gives no time for compensatory LV remodeling → flash pulmonary edema.
56Which valve is the most common congenital valvulopathy, and what late complications does it predispose to?
Bicuspid aortic valve. Predisposes to early aortic stenosis, aortic regurgitation, and ascending aortic dilation / aneurysm.
57Soft systolic ejection murmur at the left upper sternal border in an athletic teenager — likely cause?
Physiologic / flow murmur across the normal pulmonic valve because of high stroke volume. Common in young, healthy, athletic individuals; no further work-up unless other red flags.
58An adolescent has fixed widely split S2 + a soft mid-systolic murmur at the left upper sternal border. Diagnosis and shunt mechanics?
Atrial septal defect. L→R shunt ↑ pulmonary blood flow → soft flow murmur across the (normal) pulmonic valve; RA-LA equalisation makes the splitting fixed.
Cardiomyopathy phenotypes 5 cards
59An 18-year-old athlete dies suddenly on the basketball court. Autopsy shows asymmetric septal hypertrophy. Most likely pre-mortem auscultatory finding?
S4 (atrial kick into stiff hypertrophied LV) + a midsystolic murmur at the LSB that softens with squat or handgrip. Diagnosis: HCM.
60Genetic basis of HCM?
Autosomal dominant with incomplete penetrance. Most common mutations affect cardiac sarcomere proteins — β-myosin heavy chain (MYH7) and myosin-binding protein C (MYBPC3).
61Echo profile of HCM?
↑ LV mass, ↓ LV cavity size, preserved EF, impaired diastolic relaxation. Asymmetric septal hypertrophy with dynamic LVOT obstruction.
62Distinguish the echo of dilated CMP, HCM, restrictive CMP, and athlete's heart.
Dilated: ↑ mass, ↑ cavity, ↓ EF, normal diastole. HCM: ↑ mass, ↓ cavity, preserved EF, impaired diastole. Restrictive: normal mass, normal cavity, preserved EF, impaired diastole. Athlete: ↑ mass, normal/↑ cavity, preserved EF, normal diastole.
63Post-viral dilated cardiomyopathy in a 39-year-old — expected EF, SV, and CO if EDV is 125 mL, ESV 75 mL, HR 100/min?
SV 50, EF 40% (reduced), CO 5 L/min. The patient has HFrEF.
Exercise · aging · shock fluids 7 cards
64Aerobic exercise: changes in SBP, DBP, and pulse pressure?
SBP ↑ (↑ SV + ↑ contractility), DBP roughly unchanged (muscle vasodilation lowers SVR but venoconstriction maintains DBP), pulse pressure ↑.
65During exercise stress, which parameter falls compared to rest?
Total systemic vascular resistance. Active skeletal muscle vasodilation overwhelms splanchnic and cutaneous vasoconstriction. HR, contractility, CO, preload, SBP, PA pressures all rise.
66Pattern of LV hypertrophy expected in a competitive distance runner vs an Olympic weightlifter?
Distance runner (volume training): eccentric LVH — sarcomeres in series, dilated cavity, ↑ mass with preserved EF and normal diastole. Weightlifter (pressure / isometric): concentric LVH — sarcomeres in parallel.
67Why does the aging aorta give isolated systolic hypertension?
Loss of elastin and collagen cross-linking in the media → stiffer aorta → same SV creates higher systolic pressure swing without raising diastolic pressure → ↑ SBP, normal DBP, wide pulse pressure.
68Aging cardiomyocyte — how does it change in number and content?
Hypertrophy without hyperplasia (cells get larger but no new myocytes) + accumulation of lipofuscin ("wear-and-tear" pigment) in the cytoplasm.
69In acute hypovolemic shock from trauma, what changes are you likely to see in the hematocrit immediately and later?
Whole-blood loss is initially isovolemic for plasma and cells (hematocrit unchanged in the first minutes). With fluid shifts from interstitium and IV resuscitation, hemoglobin and hematocrit then fall.
70What is the appropriate first-line vasodilator in acute decompensated heart failure with hypertension, and why?
Sodium nitroprusside. It is a balanced venous + arterial vasodilator — drops both preload (EDV) and afterload (peak LV pressure) simultaneously while keeping SV essentially preserved, allowing pulmonary venous decompression without ↓ CO.
